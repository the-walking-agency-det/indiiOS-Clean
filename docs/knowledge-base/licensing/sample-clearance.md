# Sample Clearance Guide

*Legal use of samples in your music*

---

## What Is Sample Clearance?

**Sample clearance** is the legal process of obtaining permission to use a portion of someone else's copyrighted recording in your own music. When you sample—whether it's a drum break, a vocal snippet, a keyboard riff, or any recognizable audio from an existing recording—you need clearance from the rights holders.

### The Two Clearances Required

Every sample requires **two separate clearances**:

| Clearance Type | Rights Owner | What It Covers |
|----------------|--------------|----------------|
| **Master Sample Clearance** | Record label or artist | The actual sound recording |
| **Publishing Sample Clearance** | Publisher or songwriter | The underlying composition (melody, lyrics) |

**Example:** You sample the drum break from Led Zeppelin's "When the Levee Breaks":
- **Master clearance** from Atlantic Records (the recording)
- **Publishing clearance** from Warner/Chappell (the composition)

Both must be obtained for legal use.

---

## When You Need Sample Clearance

### Clearance Is Required For:

**Direct Sampling**
- Any portion of a copyrighted recording
- Even very short clips (courts have ruled on samples as short as 2 seconds)
- Loops from commercial recordings
- Drum breaks and isolated instruments

**Interpolation**
- Re-recording a melody or lyric from another song
- Not using the original recording, but copying the composition
- Still requires publishing clearance

**Remixes**
- Using stems from the original recording
- Requires master clearance

**Mashups**
- Combining multiple songs
- Requires clearance for every sampled song

### The "De Minimis" Myth

Some artists believe very short samples don't need clearance under "de minimis" doctrine (too small to matter). **This is legally risky.**

**Key Cases:**
- *Bridgeport Music v. Dimension Films* (2005): Court ruled ANY sampling of a sound recording requires license, regardless of length
- *VMG Salsoul v. Ciccone* (2016): Some courts disagree, but the risk remains

**Bottom line:** Don't rely on de minimis. Clear your samples or don't use them.

---

## When You DON'T Need Clearance

### Safe to Use Without Clearance:

**Original Recordings**
- You played/recorded it yourself
- No elements from other copyrighted works

**Public Domain**
- Recordings released before 1929 (in the US)
- Verify both recording AND composition are public domain
- International rules vary

**Royalty-Free Sample Libraries**
- Splice, Loopmasters, Sounds.com
- Legitimate libraries provide clearance
- Read the license terms carefully

**Soundalikes**
- Recreating the vibe without copying the actual recording
- Must be sufficiently different to avoid claims
- Risky if too similar

**Licensed Sample Packs**
- Some vinyl sample services (Tracklib)
- Pre-cleared samples with known costs

## The Sample Clearance Process

### Step 1: Identify the Sample

Document everything:
- Original song title and artist
- Album and release year
- Record label
- Exact portion sampled (e.g., "0:23-0:45")
- How it's used in your song (chopped, pitched, looped, etc.)

**Tools for Identification:**
- **WhoSampled** — Database of known samples
- **Shazam** — Identify songs
- **Discogs** — Detailed release information
- **Wikipedia** — Album and track details

### Step 2: Find the Rights Holders

**Finding the Master Rights Holder:**
- Check the album liner notes
- Search Discogs for label information
- Current label may have changed (research ownership transfers)
- Major label consolidation: UMG, Sony, Warner own most catalogs

**Finding the Publishing Rights Holder:**
- Search ASCAP, BMI, SESAC databases
- Check the album credits
- Contact the artist's management
- Use Harry Fox Agency Songfile search

**Example Research:**

You want to sample "Funky Drummer" by James Brown.

**Master:**
- Original: King Records
- Current: Universal Music Group (acquired King catalog)

**Publishing:**
- Search ASCAP: Dynatone Publishing (administered by Universal Music Publishing)

### Step 3: Submit a Clearance Request

**What to Include:**

```
SAMPLE CLEARANCE REQUEST

Your Information:
- Artist name
- Contact information
- Record label (if any)
- Publisher (if any)

Your Song:
- Title
- Planned release date
- Distribution plans (streaming, physical, etc.)
- Album/project name

Sample Information:
- Original song title and artist
- Album and release year
- Record label
- Publisher
- Timing of sample (start/end time)
- Description of use (loop, chop, pitch, etc.)
- Length of sample in your song

Proposed Terms:
- Buyout vs. royalty split preference
- Territory needed
- Term needed
- Budget range
```

**Where to Send:**
- **Major labels:** Sample clearance departments (search "[label] sample clearance")
- **Publishers:** Licensing departments
- **Independent artists:** Direct contact, often through management

### Step 4: Negotiate Terms

**Common Deal Structures:**

| Structure | Description | Best For |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Buyout** | One-time fee, no ongoing royalties | Artists with budget, want clean rights |
| **Royalty Split** | Percentage of your song's earnings | Limited budget, expect significant earnings |
| **Hybrid** | Upfront fee + reduced royalty | Balanced approach |
| **Advance Against Royalties** | Upfront payment recouped from royalties | Established artists with predictable income |

**Typical Terms:**

**Master Sample Clearance:**
- 15-50% of your master recording royalties
- Or: $1,000-$10,000+ buyout (varies wildly by sample)
- Major hits can cost $50,000-$100,000+

**Publishing Sample Clearance:**
- 15-50% of your publishing royalties
- Or: Negotiated buyout
- Interpolation often costs more than sampling

**Example Deal:**
- You sample a moderately known 70s soul track
- Master: 25% of master royalties OR $3,000 buyout
- Publishing: 25% of publishing royalties OR $2,500 buyout
- Total cost if buyout: $5,500

### Step 5: Get It in Writing

A proper sample clearance agreement must include:

**Master Clearance Agreement:**
- Exact sample being cleared (song, artist, timing)
- Your song that uses it
- Grant of rights (what they can use it for)
- Territory (where)
- Term (how long)
- Fee/royalty structure
- Credit requirements
- Warranties (they own the rights they're granting)
- Indemnification (you're responsible if there's a problem)

**Publishing Clearance Agreement:**
- Same elements as above
- Percentage of publishing ownership transferred
- Administration rights
- Performance royalty splits

**Have a lawyer review** any agreement over $5,000 or involving ongoing royalties.

## Sample Clearance Costs

### Factors Affecting Cost

**Sample Source:**
- Iconic/recognizable samples: $$$$
- Obscure samples: $$
- Genre classics: $$$
- Current hits: $$$$$

**Your Profile:**
- Unknown artist: Lower fees possible
- Established artist: Higher fees expected
- Major label: Full market rate

**Sample Usage:**
- Loop vs. chop vs. one-shot
- Prominence in your track
- Whether it's the "hook"

**Sample Length:**
- Longer samples generally cost more
- Some rights holders charge by the second

### Realistic Cost Ranges

| Sample Type | Unknown Artist | Indie/Established | Major Label |
|-------------|---------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Obscure 70s soul | $500-$2,000 | $2,000-$5,000 | $5,000-$15,000 |
| Known classic | $2,000-$5,000 | $5,000-$15,000 | $15,000-$50,000 |
| Iconic breakbeat | $3,000-$7,000 | $7,000-$20,000 | $20,000-$75,000 |
| Current hit | $5,000-$15,000 | $15,000-$50,000 | $50,000-$200,000+ |
| Beatles/Stones/etc. | $10,000-$25,000 | $25,000-$100,000 | $100,000-$500,000+ |

**Note:** These are estimates. Iconic samples (Amen break, Think break) have been licensed for everything from free to six figures depending on usage.

### Royalty Split Examples

If you don't pay a buyout fee, expect to give up publishing/master percentages:

| Scenario | Your Publishing | Sample Publisher | Your Master | Sample Master Owner |
|----------|-----------------|------------------|-------------|---------------------|
| No sample | 100% | 0% | 100% | 0% |
| Small sample, royalty deal | 75% | 25% | 80% | 20% |
| Major sample, royalty deal | 50% | 50% | 60% | 40% |
| Multiple samples | 25-50% | 50-75% | 40-60% | 40-60% |

## What If They Say No?

### Alternatives When Clearance Is Denied

**1. Recreate the Sample**
- Play the part yourself
- Change it enough to be unrecognizable
- Use different instruments, tempo, key
- **Risk:** If too similar, still infringement

**2. Use a Royalty-Free Alternative**
- Splice, Loopmasters, Sounds.com
- Thousands of legal samples
- Subscription or per-sample pricing

**3. Pre-Cleared Sample Services**
- **Tracklib** — Vinyl samples with guaranteed clearance
- **Sample Clearance services** — Handle negotiation for you

**4. Remove the Sample**
- Reproduce the track without it
- Often the cleanest solution
- May improve the song creatively

**5. Sample Something Else**
- Find a similar-sounding legal alternative
- Public domain recordings
- Royalty-free equivalents

### The "Sample It and See" Approach

Some artists sample first and clear later if the track gains traction. **This is risky:**

**Risks:**
- Distributor may reject the release
- Content ID claims on platforms
- Takedown notices
- Lawsuits (especially if the song becomes a hit)
- Inability to license for sync
- Damaged reputation

**If You Do Sample First:**
- Have a plan for clearance
- Budget for potential fees
- Be prepared to remove/replace the sample
- Don't build your career on uncleared samples

## Common Sampling Scenarios

### Scenario 1: The Drum Break

**Situation:** You want to use the famous Amen break from "Amen, Brother" by The Winstons.

**The Problem:**
- The Winstons never cleared or monetized the sample
- Original master rights unclear
- Richard L. Spencer (songwriter) has pursued claims

**Solutions:**
1. **Use a recreation** — Program similar drums
2. **Use royalty-free equivalent** — Splice has many Amen-style breaks
3. **Sample a different break** — Thousands of lesser-known drum breaks
4. **Risk it** — Not recommended; Spencer has pursued claims

### Scenario 2: The Vocal Chop

**Situation:** You chopped a vocal snippet from a 90s R&B song.

**Process:**
1. Identify the song and rights holders
2. Submit clearance request to label and publisher
3. Expect: 20-40% royalty split or $2,000-$10,000 buyout
4. Alternative: Replace with royalty-free vocal chop

### Scenario 3: The Interpolation

**Situation:** You re-recorded the melody from a Beatles song with different lyrics.

**Important:**
- No master clearance needed (you didn't use their recording)
- **Publishing clearance REQUIRED** — You used their composition
- May be more expensive than sampling
- Beatles catalog = $$$$

**Cost estimate:** $25,000-$100,000+ for Beatles interpolation

### Scenario 4: The Mashup

**Situation:** You combined vocals from Song A with instrumental from Song B.

**Clearance needed:**
- Master and publishing for Song A
- Master and publishing for Song B
- Four separate clearances total

**Challenges:**
- Multiple rights holders must all agree
- One "no" kills the project
- Very difficult to clear commercially

**Alternative:** Create unofficial/bootleg, don't monetize, or use royalty-free elements

## Legal Cases & Precedents

### Bridgeport Music v. Dimension Films (2005)

**The Case:**
- NWA sampled a 2-second guitar chord from Funkadelic
- Bridgeport (rights holder) sued

**The Ruling:**
- 6th Circuit Court: "Get a license or do not sample"
- ANY sampling of a sound recording requires license
- No de minimis exception for sound recordings

**Impact:**
- Changed sampling landscape
- Made clearance mandatory
- Increased costs for sample-based music

### VMG Salsoul v. Ciccone (2016)

**The Case:**
- Madonna's "Vogue" sampled horns from Salsoul Orchestra
- Sample was less than 1 second

**The Ruling:**
- 9th Circuit: De minimis doctrine CAN apply to sound recordings
- Not all sampling requires license

**Impact:**
- Conflicting precedent with Bridgeport
- Legal uncertainty remains
- Still risky to rely on de minimis

### Grand Upright Music v. Warner Bros. Records (1991)

**The Case:**
- Biz Markie's "Alone Again" sampled Gilbert O'Sullivan
- Sample was uncleared

**The Ruling:**
- Court ruled sampling is copyright infringement
- Set precedent for sample clearance requirement
- Biz Markie had to pay damages

**Impact:**
- First major sampling case
- Established that sampling without clearance is theft
- Changed hip-hop industry practices

## Best Practices for Sampling

### Before You Sample

1. **Consider the cost** — Can you afford to clear it?
2. **Have alternatives ready** — What if they say no?
3. **Document everything** — Keep records of what you sampled
4. **Know your distribution plans** — Major label = higher clearance costs

### While Creating

1. **Use royalty-free libraries** — Splice, Tracklib for safety
2. **Create original alternatives** — Play it yourself
3. **Keep stems separate** — Easy to remove if needed
4. **Note all samples** — Don't forget what you used

### Before Release

1. **Clear everything** — Don't release with uncleared samples
2. **Get written agreements** — Verbal promises aren't enough
3. **Have a lawyer review** — For deals over $5,000
4. **Budget for delays** — Clearance takes time (weeks to months)

### After Release

1. **Monitor for claims** — Content ID, takedown notices
2. **Keep clearance documents** — Proof of permission
3. **Account accurately** — Pay royalty shares on time
4. **Build relationships** — Rights holders for future samples
