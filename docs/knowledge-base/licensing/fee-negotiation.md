# Fee Negotiation Guide

*Pricing your music for licensing*

---

## Understanding Your Music's Value

Before negotiating any license, you need to understand what your music is worth in the marketplace. Value isn't just about quality—it's about context, timing, and strategic positioning.

### Factors That Determine Value

**1. Your Track Record**
- Previous placements increase value
- Streaming numbers demonstrate audience
- Awards and recognition matter
- Genre relevance to current trends

**2. The Specific Song**
- Uniqueness and memorability
- Production quality
- Lyrical content and themes
- Emotional resonance
- Genre fit for the project

**3. Rights Clarity**
- One-stop licensing (you control everything)
- Clear ownership documentation
- No sample clearance issues
- Pre-cleared for immediate use

**4. Your Flexibility**
- Instrumentals available
- Stems provided
- Multiple edits (:30, :60)
- Quick turnaround time

## Negotiation Strategies

### Before the Negotiation

**Research the Project:**
- What's the budget? (Ask directly or research)
- Who's the music supervisor? (Check their typical rates)
- What's the media type and reach?
- How important is your song to the project?

**Know Your Walk-Away Point:**
- Minimum acceptable fee
- Terms you won't accept (perpetuity for low fees, etc.)
- When to say no

**Prepare Your Position:**
- Why your song is perfect for the project
- Similar placements and their success
- Your flexibility (instrumentals, edits, etc.)

### Opening Offers

**The Golden Rule:** Whoever names a number first is at a disadvantage.

**If They Ask First:**
- "What's your budget for this placement?"
- "What range are you working with?"
- "I'd need to know more about the usage to quote accurately."

**If You Must Quote First:**
- Quote 20-30% higher than your target
- Provide a range, not a fixed number
- Include conditions ("for a 1-year term, I'd be looking at...")

### Negotiation Tactics

**1. The Package Deal**
Offer multiple songs at a slight discount:
- "I have three songs that could work. If you license all three, I can do $X total."

**2. The Future Value**
Emphasize long-term relationship:
- "For this first placement, I'm flexible. But future licenses would be at my standard rates."

**3. The Exposure Balance**
Acknowledge exposure but don't give away value:
- "I understand the exposure value, but I also need to cover my costs. How about $X?"

**4. The Term Trade**
Use term to justify higher fees:
- "For a 1-year license, I can do $X. For perpetuity, I'd need $Y."

**5. The Rights Separation**
Break down rights individually:
- "For US-only, 1-year, web-only: $X. For worldwide, all media: $Y."

### Responding to Low Offers

**When They Lowball:**

**Don't:**
- Accept immediately out of excitement
- Get offended or emotional
- Reveal your minimum too quickly

**Do:**
- Acknowledge the offer professionally
- Counter with specific justification
- Offer alternatives (shorter term, limited media)

**Example Response:**
> "I appreciate the offer, but based on the usage you're describing—network TV, 3-year term, national campaign—that's below market rate. For this type of placement, the range is typically $X-$Y. Given the exposure and my song's fit for the scene, I could do $Z."

### When to Accept Less

**Strategic Reasons to Lower Your Fee:**

1. **Career-Building Placement**
   - Major film by acclaimed director
   - Prestige TV series with cultural impact
   - Super Bowl commercial (massive exposure)

2. **Relationship Building**
   - First placement with major supervisor
   - Potential for ongoing collaboration
   - Access to bigger projects

3. **Portfolio Development**
   - Need credits for your sync resume
   - First placement in a new media type
   - Building genre-specific credentials

4. **Non-Monetary Value**
   - Prominent on-screen credit
   - Marketing support from production
   - Soundtrack album placement

**How to Frame a Lower Fee:**
> "Given the prestige of this project and the potential for future collaboration, I can be flexible on the fee. My standard rate for this usage would be $X, but for this specific opportunity, I could do $Y."

## Contract Terms to Negotiate

### Must-Have Terms

**1. Credit**
- On-screen credit in film/TV
- End credit recognition
- "Additional music by" if appropriate
- Metadata credit for digital platforms

**2. Payment Terms**
- Net 30 is standard
- 50% upfront, 50% on delivery for large fees
- Late fees for delayed payment

**3. Term Limits**
- Specific end date, not "in perpetuity" unless paid accordingly
- Renewal options and rates
- Right to renegotiate after term

**4. Territory Limits**
- Specific countries/regions
- Not "universe-wide" or "throughout the galaxy"

**5. Media Limits**
- Specific platforms (theatrical, streaming, broadcast)
- Not "all media now known or hereafter devised"

### Red Flag Terms (Reject or Negotiate Hard)

**Work-for-Hire**
- You lose ALL rights to your music
- One-time payment, no future earnings
- Never sign without legal counsel

**Perpetuity for Low Fees**
- "In perpetuity" should command 2-3x standard rate
- Don't give away your music forever for $500

**All-Media Assignments**
- "All media throughout the universe"
- Way too broad for reasonable fees
- Limit to specific, planned uses

**No Credit Requirements**
- Always require attribution
- Helps build your sync resume
- Drives performance royalties

**Delayed Payment Terms**
- Net 60+ is suspicious
- Upfront or net 30 is standard
- Consider late fees for delays

**Indemnification Clauses**
- You pay for their legal problems
- Have a lawyer review
- Limit your liability

### Negotiable Terms

**Exclusivity**
- Category exclusive (e.g., only car commercials): +25-50%
- Full exclusive: +50-100%
- Non-exclusive: Standard rate

**Renewal Options**
- First right of refusal on renewal
- Pre-negotiated renewal rates
- 10-20% increase for renewal

**Approval Rights**
- Right to approve final edit
- Right to approve marketing materials
- Right to see rough cut

## Deal Structures

### Flat Fee (Buyout)

**What it is:** One-time payment, no ongoing royalties.

**Best for:**
- Low-budget projects
- Unknown artists building credits
- Corporate/industrial uses
- When you need immediate cash

**Pros:**
- Immediate payment
- No accounting complexity
- Clean rights transfer

**Cons:**
- No upside if project is successful
- No performance royalties (if work-for-hire)
- May undervalue long-term worth

### Royalty Split

**What it is:** Percentage of revenue from the project.

**Best for:**
- Video games with revenue share
- Streaming series with backend
- Projects with uncertain performance

**Pros:**
- Upside potential
- Aligned incentives
- Fair if project becomes hit

**Cons:**
- Accounting complexity
- Delayed payment
- Risk of no payment if project flops

### Hybrid (Upfront + Royalty)

**What it is:** Lower upfront fee plus percentage of earnings.

**Best for:**
- Mid-budget projects
- When you want some guaranteed income plus upside
- Long-term relationships

**Example:**
- $5,000 upfront + 2% of net profits
- $10,000 upfront + 25% of sync fees for renewals

### Step Deals

**What it is:** Fee increases based on project success.

**Example:**
- Base fee: $5,000
- If film grosses $50M+: Additional $5,000
- If film grosses $100M+: Additional $10,000
- If nominated for Oscar: Additional $15,000

**Best for:**
- Theatrical films
- Projects with breakout potential
- When you believe in the project

## Real-World Negotiation Examples

### Example 1: The Low-Budget Indie Film

**Scenario:**
- Micro-budget indie film ($500K budget)
- They want your song for an emotional scene
- First-time director, but good script

**Their Offer:** $500, all media, in perpetuity

**Your Counter:**
> "I appreciate you considering my music. Given that this is a perpetuity license covering all media, my standard rate would be $3,000. However, I understand you're working with a limited budget. I could do $1,500 for a 5-year term with options to renew at pre-negotiated rates. This gives you the music you need while allowing me to license it elsewhere after the term."

**Likely Outcome:** $1,000-$1,500, 5-year term, limited media

### Example 2: The Major Brand Commercial

**Scenario:**
- National brand campaign (household name)
- 1-year term, US only, web and TV
- Your song is perfect for their demographic

**Their Offer:** $15,000, all media, worldwide, 3 years

**Your Counter:**
> "Thank you for the offer. I'm excited about this campaign. However, the scope you're requesting—all media, worldwide, 3 years—is significantly broader than the $15,000 reflects. For a national brand at that scale, the range is typically $50,000-$100,000. I could do $45,000 for the exact usage you mentioned (US, web/TV, 1 year), or $65,000 for the expanded terms."

**Likely Outcome:** $35,000-$50,000, negotiated scope

### Example 3: The Video Game Placement

**Scenario:**
- AAA game developer
- Want your song for in-game radio station
- Previous games in series sold 10M+ copies

**Their Offer:** $5,000 flat fee, in perpetuity, worldwide

**Your Counter:**
> "I'm a fan of your games and excited about this opportunity. Given the success of the series and the perpetuity license, I'd typically be looking at $20,000+. However, I understand game budgets work differently. Could we structure this as $8,000 upfront plus a small royalty per unit sold after 5 million copies? Or $12,000 flat for a 10-year term with renewal options?"

**Likely Outcome:** $8,000-$12,000, possibly with backend

## Building Long-Term Value

### The Portfolio Approach

Don't evaluate each license in isolation. Build a strategy:

**Year 1-2: Building Credits**
- Accept lower fees for quality placements
- Focus on recognizable projects
- Build relationships with supervisors

**Year 3-4: Raising Rates**
- Use credits to justify higher fees
- Be more selective about projects
- Develop niche expertise

**Year 5+: Premium Positioning**
- Command top rates in your niche
- Exclusive representation deals
- Passive income from earlier placements

### Tracking Your Placements

Keep detailed records:
- Project name and type
- License terms and fees
- Supervisor/producer contacts
- Outcomes (awards, viewership, etc.)

Use this data to justify future fees:
> "My music was featured in [Show], which won [Award] and reached [X] million viewers. Based on that success and the similar usage you're requesting, my rate for this placement would be $Y."

## When to Walk Away

### Red Lines

**Never accept:**
- Work-for-hire without massive compensation
- Perpetuity for less than 2x your standard rate
- All-media rights for local-market fees
- No credit requirement
- Net 90+ payment terms without late fees

### Signs of a Bad Deal

- They won't put terms in writing
- Pressure to decide immediately
- Vague about usage details
- Unwilling to negotiate at all
- Asking for rights "just in case"

### How to Decline Professionally

> "Thank you for considering my music for this project. After reviewing the terms, I don't think this is the right fit for me at this time. I hope we can work together on a future project with different parameters. Best of luck with the production."

## Key Takeaways

1. **Know your worth** — Research market rates for your tier
2. **Quote high** — Start 20-30% above your target
3. **Get it in writing** — Verbal agreements aren't enough
4. **Protect your rights** — Don't give away perpetuity cheap
5. **Build relationships** — Long-term value over one-time fees
6. **Be flexible strategically** — Lower fees for the right opportunities
7. **Have a walk-away point** — Know when to say no
8. **Consult professionals** — Lawyers for deals over $10K
9. **Track everything** — Use data to justify future rates
10. **Think long-term** — Each placement builds your value
