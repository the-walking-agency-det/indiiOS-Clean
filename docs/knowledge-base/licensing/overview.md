## Introduction

Music licensing is the process of obtaining permission to use copyrighted music in various media and commercial applications. For independent artists, understanding licensing is crucial—it can transform a song from a streaming revenue stream into a six-figure income source through a single placement.

### The Four Main License Types

| License Type | What It Covers | Typical Use Case |
|--------------|----------------|------------------|
| **Sync License** | Composition (publishing rights) | TV, film, commercials, games |
| **Master Use License** | Sound recording (master rights) | Same as above, for the actual recording |
| **Mechanical License** | Reproduction/distribution | Cover songs, physical/digital sales |
| **Grand Rights** | Dramatic/theatrical performances | Broadway, opera, theater |

## Sync Licensing

### What Is Sync Licensing?

A **synchronization license** ("sync") grants the right to pair music with moving visual media. The term comes from "synchronizing" audio with video.

### Where Sync Licenses Are Used

**Television:**
- Theme songs and title sequences
- Background source music (restaurant scenes, car radios)
- Emotional montages and transitions
- End credits

**Film:**
- Opening/closing credits
- Scene underscore
- Diegetic music (characters hear it)
- Trailer music

**Commercials:**
- Brand campaigns (national spots: $100K-$500K+)
- Regional ads ($10K-$50K)
- Local spots ($1K-$10K)
- Internet/social media ads ($500-$5K)

**Video Games:**
- Menu/lobby music
- In-game radio stations
- Cutscenes and cinematics
- Character themes

**Other Sync Uses:**
- YouTube videos (content ID claims)
- Podcasts and audiobooks
- Corporate training videos
- Livestreams and esports

### Sync License Fee Ranges

| Media Type | Indie/Unknown Artist | Mid-Tier Artist | Major Artist |
|------------|---------------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Indie Film (<$1M budget) | $500-$2,000 | $2,000-$10,000 | $10,000-$50,000 |
| Studio Film | $5,000-$15,000 | $15,000-$75,000 | $75,000-$500,000+ |
| TV Episode (network) | $1,000-$5,000 | $5,000-$25,000 | $25,000-$100,000+ |
| TV Episode (cable/streaming) | $500-$3,000 | $3,000-$15,000 | $15,000-$75,000 |
| National Commercial | $10,000-$50,000 | $50,000-$200,000 | $200,000-$1M+ |
| Video Game (AAA) | $5,000-$15,000 | $15,000-$50,000 | $50,000-$200,000+ |
| Indie Video Game | $500-$2,000 | $2,000-$10,000 | $10,000-$30,000 |
| Web/YouTube | $100-$500 | $500-$2,000 | $2,000-$10,000 |

**Note:** These are typical ranges. Super Bowl commercials have paid $1M+ for the right song. A song in Stranger Things can generate $100K+ in sync fees alone.

## Master Use vs. Sync Licenses

### The Two Copyrights in Every Recording

Every song recording contains **two separate copyrights**:

1. **The Composition (Publishing)** — The song itself: melody, lyrics, chords
   - Owned by: Songwriter(s) and/or publisher
   - Controlled by: Publisher or songwriter's PRO

2. **The Sound Recording (Master)** — The specific recorded version
   - Owned by: Record label or independent artist
   - Controlled by: Label or artist directly

### When You Need Both Licenses

For most sync placements, you need **both**:
- **Sync License** — From the publisher/songwriter (for the composition)
- **Master Use License** — From the master rights holder (for the recording)

**Example:** You want to use The Beatles' "Yesterday" in your film:
- Sync license from Sony/ATV (publisher) for the composition
- Master use license from Capitol/UMG (label) for the specific recording

### When You Only Need One

- **Cover songs in sync:** You only need a sync license for the composition. You'd create your own master, so no master use license needed.
- **Library music:** Production music libraries often own both rights and offer one-stop licensing.

## Mechanical Licenses

### What Is a Mechanical License?

A mechanical license grants the right to reproduce and distribute a copyrighted musical composition in **audio-only** formats (not video). The term dates back to player piano rolls—"mechanically" reproducing music.

### When You Need a Mechanical License

- **Cover songs:** Recording and distributing someone else's song
- **Physical media:** CDs, vinyl, cassette tapes
- **Digital downloads:** iTunes, Bandcamp downloads
- **Interactive streaming:** Spotify, Apple Music (handled by services)

### When You DON'T Need One

- **Non-interactive streaming:** Pandora, SiriusXM (services pay directly)
- **Sync uses:** Covered by sync license, not mechanical
- **Public domain songs:** Compositions published before 1929 (in the US)
- **Original songs you wrote:** You own the rights

### Obtaining Mechanical Licenses

**Option 1: Harry Fox Agency (HFA)**
- The largest mechanical rights organization in the US
- Represents 48,000+ publishers
- Online licensing through Songfile.com
- Rates set by statutory law (currently ~$0.091 per reproduction for songs under 5 minutes)

**Option 2: Easy Song Licensing**
- User-friendly alternative to HFA
- Handles the research if you don't know the publisher
- Slightly higher fees but more convenient
- Good for independent artists

**Option 3: DistroKid, CD Baby, etc.**
- Many distributors handle mechanicals automatically for covers
- Check your distributor's policy

**Option 4: Direct from Publisher**
- For songs not represented by HFA
- Contact publisher directly
- May negotiate different terms

### Compulsory License

US law provides a "compulsory license" for mechanical rights—you can record and distribute a cover song without the copyright owner's permission, provided you:
- Pay the statutory rate
- Don't change the lyrics or fundamental melody
- Give proper notice
- Account and pay royalties

## Grand Rights

### What Are Grand Rights?

**Grand rights** (dramatic rights) refer to the use of music in a **dramatic or theatrical context** where the music advances the plot or is integral to the story.

### Grand Rights vs. Small Rights

| Small Rights | Grand Rights |
|--------------|--------------|
| Non-dramatic performances | Dramatic/theatrical performances |
| Concerts, background music | Broadway, opera, musical theater |
| Covered by PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC) | Must be licensed directly from publisher |
| Set fees and blanket licenses | Negotiated per-production |

### Examples of Grand Rights Uses

- **Broadway musicals:** Using existing songs in a new musical
- **Opera:** Any opera performance
- **Ballet:** Dance performances with narrative
- **Dramatic plays:** Music integral to the plot
- **Musical revues:** Shows built around an artist's catalog

### Licensing Grand Rights

Grand rights **cannot** be obtained through PROs. You must:

1. **Identify the publisher** — Use ASCAP, BMI, or SESAC databases
2. **Contact the publisher directly** — Explain your production
3. **Negotiate terms** — Fees vary widely:
   - Community theater: $500-$2,000 per song
   - Regional theater: $2,000-$10,000 per song
   - Broadway: $25,000-$100,000+ per song

### Common Grand Rights Scenarios

**Creating a Jukebox Musical:**
Want to build a show around 80s hits? You'll need grand rights for every song. This is why shows like "Mamma Mia!" (ABBA) and "Jersey Boys" (Four Seasons) require massive licensing deals.

**Using Pop Songs in a Play:**
A character singing "Happy Birthday" at a party? Small rights (PRO). A character singing "Yesterday" as part of their emotional breakdown that advances the plot? Grand rights.

## Direct Licensing vs. PRO Licensing

### Performing Rights Organizations (PROs)

PROs collect royalties for **public performances** of your music:

| PRO | Territory | Notes |
|-----|-----------|-------|
| **ASCAP** | US | Artist-owned, democratic structure |
| **BMI** | US | Owned by broadcasters, largest catalog |
| **SESAC** | US | Invitation-only, smaller but prestigious |
| **GEMA** | Germany | Government-monitored |
| **PRS for Music** | UK | Merger of PRS and MCPS |
| **SOCAN** | Canada | Covers both performance and mechanical |

### What PROs Cover

- Radio airplay
- TV broadcasts (performance royalties, not sync)
- Live venue performances
- Streaming (performance portion)
- Retail background music
- Restaurants, bars, gyms

### Direct Licensing

**When to license directly:**
- Sync placements (TV, film, commercials)
- Video games
- YouTube directly with creators
- Corporate videos
- Any use not covered by PRO blanket licenses

**Benefits of direct licensing:**
- Higher fees (no PRO middleman)
- More control over usage
- Faster clearance for time-sensitive projects
- Build relationships with music supervisors

### The PRO + Direct Hybrid Model

Most successful artists use **both:**

1. **Register with a PRO** — Collect performance royalties automatically
2. **Pursue direct sync licenses** — Negotiate placements directly or through publishers/reps
3. **Use a publisher** — They handle both PRO registration and direct licensing

**Royalty Flow Example:**
```
Your song plays on Netflix show
         ↓
    Netflix pays PRO
         ↓
    PRO pays you (quarterly)
    
Your song is licensed for Netflix show
         ↓
    You negotiate sync fee directly
         ↓
    Netflix pays you upfront (immediate)
```

## Fee Negotiation

### Factors That Affect License Fees

**1. Media Type & Reach**
- Theatrical film > Streaming film > TV > Commercial > Web
- National > Regional > Local
- Prime time > Daytime > Late night

**2. Usage Type**
- Opening credits (most valuable)
- Closing credits
- Featured vocal (character singing along)
- Background instrumental
- Source music (car radio, restaurant)

**3. Term & Territory**
- In perpetuity vs. limited term (1-5 years)
- Worldwide vs. specific territories
- All media vs. specific platforms

**4. Exclusivity**
- Non-exclusive (can license elsewhere)
- Exclusive to product category (e.g., only car commercials)
- Exclusive to media type

**5. Artist Profile**
- Unknown/indie: $500-$5,000
- Emerging: $5,000-$25,000
- Established: $25,000-$100,000
- Major/iconic: $100,000-$1M+

### What to Charge: Guidelines

**If you're licensing your own music:**

| Your Status | TV (Cable) | TV (Network) | Indie Film | Studio Film | Commercial |
|-------------|------------|--------------|------------|-------------|------------|
| Completely unknown | $500-$1,500 | $1,000-$3,000 | $1,000-$3,000 | $3,000-$10,000 | $5,000-$15,000 |
| Some streaming presence | $1,500-$5,000 | $3,000-$10,000 | $3,000-$8,000 | $10,000-$30,000 | $15,000-$50,000 |
| Established indie | $5,000-$15,000 | $10,000-$30,000 | $8,000-$25,000 | $30,000-$75,000 | $50,000-$150,000 |

**Negotiation Tips:**

1. **Always ask for more than you expect** — They'll negotiate down
2. **Know your walk-away point** — Don't undervalue your work
3. **Consider the exposure** — A major film at a lower fee can lead to more
4. **Get everything in writing** — Term, territory, media, payment terms
5. **Consult a lawyer** — Especially for deals over $10,000
6. **Don't give away perpetuity cheap** — Charge 2-3x for "in perpetuity"

### Red Flags in License Deals

- **Work-for-hire clauses** — You lose all rights
- **Perpetuity for small fees** — They own it forever for pennies
- **All-media assignments** — Way too broad
- **No credit requirement** — You need attribution
- **Delayed payment terms** — Net 30 is standard; net 90+ is suspicious

## Music Libraries & Supervisors

### Music Libraries

**What they are:** Companies that pre-clear music for licensing, offering one-stop shopping for music supervisors.

**Major Libraries:**
- APM Music (Associated Production Music)
- Extreme Music
- Universal Production Music
- Warner Chappell Production Music
- Epidemic Sound
- Artlist
- Musicbed

**How they work:**
- You submit your music (often exclusively)
- They handle all licensing paperwork
- They take a cut (typically 50% of sync fees)
- You collect writer's share of performance royalties

**Pros:**
- Access to high-volume placements
- No negotiation required
- Passive income potential

**Cons:**
- Lower per-placement fees
- Often require exclusive representation
- Less control over usage

### Music Supervisors

**What they do:** Select and license music for film, TV, commercials, and games. They're the gatekeepers to sync placements.

**Finding Music Supervisors:**

1. **IMDb Pro** — Search "music supervisor" credits on shows/films you admire
2. **Guild of Music Supervisors** — Member directory at musicsupervisorsguild.com
3. **LinkedIn** — Search "music supervisor" + your city
4. **Music conferences** — SXSW, ASCAP Experience, Billboard conferences
5. **Agencies** — Some supervisors work through agencies like Formosa Group

**Approaching Music Supervisors:**

**DO:**
- Research their recent projects
- Send 3-5 songs max, not your entire catalog
- Include metadata (BPM, key, mood, lyrics)
- Make it easy to listen (private SoundCloud links, not attachments)
- Follow up once, then move on

**DON'T:**
- Send unsolicited attachments
- Spam their inbox
- Ask "did you listen yet?"
- Send music that doesn't fit their projects
- Be pushy or demanding

**The Pitch Email Template:**

```
Subject: Music submission for [Project Type] — [Your Name]

Hi [Name],

I hope you're doing well. I'm a [genre] artist based in [city] and 
I wanted to share some music that might work for your projects.

Recent placements: [if any, list them]
Similar artists: [2-3 comparable acts]

[Link to private playlist with 3-5 songs]

All tracks are one-stop cleared and ready to go.

Thanks for your time,
[Your name]
[Website]
[Social media]
```

### Building Relationships

Sync is a relationship business. Music supervisors work with people they trust:

1. **Attend industry events** — SXSW, A3C, music supervisor panels
2. **Join organizations** — Association of Independent Music Publishers (AIMP)
3. **Use rep services** — Companies like Terrorbird, Bank Robber Music pitch for you
4. **Be reliable** — Respond quickly, deliver what you promise
5. **Stay in touch** — Quarterly updates with new music, not constant spam

## Sample Clearance

### What Is Sample Clearance?

Using a portion of someone else's recording in your song requires **sample clearance**—permission from both the master rights holder AND the publisher of the sampled composition.

### The Two Clearances Needed

1. **Master Sample Clearance** — From the record label/artist who owns the recording
2. **Publishing Sample Clearance** — From the publisher/songwriter of the composition

### When You MUST Clear Samples

- Any recognizable portion of a copyrighted recording
- Even short clips (courts have ruled on samples as short as 2 seconds)
- Interpolations (re-recording the melody, not the actual recording)
- Drum breaks and loops from commercial recordings

### When You DON'T Need Clearance

- **Original composition** — You created it yourself
- **Public domain** — Recording and composition both pre-1929
- **Royalty-free samples** — From legitimate sample libraries (Splice, Loopmasters)
- **Soundalikes** — Recreation that doesn't copy the actual recording
- **De minimis** — So minimal it's not recognizable (legally risky to assume)

### The Sample Clearance Process

**Step 1: Identify What You Sampled**
- Song title and artist
- Record label that released it
- Publisher of the composition
- Exact timing of the sample in your song

**Step 2: Find the Rights Holders**
- **Master:** Check Discogs, AllMusic, or the album liner notes
- **Publishing:** Search ASCAP, BMI, SESAC databases

**Step 3: Submit a Sample Clearance Request**

Your request should include:
- Your song title and artist name
- Full details of the sampled recording
- Timing of the sample (e.g., "0:23-0:45")
- How the sample is used in your song
- Your distribution plans (streaming, physical, etc.)
- Proposed terms (buyout vs. royalty split)

**Step 4: Negotiate Terms**

Common deal structures:

| Structure | Description | Typical Terms |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Buyout** | One-time fee, no ongoing royalties | $1,000-$10,000+ depending on sample |
| **Royalty Split** | Percentage of your song's earnings | 15-50% of publishing, 15-50% of master |
| **Hybrid** | Upfront fee + reduced royalty | $2,500 + 25% of publishing |

**Step 5: Get It in Writing**

A proper sample clearance agreement should specify:
- Exact sample being cleared
- Your song that uses it
- Territories covered
- Term (perpetuity or limited)
- Fee/royalty structure
- Credit requirements
- Warranties and indemnification

### What If They Say No?

**Options:**
1. **Recreate the sample** — Play it yourself, change it enough to be unrecognizable
2. **Find a royalty-free alternative** — Splice, Loopmasters, etc.
3. **Use a sample clearance service** — Companies like Tracklib offer pre-cleared samples
4. **Remove the sample** — Reproduce the track without it

### Sample Clearance Services

**Tracklib** — Pre-cleared vinyl samples
- Subscription-based ($5.99-$29.99/month)
- Three license tiers based on sample length
- Clearance guaranteed

**Sample Clearance Companies:**
- **DMG Clearances** — Full-service clearance
- **Royalty Solutions** — Specializes in sample clearance
- **The Clearance Lab** — Boutique service for indie artists

### The Risk of uncleared Samples

**What can happen:**
- Your distributor rejects the release
- Content ID claims on YouTube
- Takedown notices on streaming platforms
- Lawsuits (rare but possible, especially for hits)
- Inability to license for sync (no supervisor will touch uncleared samples)

**The "Sue Me" Myth:**
Just because you're small doesn't mean you're safe. Content ID systems automatically flag samples. Plus, if your song blows up, the rights holders will come calling—and the fees go way up after the fact.

## One-Stop vs. Two-Stop Licensing

### One-Stop Licensing

**Definition:** One entity controls **both** the master and publishing rights, allowing a single license to clear the song.

**Common One-Stop Sources:**
- Production music libraries (APM, Extreme Music)
- Artists who own 100% of their masters and publishing
- Some indie labels that also publish their artists

**Advantages:**
- Faster clearance (hours instead of weeks)
- Single point of contact
- Often lower fees
- Preferred by music supervisors

**Disadvantages:**
- May be less unique/recognizable music
- Library music can feel generic

### Two-Stop Licensing

**Definition:** Separate entities control master and publishing, requiring two separate licenses.

**Typical Scenario:**
- Master: Owned by record label
- Publishing: Owned by separate publisher or songwriter

**Advantages:**
- Access to major commercial releases
- Can use iconic, recognizable songs
- More options overall

**Disadvantages:**
- Slower clearance process
- More expensive (two parties to pay)
- One party can derail the deal
- Music supervisors avoid when possible

### Making Your Music One-Stop

**As an independent artist, you can offer one-stop licensing by:**

1. **Own your masters** — Don't sign away master rights
2. **Control your publishing** — Don't sign a publishing deal, or ensure you retain sync rights
3. **Register with a PRO** — But don't give exclusive administration rights
4. **Use a rep that offers one-stop** — Some indie reps bundle both rights
5. **Document your ownership** — Have clear chain-of-title documentation

**Why it matters:**
Music supervisors work under tight deadlines. Given the choice between a $5,000 one-stop indie track and a $50,000 two-stop major label track, many will choose the one-stop—even if the music is slightly less perfect.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Understand both copyrights** — Master and publishing are separate; most sync needs both

2. **Register with a PRO** — Collect performance royalties, but don't rely on them for sync

3. **Own your rights** — The more you control, the more you earn and the easier you are to license

4. **Make your music one-stop** — Music supervisors prefer it; you'll get more placements

5. **Clear your samples** — Or use royalty-free alternatives; uncleared samples kill sync deals

6. **Build relationships** — Sync is who you know; network with supervisors and libraries

7. **Price appropriately** — Know your worth, but be willing to negotiate for the right exposure

8. **Get it in writing** — Every license should be documented, even "free" placements

9. **Consider a publisher or rep** — They take a cut but open doors you can't open alone

10. **Be patient** — Sync is a long game; placements can take months or years to materialize
