# Publishing Deal Types: Complete Comparison

Understanding the different types of publishing deals is crucial for making informed decisions about your career. Here's a breakdown of every major deal type, with real-world examples and when each makes sense.

---

## Quick Comparison Table

| Deal Type | You Keep (Publisher Share) | Advance | Creative Support | Admin Fee | Best For |
|-----------|---------------------------|---------|------------------|-----------|----------|
| **Administration** | 100% | None | None | 10-20% | Established writers, DIY artists |
| **Co-Publishing** | 50% | $10K-$500K | Moderate | 0% | Growing writers with momentum |
| **Full Publishing** | 0% | $50K-$1M+ | Extensive | 0% | Hit songwriters, major artists |
| **Joint Venture** | 50% | Varies | Shared | Split costs | Publishers starting their own imprint |
| **Single Song** | 50-100% | Per song | Minimal | 0% | One-off opportunities |

## 1. Administration Deals

### What It Is
You keep 100% of your publishing rights. The administrator handles registration, collection, and licensing for a fee.

### How It Works
- You assign **administration rights only** (not ownership)
- Admin collects your royalties worldwide
- Takes 10-20% fee off the top
- You retain creative control
- Typically 1-3 year terms, renewable

### Real Examples

**Songtrust**
- Fee: 15% of publishing royalties collected
- No advance
- Global collection network
- You keep 100% of rights
- Good for: Independent artists, producers, songwriters with existing catalog

**TuneCore Publishing**
- Fee: 15% of publishing royalties
- One-time setup fee (~$75)
- You keep 100% of rights
- Good for: DIY artists who already use TuneCore for distribution

**CD Baby Pro Publishing**
- Fee: 15% of publishing royalties
- Included with Pro distribution tier
- Good for: Artists already distributing through CD Baby

**Traditional Admin (Kobalt, Universal Music Publishing Group Admin)**
- Fee: 10-15%
- May offer small advances ($5K-$50K) for proven catalogs
- More hands-on support than DIY options
- Good for: Writers earning $25K+/year in publishing

### Pros
- Keep all your rights
- Full creative control
- Lower fees than other deal types
- Can terminate relatively easily

### Cons
- No advance money
- No creative support (no A&R, no writing camp invites)
- You're responsible for your own sync pitching
- No development investment

### When to Sign
- You have an established catalog generating royalties
- You want to maintain control
- You don't need upfront money
- You have your own sync relationships or don't prioritize sync

## 2. Co-Publishing Deals

### What It Is
The most common "traditional" publishing deal. You split the publisher's share 50/50 with the publisher.

### How It Works
- You assign 50% of your publisher's share to the publisher
- You keep: 100% writer's share + 50% publisher's share = 150% total
- Publisher keeps: 50% publisher's share = 50% total
- Publisher provides creative support, sync pitching, A&R
- Typically includes an advance

### The Math

**Without a Deal (Self-Published):**
- Writer's share: 100%
- Publisher's share: 100%
- **You collect: 200%**

**With Co-Pub Deal:**
- Writer's share: 100% (yours)
- Publisher's share: 50% yours, 50% publisher's
- **You collect: 150%**
- **Publisher collects: 50%**

### Real Deal Examples

**Indie Publisher Co-Pub (e.g., Domino, Secretly Canadian)**
- Advance: $10,000 - $50,000
- Term: 3-5 years, sometimes life of copyright
- Creative support: Moderate (some sync pitching, some A&R)
- Good for: Indie artists with growing buzz

**Major Publisher Co-Pub (Sony, Warner, Universal)**
- Advance: $50,000 - $500,000+
- Term: Usually life of copyright (but with reversion clauses)
- Creative support: Extensive (writing camps, artist relationships, major sync opportunities)
- Good for: Proven hit writers, artists with radio play

**Sample Deal Terms (Realistic)**
```
Advance: $75,000 (recoupable)
Term: 3 years initial + 2 option periods
Rights: 50% of publisher's share
Reversion: 15 years after term ends
Minimum Delivery: 12 songs per year
Exclusivity: Yes (can't sign elsewhere)
```

### Pros
- Advance money to fund your career
- Professional creative support
- Access to sync supervisors and A&R
- Established publisher's reputation helps placements
- Still retain significant ownership (150% of 200%)

### Cons
- Giving up 25% of total publishing revenue (50% of the 100% publisher share)
- Advances must be recouped before you see additional royalties
- Locked into exclusivity
- May have delivery requirements (must write X songs per year)

### When to Sign
- You have momentum (songs being cut, sync placements happening)
- You need advance money to quit your day job
- You want professional creative support
- Your catalog is earning enough that 25% haircut is worth the services

### When to Walk Away
- The advance is too small to justify giving up rights
- Term is life of copyright with no reversion
- Publisher can't demonstrate real sync or placement success
- You're not ready to commit to delivery requirements

## 3. Full Publishing Deals ("Pub-to-Pub")

### What It Is
You assign 100% of your publisher's share to the publisher. They become the sole publisher.

### How It Works
- You keep: 100% writer's share only
- Publisher keeps: 100% publisher's share
- **You collect: 100%**
- **Publisher collects: 100%**
- Maximum creative support, maximum advance

### The Math

**Full Pub Deal:**
- Writer's share: 100% (yours)
- Publisher's share: 100% (theirs)
- **You collect: 100%**
- **Publisher collects: 100%**

### Real Deal Examples

**Major Full Pub (Sony, Warner Chappell, Universal)**
- Advance: $250,000 - $1,000,000+
- Term: Life of copyright
- Creative support: Full team dedicated to you
- Good for: Established hit songwriters, major artists

**Sample Deal Terms**
```
Advance: $300,000 (recoupable)
Term: Life of copyright
Rights: 100% of publisher's share
Reversion: 25 years after delivery
Minimum Delivery: 15 songs per year
Exclusivity: Yes
Writing Camps: Included
Travel Budget: $10,000/year
```

### Pros
- Largest advances
- Maximum creative support and resources
- Full publisher muscle behind your catalog
- Often includes perks (writing camps, travel, relationships)

### Cons
- Giving up 50% of total publishing revenue
- Life of copyright terms (though reversion clauses are standard now)
- Most restrictive deals
- Harder to get out of if things don't work

### When to Sign
- You're a proven hit songwriter
- You need significant capital
- The publisher has a track record of success with writers like you
- You value the relationships and opportunities over ownership

### When to Walk Away
- The advance isn't life-changing money
- No reversion clause
- Publisher's roster is too big (you'll get lost)
- You're not ready to commit to the delivery requirements

## 4. Joint Venture (JV) Deals

### What It Is
You form a partnership with a major publisher. You bring the catalog/vision, they bring infrastructure.

### How It Works
- You create a separate publishing entity (e.g., "Your Name Music")
- Major publisher provides admin, distribution, sync team
- Profits split 50/50 after expenses
- You often get an imprint name and logo

### Real Examples
- Producer/songwriter imprints under major publishers
- Artist-owned publishing companies
- Producer collectives with publisher backing

### Pros
- Keep 50% ownership at the entity level
- Major publisher infrastructure
- Your own imprint/brand
- More control than traditional deals

### Cons
- You're running a business (administrative burden)
- Shared profits
- Still tied to major publisher

### When to Sign
- You have a catalog and want to sign other writers
- You want to build a publishing brand
- You have business acumen or a manager who does

---

## 5. Single Song Deals

### What It Is
Publisher licenses or buys rights to one specific song.

### How It Works
- Can be an assignment (they buy the rights) or license
- One-time payment or ongoing split
- No long-term commitment

### Real Examples
- Film/TV sync houses buying rights to one song
- Publishers picking up a viral TikTok hit
- Song specific to a major artist project

### Pros
- No long-term lock-in
- Get paid for one song
- Can be lucrative for a hit

### Cons
- No relationship building
- One-time payment vs. ongoing catalog value
- No career support

## Deal Red Flags 🚩

Watch out for these terms:

1. **Life of copyright with NO reversion clause** — You should always be able to get your rights back eventually

2. **Cross-collateralization** — They can use earnings from Song A to recoup advances on Song B

3. **Excessive delivery requirements** — Writing 20+ songs a year while touring is unrealistic

4. **No audit rights** — You should be able to audit their books

5. **Vague creative support promises** — Get specifics on what they'll actually do

6. **High admin fees on top of rights splits** — Double-dipping

---

## Negotiation Tips

### Always Negotiate:
- **Reversion clause** — Aim for 10-15 years post-term
- **Advance size** — Ask for 2-3x what you need to account for taxes and recoupment time
- **Delivery requirements** — Make them realistic for your workflow
- **Territory** — Try to keep foreign rights if they don't have strong international presence
- **Option periods** — Shorter is better when you're starting out

### Get a Lawyer
Always use an experienced music attorney (not your cousin who does real estate). They typically charge:
- $500-$1,000/hour, or
- 5-10% of the advance

This is non-negotiable. A bad deal lasts forever.

## Decision Framework

**Start with Self-Publishing/Admin if:**
- You're early in your career
- You have fewer than 20 songs
- Your publishing income is under $10K/year
- You value control over money

**Consider Co-Pub if:**
- You have momentum (songs being cut, sync happening)
- You need $25K-$100K to level up
- You want professional support
- You have 30+ songs in your catalog

**Consider Full Pub if:**
- You're a proven hit writer
- You need $250K+ to make sense
- You want maximum resources
- You're ready to commit long-term
