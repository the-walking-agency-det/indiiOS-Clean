# Self-Publishing Guide: Doing It Yourself

You don't need a publisher to collect your publishing royalties. This guide covers everything you need to know to self-publish successfully — from setup to collection to when (and if) you should consider signing a deal.

---

## What Is Self-Publishing?

Self-publishing means you act as your own publisher. You:
- Register your own songs with PROs
- Collect your own royalties
- Handle your own licensing
- Keep 100% of the publisher's share (plus your 100% writer's share = 200% total)

**The trade-off:** You do all the work, but you keep all the money and control.

---

## Step-by-Step Setup

### Step 1: Join a PRO (Performance Rights Organization)

You need to be affiliated with a PRO to collect performance royalties. In the US, your options are:

| PRO | Best For | Notable Features |
|-----|----------|------------------|
| **ASCAP** | Songwriters who want flexibility | Can join as writer and publisher separately; no membership dues for writers |
| **BMI** | Songwriters who want stability | Non-profit; writer membership is free |
| **SESAC** | Established writers (invite-only) | Smaller, more curated; better international rates |
| **GMR** | Hit songwriters (invite-only) | Aggressive rate negotiations; very selective |

**You can only join ONE as a writer.** Choose based on:
- Which PRO your collaborators use
- Your genre (some PROs have better relationships in certain spaces)
- Personal preference

**To join:**
1. Visit the PRO's website
2. Complete application (takes 10-15 minutes)
3. Pay membership fee (if applicable)
4. Receive your IPI/CAE number (your unique identifier)

### Step 2: Create Your Publishing Entity

To collect the publisher's share, you need a publishing entity:

**ASCAP:**
- Create a separate publisher membership
- Cost: $50 application fee
- Name your publishing company (e.g., "Your Name Music")
- Get a separate IPI number for the entity

**BMI:**
- Create an "affiliated publisher" account
- Cost: $150-$250 depending on structure
- Name your publishing company
- Get a separate IPI number

**Important:** Your publishing entity name must be unique. Check availability before applying.

### Step 3: Register Your Songs

For every song you write, register with your PRO:

**Required Information:**
- Song title
- All co-writers and their PROs/IPIs
- Split percentages (must total 100%)
- Publishers for each writer
- Artist/performer (if known)
- Album/project (if known)

**Timing:** Register BEFORE the song is released or performed publicly.

### Step 4: Set Up Mechanical Royalty Collection

Mechanical royalties require separate collection:

**For Streaming (US):**
- The MLC (Mechanical Licensing Collective) now handles streaming mechanicals
- Sign up at theMLC.com
- Free to join
- They collect from Spotify, Apple Music, etc.

**For Downloads/Physical (US):**
- Harry Fox Agency (HFA) — Songfile.com
- Cost: $0 (they take a percentage)
- For DIY: Use services like Easy Song Licensing

**For International:**
- Use an admin service (Songtrust, TuneCore) OR
- Register directly with foreign mechanical societies (complex)

### Step 5: Set Up Sync Licensing

You have three options for sync:

**Option A: Do It Yourself**
- Build relationships with music supervisors
- Respond to briefs on platforms like Musicbed, Songtradr
- Create a pitch deck with your best tracks
- Handle negotiations and contracts

**Option B: Hire a Sync Rep**
- Commission-based (usually 20-30% of sync fees)
- They pitch your catalog
- You keep control
- Good middle ground

**Option C: Non-Exclusive Sync Libraries**
- Upload to libraries like Artlist, Epidemic Sound, Musicbed
- They take a cut but handle everything
- Lower fees but more volume

## What You Give Up vs. What You Keep

### What You Keep (vs. Co-Pub Deal)
- 100% of publisher's share (vs. 50%)
- Full creative control
- Ability to license however you want
- No delivery requirements
- No recoupment obligations

### What You Give Up (vs. Traditional Deal)
- Advance money
- Professional A&R support
- Existing sync supervisor relationships
- Writing camp invitations
- Administrative support

### The Math

**Scenario: Your song earns $10,000 in publishing royalties**

| Deal Type | You Receive | Publisher Receives |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Self-Published | $10,000 (200%) | $0 |
| Admin (15% fee) | $8,500 | $1,500 (fee) |
| Co-Pub (50% split) | $7,500 (150%) | $2,500 (50%) |
| Full Pub (100% assignment) | $5,000 (100%) | $5,000 (100%) |

**But remember:** A good publisher might generate MORE than $10,000 through their efforts. The question is whether that 25-50% haircut is worth it.

## When Self-Publishing Makes Sense

### ✅ Do It Yourself If:
- You have fewer than 30 songs
- Your publishing income is under $10,000/year
- You have strong DIY skills
- You enjoy the business side
- You have existing sync relationships
- You value control over convenience
- You're not offered a meaningful advance

### ✅ Consider a Publisher If:
- You're earning $25,000+/year in publishing
- You have too many songs to manage
- International collection is getting complex
- You want professional sync pitching
- You need advance money
- You want A&R and creative support

## Common Self-Publishing Mistakes

### 1. Not Registering Songs Promptly
**Problem:** Royalties go into the "black box" if songs aren't registered before use.
**Solution:** Register within 30 days of completion.

### 2. Ignoring International Collection
**Problem:** You're leaving money on the table overseas.
**Solution:** Use an admin service or register with foreign societies.

### 3. No Split Sheets
**Problem:** Disputes over ownership percentages.
**Solution:** Always complete split sheets before leaving the studio.

### 4. Not Monitoring Usage
**Problem:** Your music is being used without proper licensing.
**Solution:** Use Content ID (YouTube), Tunesat, or similar monitoring services.

### 5. Missing Mechanical Royalties
**Problem:** Only collecting performance, not mechanical.
**Solution:** Register with The MLC and HFA.

### 6. Poor Metadata
**Problem:** Songs can't be matched to you.
**Solution:** Use consistent spelling, include all identifiers, double-check everything.

## Building Your Self-Publishing Business

### Year 1: Foundation
- Join PRO and create publishing entity
- Register all existing songs
- Set up The MLC and HFA accounts
- Create split sheet templates
- Organize your catalog

### Year 2: Optimization
- Evaluate if you need an admin service
- Start pitching for sync yourself
- Build relationships with music supervisors
- Track your royalties monthly

### Year 3+: Growth
- Consider hiring a sync rep
- Evaluate if you're ready for a traditional deal
- Expand internationally
- Potentially sign other writers to your publishing entity

---

## The "Graduate" Path

Many successful songwriters start self-published and "graduate" to deals:

1. **Phase 1:** Self-publish, build catalog, learn the business
2. **Phase 2:** Sign admin deal for international collection
3. **Phase 3:** Sign co-pub deal when you have momentum
4. **Phase 4:** Renegotiate or reversion — own your masters, license your publishing

This path keeps you in control while you're learning, then leverages publisher resources when you have leverage to negotiate.

## Final Thoughts

Self-publishing isn't for everyone, but it's more viable than ever. The tools exist to collect your own money worldwide. The question is whether the time investment is worth the extra revenue and control.

**My advice:** Start self-published. Learn the business. Build your catalog. When a publisher offers you a deal, you'll be educated enough to negotiate from strength — or confident enough to say no.
