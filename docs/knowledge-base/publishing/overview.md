# Music Publishing: The Complete Guide

## What Is Music Publishing?

Music publishing is the business of protecting, promoting, and monetizing the **musical composition** (the song itself — lyrics and melody). This is separate from the **sound recording** (the master), which is the actual audio file people stream or buy.

Think of it this way:
- **Sound Recording** = The specific performance captured in the studio (owned by artist/label)
- **Musical Composition** = The underlying song — melody, lyrics, harmony (owned by songwriter/publisher)

Every time a song generates revenue, there are two separate income streams: one for the master, one for the publishing.

---

## The 200% Pie Explained

This is the foundation of music publishing economics. Every song has **200% of rights** to distribute:

### The Songwriter's Share (100%)
- Also called the "writer's share"
- Represents the creative contribution
- Cannot be sold or transferred permanently in the US
- Always paid directly to the songwriter by PROs (Performance Rights Organizations)

### The Publisher's Share (100%)
- Represents the business/administrative contribution
- Can be assigned, sold, or licensed
- Whoever controls this collects and distributes the money

**Example:** If you write a song alone and haven't signed any deals:
- You own 100% songwriter share
- You own 100% publisher share
- You collect 200% total

When you sign a publishing deal, you're typically assigning some or all of your **publisher's share** — never your songwriter's share.

## Where Publishing Money Comes From

### 1. Performance Royalties
Generated when your song is performed publicly:
- Radio airplay (terrestrial and satellite)
- TV and film placements
- Streaming services (yes, streaming pays performance royalties)
- Live performances (venues pay PROs, PROs pay songwriters)
- Bars, restaurants, retail stores playing music
- Jukeboxes

**Collected by:** ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, GMR (US PROs)

### 2. Mechanical Royalties
Generated from the reproduction of your song:
- Physical sales (CDs, vinyl)
- Digital downloads (iTunes)
- Streaming (yes, again — separate from performance)
- Ringtones

**Collected by:**
- Harry Fox Agency (HFA) in the US
- Mechanical licensing collectives (MLC) for streaming
- Direct licenses from record labels

**Rate:** Currently ~12.4 cents per reproduction (physical/download) or ~15.1% of revenue (streaming)

### 3. Synchronization (Sync) Fees
One-time fees for pairing your song with visual media:
- TV shows and films
- Commercials
- Video games
- YouTube (Content ID)
- Trailers

**Negotiated directly** — no set rate. Can range from $500 for a small indie film to $500,000+ for a major commercial.

### 4. Print Rights
Sheet music sales, lyric reprints, karaoke lyrics.

### 5. Grand Rights
Theatrical performances (musicals, opera, ballet).

## The Role of a Publisher

A music publisher's job is to:

1. **Register songs** with PROs and mechanical rights organizations worldwide
2. **Collect royalties** from all sources globally
3. **License songs** for sync placements
4. **Pitch songs** to artists, supervisors, and brands
5. **Administer splits** — ensuring all co-writers get paid correctly
6. **Protect copyrights** — fighting infringement, policing uses
7. **Develop songwriters** — setting up writing sessions, career guidance

### Do You Need a Publisher?

**You don't need a publisher to collect your money.** PROs pay songwriters directly. However, a good publisher:
- Has relationships with sync supervisors
- Has infrastructure to collect internationally
- Can advance money against future earnings
- Provides creative and career support

## Key Terms to Know

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Catalog** | All songs owned/controlled by a writer or publisher |
| **Controlled Composition** | A song you own or control publishing rights to |
| **Split Sheet** | Document showing ownership percentages among co-writers |
| **Work-for-Hire** | You write, they own 100% (common in film/TV scoring) |
| **Reversion** | Getting your rights back after a certain period |
| **Option Period** | Timeframe a publisher has exclusive rights |
| **Recoupment** | Earning back an advance before receiving additional royalties |

## The Split Sheet

Before you leave the studio, fill out a split sheet:

```
Song Title: ___________________
Date Written: ___________________
Artist/Project: ___________________

Writers:
1. Name: _____________ | PRO: _____ | IPI#: _________ | Split: ___%
2. Name: _____________ | PRO: _____ | IPI#: _________ | Split: ___%
3. Name: _____________ | PRO: _____ | IPI#: _________ | Split: ___%

Publishing:
1. Publisher: _________ | Split: ___%
2. Publisher: _________ | Split: ___%

Signatures: ___________ ___________ ___________
Date: _________________
```

**Total writer splits must equal 100%. Total publishing splits must equal 100%.**

## When to Consider a Publishing Deal

### Signs You Might Be Ready:
- You're earning $10,000+/year in publishing royalties
- You're getting regular sync placement opportunities
- You have songs recorded by other artists
- You're writing with established artists/producers
- International collection is becoming complex

### Signs You Should Wait:
- You're still building your catalog
- Your royalties are minimal
- You value complete control over your work
- You have the capacity to self-administer
