# Album Artwork

Album artwork is your music's visual ambassador. It appears in streaming platforms, social media, physical packaging, merchandise, and promotional materials. This guide covers technical specifications, rights management, hiring designers, and budgeting for independent artists.

## Technical Specifications

### Streaming Platform Requirements

**Universal Standard (Apply to All Platforms):**

| Specification | Requirement |
|--------------|-------------|
| **Minimum Size** | 3000 x 3000 pixels |
| **Recommended Size** | 4000 x 4000 pixels or higher |
| **Aspect Ratio** | 1:1 (square) |
| **Color Mode** | RGB (sRGB color space) |
| **Bit Depth** | 24-bit minimum |
| **File Format** | JPG, PNG, or TIFF |
| **File Size** | Under 25MB (platform-specific) |
| **Resolution** | 300 DPI minimum for source files |

**Platform-Specific Notes:**

- **Spotify:** Displays as small as 64x64px in playlist view; must read at thumbnail size
- **Apple Music:** Requires 3000x3000 minimum; displays on high-DPI Retina screens
- **Bandcamp:** Accepts up to 10MB; supports PNG with transparency
- **YouTube Music:** Pulls from Google Play Music database; same specs as streaming standard
- **Tidal:** Emphasizes high-quality artwork; 4000x4000 recommended

### Print Specifications

**Vinyl Records (12"):**

| Element | Dimensions | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Front Cover | 12.375" x 12.375" | Include 0.125" bleed |
| Back Cover | 12.375" x 12.375" | Include barcode, tracklist, credits |
| Spine | 0.25" - 0.5" | Varies by pressing plant |
| Inner Sleeve | 12" x 12" | Optional lyrics, artwork |
| Labels | 4" diameter | Center hole at 1.5" |

**CD Packaging:**

| Format | Dimensions | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Jewel Case Insert | 4.75" x 4.75" | Front booklet |
| Jewel Case Back | 5.906" x 4.625" | Includes spine (0.25") |
| Digipak Front | 4.75" x 4.75" | Cardboard fold |
| Digipak Back | 5.5" x 4.75" | Varies by panel count |

**Cassettes:**

| Element | Dimensions |
|---------|-----------|
| J-Card Front | 4" x 2.5" |
| J-Card Back | 4" x 2.5" |
| J-Card Spine | 0.25" - 0.5" |
| Cassette Label | 3.875" x 2.375" |

### Color Modes: RGB vs. CMYK

**RGB (Red, Green, Blue)** — Digital Only
- Used for: Streaming platforms, social media, websites, digital displays
- Color gamut: Wider, more vibrant
- File formats: JPG, PNG, TIFF

**CMYK (Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Key/Black)** — Print Only
- Used for: Vinyl jackets, CD booklets, posters, merchandise
- Color gamut: Narrower, more limited
- File formats: TIFF, PDF (with bleed marks)

**Critical:** Never use RGB files for print. Colors will shift dramatically—especially bright blues, greens, and oranges. Always request CMYK conversions from your designer or convert using professional color management.

### Safe Zones and Bleed

**Bleed:** Extend artwork 0.125" (1/8 inch) beyond the trim line to ensure no white edges after cutting.

**Safe Zone:** Keep critical text/logos 0.25" inside the trim line to avoid being cut off.

**Center Hole (Vinyl):** Keep essential imagery outside the 4" diameter label area.

## Rights and Ownership

### Work-for-Hire vs. Licensing

This is the most critical decision in artwork agreements. Get it wrong, and you may not actually own your own album cover.

**Work-for-Hire (Recommended for Album Art):**

- You own the copyright completely
- Artist cannot reuse the work for other clients
- You control all future uses (merch, reissues, licensing)
- No ongoing payments or royalties to the artist
- Must be explicitly stated in writing to be valid

**Licensing (Use with Caution):**

- Artist retains copyright ownership
- You receive limited usage rights
- May have restrictions (time limits, media types, territories)
- May require additional fees for new uses
- Artist can license the same work to others

**Standard Recommendation:** Album artwork should always be work-for-hire. You're building a permanent asset that represents your brand indefinitely.

### Contract Essentials

Every artwork agreement should include:

**1. Scope of Work**
- Specific deliverables (front cover, back cover, digital files, print files)
- Number of revision rounds included
- Timeline with milestones

**2. Rights Assignment**
- Explicit work-for-hire language
- Transfer of all copyrights to you
- Right to modify/adapt for future uses
- Moral rights waiver (if applicable in your jurisdiction)

**3. Usage Rights Confirmation**
- All media (digital, print, merchandise)
- All territories worldwide
- Perpetual (no time limit)
- Right to sublicense (for sync, compilation appearances)

**4. Payment Terms**
- Total fee
- Payment schedule (deposit, milestones, final)
- Kill fee if project is cancelled
- Additional revision rates

**5. Credit Requirements**
- How the artist is credited (if at all)
- Where credit must appear
- Size/placement of credit

**6. Warranties**
- Artist warrants they created original work
- No infringement on third-party rights
- Right to enter the agreement

### Stock Imagery and Fonts

**Stock Photos/Illustrations:**
- Ensure you purchase extended/commercial license (not just standard)
- Verify license covers merchandise and derivative works
- Document license terms and keep receipts
- Some licenses exclude use in logos or as primary artwork

**Fonts:**
- Commercial use license required
- Some fonts prohibit use in logos
- Web font licenses are separate from desktop licenses
- Document all font licenses

## Hiring Designers

### Where to Find Designers

**Portfolio Platforms:**
- **Behance** — Adobe's creative community, extensive music artwork
- **Dribbble** — Designer showcase, invite-only (higher quality barrier)
- **Instagram** — Search hashtags: #albumart #albumcover #musicdesign

**Specialized Services:**
- **Fiverr/Upwork** — Budget options, variable quality
- **99designs** — Contest model, multiple concepts
- **Working Not Working** — Premium creative talent
- **Music-specific designers** — Often found via label credits

**Direct Outreach:**
- Check credits on albums you admire
- Attend gallery shows, art school exhibitions
- Ask other musicians for recommendations

### Evaluating Portfolios

**Look For:**
- Consistent quality across multiple projects
- Experience with music industry work
- Versatility in style (or perfect match to your aesthetic)
- Understanding of technical requirements
- Strong typography skills

**Red Flags:**
- Only one style repeated endlessly
- No music-related work
- Poor communication in initial contact
- No contract or vague terms
- Reluctance to discuss rights

### The Hiring Process

**1. Initial Contact**
- Introduce yourself and your project
- Share reference images and your music
- Ask about availability and general rate range
- Request relevant portfolio examples

**2. Creative Discussion**
- Schedule call or detailed email exchange
- Share your vision, references, and goals
- Discuss their creative approach
- Establish rapport and communication style

**3. Proposal Review**
- Detailed scope of work
- Timeline with milestones
- Total cost and payment terms
- Revision policy

**4. Contract Signing**
- Use the work-for-hire template above
- Have a lawyer review if budget allows
- Both parties sign before work begins

**5. Deposit Payment**
- Standard: 50% upfront
- Never pay 100% before seeing work
- Use contracts that specify kill fees

## Budgets and Rates

### North American Market Rates (2024-2025)

**Single Artwork (Digital Only):**
- Emerging designer: $150-$400
- Mid-level designer: $400-$800
- Established designer: $800-$2,000
- Premium/celebrity designer: $2,000-$10,000+

**Album Artwork (Full Package):**
- Emerging designer: $500-$1,500
- Mid-level designer: $1,500-$4,000
- Established designer: $4,000-$10,000
- Premium/celebrity designer: $10,000-$50,000+

**What's Included in "Full Package":**
- Front cover artwork
- Back cover design
- Spine design
- Digital files (all streaming specs)
- Print files (CMYK, with bleed)
- 2-3 rounds of revisions
- Social media assets (optional, may be extra)

### Additional Costs

**Photography/Illustration:**
- Stock imagery: $50-$500 per image (extended license)
- Custom photography: $500-$5,000+ (see Photography module)
- Custom illustration: $300-$2,000+
- 3D rendering: $500-$3,000+

**Typography/Lettering:**
- Custom lettering: $500-$2,000
- Font licensing: $50-$500 per font

**Revisions:**
- Typically 2-3 rounds included
- Additional rounds: $100-$300/hour or flat per-round fee

**Rush Fees:**
- Standard turnaround: 2-4 weeks
- Rush (1 week): +25-50%
- Emergency (few days): +50-100%

### Budget Strategies

**Low Budget ($200-$500):**
- Art school students (quality varies, but often excellent)
- Emerging designers building portfolios
- Template-based customization (limited originality)
- DIY with professional feedback

**Mid Budget ($1,000-$3,000):**
- Working professional designers
- Full custom artwork
- Print and digital deliverables
- Multiple revision rounds

**Higher Budget ($5,000+):**
- Established designers with music industry experience
- Complex production (photography, illustration, 3D)
- Comprehensive asset packages
- Priority scheduling

## The Design Process

### Phase 1: Discovery (Week 1)

**Your Preparation:**
- Compile 10-15 reference images (what you like AND what you don't)
- Write a creative brief (1-2 pages)
- Share your music and lyrics
- Define your audience and goals

**The Brief Should Include:**
- Album concept and themes
- Mood and emotional tone
- Color preferences (or restrictions)
- Typography style preferences
- Must-include elements
- Must-avoid elements
- Technical requirements

### Phase 2: Concepts (Week 2)

**What to Expect:**
- 2-3 distinct visual directions
- Rough/mockup quality (not final)
- Different approaches to your brief

**How to Evaluate:**
- Which direction feels most "you"?
- Which will stand out at thumbnail size?
- Which has longevity (won't look dated)?
- Which works across all required formats?

**Providing Feedback:**
- Be specific, not subjective
- Good: "The type feels too aggressive; can we try something more delicate?"
- Bad: "I don't like it"
- Reference specific elements: color, composition, typography, imagery

### Phase 3: Refinement (Week 3)

**Revision Rounds:**
- Round 1: Major changes to chosen direction
- Round 2: Refinements and adjustments
- Round 3: Final polish (if included)

**Scope Control:**
- Major direction changes after approval = new project
- Additional rounds beyond contract = additional fees
- Be decisive; indecision costs money

### Phase 4: Delivery (Week 4)

**Required Deliverables:**
- Master files (PSD, AI, or equivalent)
- High-res JPG/PNG (digital)
- Print-ready PDF/TIFF (CMYK, with bleed)
- Web-optimized versions
- Social media crops (if contracted)

**File Organization:**

## Common Mistakes

**Technical Errors:**
- Using RGB files for print (color shifts)
- Insufficient resolution (pixelation)
- Missing bleed (white edges on print)
- Text too small for vinyl/CD scale

**Rights Issues:**
- No written agreement
- Licensing instead of work-for-hire for primary artwork
- Unclear stock image licenses
- Font license violations

**Creative Mistakes:**
- Overly complex designs (don't read at small sizes)
- Illegible text/typography
- Trend-chasing (dated within a year)
- Ignoring brand consistency

**Process Mistakes:**
- Insufficient reference sharing
- Vague feedback
- Too many decision-makers
- Scope creep without budget adjustment

## Platform-Specific Considerations

### Spotify

- Thumbnail visibility is critical (64x64px minimum display)
- Avoid tiny details that disappear at small sizes
- Test your artwork at multiple sizes before finalizing
- Consider how it appears in dark mode (most users)

### Apple Music

- High-resolution displays benefit from 4000x4000+ source files
- "Now Playing" view shows larger artwork—detail matters
- Connect/artist pages use artwork prominently

### Bandcamp

- Fans often buy digital + physical together
- Artwork appears in player, download, and physical product
- PNG transparency supported (useful for creative layouts)
- Consider how artwork pairs with vinyl variants

### Social Media

- Instagram: Square crops (1:1) work best
- Stories: 9:16 aspect ratio—consider how artwork adapts
- Twitter/X: Horizontal crops (16:9) often better
- TikTok: Vertical video format—artwork may need recomposition
