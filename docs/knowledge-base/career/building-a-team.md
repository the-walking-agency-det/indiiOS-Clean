# Building Your Team

A music career is not a solo sport. At every stage, the right team members amplify what you can accomplish. But hiring too early wastes money; hiring too late costs opportunities. Here's how to think about building your team as you grow.

---

## The Core Team: Who Does What

### Manager
**What they do:** Your strategic partner. Managers handle day-to-day business, coordinate releases, build your team, and advocate for your long-term vision. They connect the dots between creative and commercial.

**When to hire:** When you have momentum (consistent releases, growing fanbase, incoming opportunities) but you're drowning in logistics. If you're spending more time on emails than music, it's time.

**Commission:** Typically 15-20% of gross income. Some take 10% early on with escalators as you grow.

**Red flags:** Managers who want to change your sound, make decisions without consulting you, or lack transparent accounting.

---

### Booking Agent
**What they do:** Get you paid shows. Agents negotiate fees, route tours, and maintain relationships with promoters and talent buyers. They live in the live music ecosystem.

**When to hire:** When you're ready to tour regularly and have proven you can draw crowds. Agents typically want to see you selling 100-200 tickets in your home market before they'll take you on.

**Commission:** 10% of performance fees (industry standard).

**The split:** Many agents work at agencies that take a percentage of their commission. Don't be surprised if your agent mentions this.

---

### Music Attorney
**What they do:** Protect you legally. They review contracts, negotiate deals, handle disputes, and structure business entities. A good music lawyer knows industry norms and fights for terms that matter.

**When to hire:** Before you sign anything significant—recording contracts, publishing deals, management agreements, label deals. The "I'll have my lawyer look at this" moment should come early, not after you've already agreed to terms.

**Fee structure:** Hourly ($300-800/hr for experienced attorneys) or percentage of the deal (5% for major deals). Some do flat fees for standard agreements.

**The retainer relationship:** Many artists keep a lawyer on a small retainer for quick questions, paying hourly for heavy lifting.

---

### Business Manager
**What they do:** Handle your money. They pay bills, manage cash flow, prepare taxes, track royalties, and help with financial planning. They ensure you don't accidentally go broke while successful.

**When to hire:** When money gets complicated—multiple income streams, touring revenue, merchandise, royalties from multiple sources. If you're earning $100K+/year, you probably need one.

**Fee structure:** Monthly retainer ($500-3000/month) or percentage of income (3-5%).

**The CPA vs. business manager distinction:** A CPA prepares taxes. A business manager handles ongoing financial operations. Some do both; many artists have both.

---

### Publicist
**What they do:** Get you press coverage. They pitch stories to media, coordinate interviews, and manage your public narrative. They don't guarantee coverage—no one can—but they know who to pitch and how.

**When to hire:** When you have a specific story to tell (album release, tour announcement, significant milestone) and the budget to support a campaign. Publicity is expensive; make sure you have something worth publicizing.

**Fee structure:** Monthly retainer ($2000-5000/month for indie-focused publicists) or project-based for specific campaigns.

**DIY first:** Many artists handle their own PR early on, building relationships with local media and niche blogs before hiring professionals.

---

## The Hiring Sequence

### Phase 1: DIY (0-2 years)
You are your own manager, agent, and publicist. Learn by doing. Make mistakes with small stakes. Build relationships directly.

**Focus:** Making great music, playing local shows, building a small but engaged fanbase.

### Phase 2: Manager First (2-4 years)
When opportunities start coming in faster than you can handle them, hire a manager. They'll help you decide what to say yes to and what to decline.

**Why manager before agent?** A good manager helps you become agent-ready. They'll know when you've reached the threshold where an agent can actually get you gigs.

### Phase 3: Agent + Lawyer (3-5 years)
Once you're touring regularly and deals are on the table, add an agent and attorney. The agent grows your live revenue; the attorney protects you as contracts get complex.

### Phase 4: Full Team (5+ years)
Business manager, publicist, possibly secondary agents (film/TV, international), social media manager, tour manager. Your team grows with your career.

## Finding the Right People

### Referrals Work Best
The music industry runs on relationships. Ask artists you respect who they work with. Ask managers who they recommend for agents. Personal introductions beat cold emails.

### Attend Industry Events
Conferences like SXSW, Canadian Music Week, and Folk Alliance are where team members scout talent. Be visible, be professional, be ready.

### Do Your Research
Before reaching out, know their roster. Do they work with artists at your level? Do they understand your genre? A great hip-hop manager might be wrong for a folk artist.

### The Meeting
When you get a meeting, come prepared:
- Your story (concise, compelling)
- Your numbers (streaming, social, ticket sales)
- Your goals (what you want to accomplish)
- Your questions (interview them too)

## Commission Structures: What to Expect

| Role | Standard Commission | Notes |
|------|---------------------|-------|
| Manager | 15-20% | Sometimes 10% early with escalators |
| Agent | 10% | Industry standard, rarely negotiable |
| Attorney | 5% of deal or hourly | Percentage only for major deals |
| Business Manager | 3-5% or monthly retainer | Retainer more common at lower incomes |
| Publicist | $2-5K/month | Project-based also common |

**The stacking problem:** At 15% manager + 10% agent + 5% attorney, you're giving up 30% before taxes. This is normal, but budget for it.

---

## Firing Team Members

Sometimes relationships don't work out. Here's how to handle it:

**Check your contract:** Most agreements have termination clauses (often 30-90 days notice). Follow them exactly.

**Document everything:** Keep records of communications, especially around disputes.

**Don't burn bridges unnecessarily:** The industry is small. Professional transitions preserve relationships even when ending business arrangements.

**The sunset clause:** Some contracts continue paying commission on deals secured during the term for 6-12 months after termination. This is standard; negotiate the duration.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Manager first, then agent, then lawyer** as you grow
- Hire when the cost of not having them exceeds their commission
- Referrals beat cold outreach
- Interview them as much as they interview you
- Contracts protect both parties—read them carefully
- Your team amplifies you; they don't replace your hustle

---

*Remember: The best team members believe in your music as much as you do. Don't settle for people who see you as just another client.*
