# Networking & Relationships

The music industry runs on relationships. Not in a sleazy "who you know" way—in a human "people work with people they trust" way. Networking isn't about collecting contacts; it's about building genuine connections that create opportunities over time.

---

## Reframing Networking

### It's Not Transactional
The worst networking mistake is treating people like stepping stones. "What can this person do for me?" is the wrong question. "How can we help each other?" is closer. "Do I genuinely like and respect this person?" is best.

### It's Long-Term
Relationships that matter take years to develop. The person you meet today might not help your career for five years—or ever. That's fine. The goal is building a network of people you actually want to know.

### It's Mutual
Good networking creates value for everyone involved. You have something to offer, even when you're starting out. Enthusiasm, support, connections, skills—everyone has currency.

---

## Where to Meet People

### Live Shows
The most natural networking happens at shows. Other artists, promoters, journalists, and industry folks all show up to see music.

**How to work it:**
- Go to shows regularly, not just when you're playing
- Actually watch the openers (they notice who's in the crowd)
- Stick around after your set to meet people
- Introduce yourself to the sound engineer, venue staff, other bands

**The opener strategy:** If you can't get on a bill as a headliner, open for everyone. You'll meet the headliners, their teams, and their fans.

---

### Industry Conferences & Showcases

**SXSW (Austin, TX)**
The biggest industry event in North America. Thousands of artists, hundreds of showcases, endless networking opportunities. Overwhelming but essential.

**Canadian Music Week (Toronto)**
Canada's premier industry event. Great for meeting Canadian talent buyers, media, and labels. More manageable than SXSW.

**Folk Alliance International**
If you're in folk/Americana, this is the room where it happens. Heavy on community, light on industry BS.

**APAP (Association of Performing Arts Professionals)**
For artists interested in theater, performing arts centers, and non-traditional venues.

**Regional conferences:**
- NAMM (gear and technology)
- ASCAP Experience (songwriting focus)
- SF MusicTech Summit

**Conference strategy:**
- Don't try to meet everyone
- Have a goal (meet 3 specific people, learn about one topic)
- Follow up within 48 hours
- The real networking happens at unofficial events, showcases, and late-night hangs

### Online Communities

**Discord servers:** Genre-specific and industry-focused communities where real relationships form.

**Twitter/X:** Still surprisingly active for music industry conversations. Follow artists, managers, journalists you admire. Engage genuinely.

**LinkedIn:** Underrated for music business connections. More professional, less chaotic than other platforms.

**Reddit:** r/WeAreTheMusicMakers, r/MusicIndustry, genre-specific subreddits.

**Online course communities:** If you take a music business course, the community is often as valuable as the content.

---

### Local Scenes

Your local music community is your foundation. Support other artists. Go to their shows. Share their music. Show up for scene events.

**Why local matters:**
- These are the people you'll grow with
- Local relationships are easier to maintain
- Your hometown success creates momentum
- Local promoters become regional become national

## How to Build Genuine Relationships

### Be Interested, Not Interesting
Ask questions. Listen more than you talk. People remember how you made them feel, not what you said about yourself.

### Follow Up
The magic happens in follow-up. Send a quick message after meeting someone: "Great talking about [specific thing]. Let's stay in touch."

**The 48-hour rule:** Follow up within 48 hours while the conversation is fresh. A simple email or DM works.

### Give Before You Ask
Share someone's new single. Introduce two people who should know each other. Offer to help load out gear. Generosity creates goodwill.

### Be Consistent
Show up repeatedly. The person who attends every local show becomes part of the fabric. The person who shows up once and hands out business cards is forgotten.

### Remember Details
Use a simple CRM (even a spreadsheet) to track:
- Where you met someone
- What you talked about
- Their current projects
- When you last connected

Reference these details in future conversations. "How did that tour go?" lands better than generic small talk.

## Networking for Introverts

Not everyone loves schmoozing. That's fine.

**Quality over quantity:** Three deep connections beat fifty business cards.

**One-on-one coffee:** Skip the networking events. Invite specific people for coffee. Deeper conversations, less exhaustion.

**Online first:** Build relationships in Discord, Twitter DMs, or email before meeting in person. Online rapport makes in-person easier.

**The buddy system:** Bring a friend to events. Having someone to stand with reduces anxiety.

**It's okay to leave:** You don't have to stay until the end. Make your connections, then go home and recover.

---

## What NOT to Do

### Don't Pitch Immediately
Nothing kills a potential relationship faster than an immediate sales pitch. Get to know someone first. Pitching comes after trust exists.

### Don't Be a Name-Dropper
Mentioning every famous person you've met doesn't impress anyone. It signals insecurity.

### Don't Ghost
If someone helps you—introduction, advice, a favor—acknowledge it. A simple thank-you maintains the relationship.

### Don't Be Flaky
Say what you'll do, then do it. Reliability is rare and valuable. Canceling last minute or not following through destroys trust.

### Don't Treat People Differently Based on Status
The intern today is the A&R tomorrow. The local blogger is the Rolling Stone writer in five years. Treat everyone with respect.

## Maintaining Relationships

### The Check-In
Every few months, reach out to people in your network without asking for anything:
- "Saw your tour announcement—congrats!"
- "Thought of you when I heard this band"
- "How's the new project going?"

### Celebrate Wins
When someone in your network succeeds, celebrate publicly. Share their news, congratulate them, be genuinely happy for them.

### The Ask (When It Comes)
When you do need something, be specific and respectful:
- "Would you be open to introducing me to [person]? I'd love to [specific reason]."
- "I'm looking for advice on [specific topic]. Do you have 15 minutes for a call?"
- Make it easy to say yes or no

---

## Building Your Inner Circle

Not all relationships are equal. Over time, you'll develop:

**Peers:** Artists at your level, growing together. These become lifelong collaborators and friends.

**Mentors:** People ahead of you who offer guidance. Respect their time. Apply their advice. Report back on results.

**Mentees:** People coming up behind you. Teaching reinforces your knowledge and builds the next generation.

**Champions:** People who actively advocate for you. These are gold. Nurture these relationships carefully.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Networking = genuine relationships, not transactions**
- Show up consistently in your local scene
- Follow up within 48 hours of meeting someone
- Give before you ask
- Be interested, not interesting
- Quality connections beat quantity
- The industry is small—reputation matters
- Relationships take years to build; start now

---

*Your network is your net worth isn't just a cliché—it's true. But the value comes from genuine connection, not contact collection.*
