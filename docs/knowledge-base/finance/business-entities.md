# Business Entities for Musicians

Choosing the right business structure affects your taxes, liability, and administrative burden. For independent musicians, the choice typically comes down to sole proprietorship, LLC, or S-Corporation.

---

## Quick Comparison

| Structure | Liability Protection | Tax Complexity | Setup Cost | Best For |
|-----------|---------------------|----------------|------------|----------|
| **Sole Proprietorship** | None | Low | Free | Testing the waters, very low income |
| **Single-Member LLC** | Yes | Low-Medium | $50-$500 | Most independent musicians |
| **Multi-Member LLC** | Yes | Medium | $50-$500 | Bands, collaborative projects |
| **S-Corporation** | Yes | High | $500-$2,000+ | $60K+ annual profit |

---

## Sole Proprietorship

### What It Is
The default when you start earning money from music without filing paperwork. You and your business are legally the same entity.

### Pros
- **Zero setup cost** — no filing fees or paperwork
- **Simple taxes** — report income on Schedule C of your personal return
- **Full control** — no partners, boards, or formal requirements

### Cons
- **No liability protection** — personal assets at risk if sued
- **Harder to build business credit**
- **Less professional credibility** with venues, labels, licensors

### When to Use
- Just starting out with minimal income
- Side hustle status (under $10K/year)
- Testing whether music income will grow

### When to Graduate
Once you have:
- Regular paid gigs ($15K+ annually)
- Released music available commercially
- Contracts with venues, publishers, or licensors
- Any significant assets to protect

## Limited Liability Company (LLC)

### What It Is
A legal entity separate from you personally. Creates a liability shield between your business activities and personal assets.

### Single-Member LLC

**Best for:** Solo artists, producers, songwriters

**How it works:**
- You own 100% of the LLC
- IRS treats it as a "disregarded entity" — you still report income on Schedule C
- No separate business tax return needed

**Key benefits:**
- Personal asset protection if someone sues over your music
- Professional appearance on contracts
- Ability to open business bank accounts and build credit
- Can convert to S-Corp later without changing legal structure

### Multi-Member LLC

**Best for:** Bands, production duos, collaborative projects

**How it works:**
- Two or more owners ("members")
- IRS taxes as partnership by default (Form 1065 + K-1s)
- Requires Operating Agreement defining ownership, profit splits, decision-making

**Critical for bands:**
Your Operating Agreement should address:
- Song ownership percentages
- Revenue split (live vs. recorded vs. publishing)
- What happens when a member leaves
- Voting on major decisions
- Adding new members
- Dissolution procedures

**Without an Operating Agreement**, state default rules apply — often splitting everything equally regardless of contribution.

### LLC Formation Steps (US)

1. **Choose a name** — must be unique in your state, usually requires "LLC" or "Limited Liability Company"
2. **File Articles of Organization** — with your state's Secretary of State ($50-$500)
3. **Get an EIN** — free from IRS.gov (required for multi-member, recommended for single-member)
4. **Create Operating Agreement** — especially critical for multi-member LLCs
5. **Open business bank account** — use EIN and Articles of Organization
6. **Register for state taxes** — sales tax, unemployment, etc. if applicable

### LLC Costs (Annual)

| State | Formation Fee | Annual Fee | Notes |
|-------|--------------|------------|-------|
| California | $70 | $800 minimum franchise tax | Expensive but big music market |
| New York | $200 | $25 every 2 years | Publication requirement adds $500-1500 |
| Texas | $300 | $0 | No annual fee |
| Tennessee | $300 | $300 minimum | Franchise tax based on revenue |
| Delaware | $90 | $300 | Popular for flexibility |
| Wyoming | $100 | $60 | Privacy-friendly, low cost |

**Foreign qualification:** If you form in one state but operate in another, you may need to "foreign qualify" — essentially registering your out-of-state LLC locally.

### LLC Taxation Options

By default:
- **Single-member:** Disregarded entity (Schedule C)
- **Multi-member:** Partnership (Form 1065)

**Electing S-Corp taxation:** File Form 2553 with the IRS. Same legal structure, different tax treatment.

## S-Corporation

### What It Is
A tax election, not a legal structure. You remain an LLC legally but file taxes as an S-Corp. Requires taking a "reasonable salary" and can reduce self-employment tax.

### How S-Corp Saves Money

**The problem with LLCs:** All profit is subject to self-employment tax (15.3%) plus income tax.

**The S-Corp solution:** Split income into:
- **Salary** — subject to payroll taxes (employer + employee share = 15.3%)
- **Distributions** — NOT subject to self-employment tax, only income tax

**Example:** $80,000 profit

| Structure | Self-Employment/Payroll Tax | Savings |
|-----------|---------------------------|---------|
| LLC | $80,000 × 15.3% = **$12,240** | — |
| S-Corp | $45,000 salary × 15.3% = **$6,885** | **$5,355** |

*(Assumes $45K is "reasonable salary" for your role)*

### S-Corp Requirements

1. **Reasonable salary** — must pay yourself what someone in your position would earn
   - Research comparable salaries (Bureau of Labor Statistics, Glassdoor)
   - Document your reasoning
   - IRS scrutinizes unreasonably low salaries

2. **Payroll setup** — must run payroll at least quarterly
   - Use Gusto, ADP, or similar ($40-150/month)
   - Withhold income tax, Social Security, Medicare
   - File quarterly 941s and annual W-2s

3. **Separate tax return** — Form 1120-S (or pay accountant $800-2,000)

4. **Formalities** — shareholder meetings, corporate minutes (even solo)

### When S-Corp Makes Sense

**Generally worth it when:**
- Consistent profit of $60,000+ after reasonable salary
- You can pay yourself a salary that leaves meaningful distribution savings
- The tax savings exceed added compliance costs (~$1,500-3,000/year)

**Break-even analysis:**

| Annual Profit | Est. S-Corp Savings | Compliance Costs | Net Benefit |
|--------------|---------------------|------------------|-------------|
| $40,000 | $1,500 | $2,000 | **-$500** ❌ |
| $60,000 | $3,000 | $2,000 | **+$1,000** ✓ |
| $80,000 | $5,000 | $2,500 | **+$2,500** ✓ |
| $100,000 | $7,500 | $3,000 | **+$4,500** ✓ |

### When to Elect S-Corp Status

- **New LLC:** File Form 2553 within 2 months 15 days of formation
- **Existing LLC:** File by March 15 to be effective for current tax year
- **Missed deadline:** File late and hope for relief, or wait until next year

## Canadian Business Structures

### Sole Proprietorship
- Register with provincial/territorial government
- No liability protection
- Report income on personal T1 return

### Corporation
- Federal or provincial incorporation
- Liability protection
- More complex tax filing (T2 return)
- Can income split with family members (subject to tax on split income rules)

**Small Business Deduction:** Canadian Controlled Private Corporations (CCPCs) get reduced federal tax rate (~9%) on first $500K of active business income.

### GST/HST Registration
- Required once revenue exceeds $30,000 in any 12-month period
- Voluntary registration allowed below threshold
- Must charge and remit GST/HST on Canadian sales

## Decision Framework

### Start Here
```
Are you earning money from music?
├── No → Wait, no rush
└── Yes → How much annually?
    ├── Under $15K → Sole prop (for now)
    ├── $15K-$60K → Single-member LLC
    └── $60K+ → LLC, evaluate S-Corp election
```

### Red Flags That Push You Toward LLC/S-Corp

- You have significant personal assets (home, savings, investments)
- You sign contracts with venues, publishers, or licensors
- You hire employees or contractors regularly
- You sell merchandise or physical products
- You tour regularly (higher liability exposure)

### Questions to Ask

1. **What's my risk exposure?** — Touring, physical products, employees = higher liability
2. **What's my income trajectory?** — Fast growth may justify S-Corp sooner
3. **How much complexity can I handle?** — S-Corp requires ongoing attention
4. **Do I have a band/collaborators?** — Multi-member LLC needs Operating Agreement
5. **What's my state cost structure?** — California's $800 annual fee changes the math

## Common Mistakes

### ❌ "I'll stay a sole prop to save money"
One lawsuit can wipe out years of savings. LLC formation costs less than one decent guitar.

### ❌ "I formed an LLC, I'm protected"
You must maintain separation: business bank account, no commingling funds, proper contracts in LLC name. Courts can "pierce the corporate veil."

### ❌ "S-Corp saves taxes automatically"
Compliance costs eat small savings. Do the math. Also, you MUST take a reasonable salary.

### ❌ "I don't need an Operating Agreement"
Especially dangerous for bands. Disputes happen. Document everything while relationships are good.

### ❌ "I can form in any state"
If you live and work in California, forming a Wyoming LLC doesn't avoid California taxes. You'll likely need to foreign qualify anyway.

## Action Steps

### If You're Starting Out
1. Track all income and expenses (even as sole prop)
2. Set aside 25-30% for taxes
3. Form LLC once income exceeds $10-15K or you sign your first major contract

### If You're Ready for LLC
1. Check name availability in your state
2. File Articles of Organization
3. Get EIN from IRS
4. Draft Operating Agreement (use lawyer or quality template)
5. Open business bank account
6. Update contracts to use LLC name

### If You're Evaluating S-Corp
1. Calculate potential savings (profit minus reasonable salary × 15.3%)
2. Get quotes for payroll service and accountant
3. Compare net savings to compliance costs
4. Make election if savings exceed $1,500+ annually
