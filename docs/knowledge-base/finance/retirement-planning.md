# Retirement Planning for Musicians

Musicians are often so focused on surviving today that retirement feels like a distant luxury. But the earlier you start, the less you need to save — and the more options you'll have when you're ready to slow down.

---

## The Retirement Challenge for Musicians

### Irregular Income

Unlike salaried employees, musicians face:
- Feast-or-famine cash flow
- No employer-sponsored 401(k)
- No employer matching contributions
- Gaps between projects or tours

### No Safety Net

Traditional employees get:
- Employer 401(k) with matching
- Pension plans (rare now)
- Automatic payroll deductions
- HR departments to explain benefits

Musicians must build all of this themselves.

### The Good News

Self-employed retirement plans often have:
- **Higher contribution limits** than employee 401(k)s
- **More investment choices**
- **Tax advantages** that can reduce current-year taxes
- **Flexibility** to contribute in good years, skip in lean years

## Retirement Accounts for Self-Employed Musicians

### Traditional IRA

**Best for:** Beginners, low income, tax deduction needs

**Basics:**
- Contribute up to $7,000/year (2024; $8,000 if age 50+)
- Tax-deductible contributions (income limits apply if covered by workplace plan)
- Growth is tax-deferred
- Pay taxes on withdrawals in retirement

**Pros:**
- Simple to open (any brokerage)
- Low minimums ($0-$1,000)
- Wide investment options

**Cons:**
- Low contribution limit
- Income limits for deduction
- Required minimum distributions (RMDs) at 73

**Where to open:** Vanguard, Fidelity, Schwab, Betterment

### Roth IRA

**Best for:** Younger musicians, lower current tax bracket, tax-free growth

**Basics:**
- Contribute up to $7,000/year (2024; $8,000 if age 50+)
- No tax deduction on contributions
- Growth and withdrawals in retirement are **tax-free**
- Can withdraw contributions (not earnings) anytime without penalty

**Pros:**
- Tax-free retirement income
- No RMDs during your lifetime
- Flexibility to access contributions

**Cons:**
- Income limits: phases out at $153K single / $228K married (2024)
- No immediate tax break
- Lower contribution limit

**Musician advantage:** If you have low-income years (common early career), Roth contributions lock in low tax rates.

### SEP-IRA (Simplified Employee Pension)

**Best for:** Solo musicians with variable income, no employees

**Basics:**
- Contribute up to 25% of net self-employment income
- Maximum: $69,000 (2024)
- Employer-only contributions (you are the employer)
- Tax-deductible contributions

**Calculation:**
```
Net self-employment income = Business profit - 1/2 self-employment tax
Maximum SEP contribution = 20% of net SE income (effectively)
```

**Example:**
- Business profit: $50,000
- 1/2 SE tax: ~$3,825
- Net SE income: $46,175
- Maximum SEP contribution: $9,235 (20%)

**Pros:**
- High contribution limits
- Simple to set up and maintain
- No annual filing requirements
- Can skip contributions in lean years

**Cons:**
- Must contribute same percentage for all employees (if you have any)
- No Roth option
- RMDs at 73

**Best for:** Musicians with no employees who want simplicity + high limits.

### Solo 401(k) (Individual 401k)

**Best for:** Musicians wanting maximum contributions, Roth option, loan feature

**Basics:**
- Contribute as both employee and employer
- Employee deferral: $23,000 (2024; $30,500 if age 50+)
- Employer contribution: 25% of compensation (up to $69,000 total)
- Can be Traditional (tax-deferred) or Roth (after-tax)

**Maximum contribution example (under 50):**
- Employee deferral: $23,000
- Employer contribution (20% of $230,000): $46,000
- **Total: $69,000**

**Pros:**
- Highest possible contribution limits
- Roth option available
- Can borrow from account (loan feature)
- Can include spouse as employee

**Cons:**
- More paperwork than SEP
- Must file Form 5500-EZ once assets exceed $250,000
- Must establish by December 31 (but can contribute until tax deadline)

**Where to open:** Vanguard, Fidelity, Schwab, E*Trade, TD Ameritrade

### SIMPLE IRA

**Best for:** Musicians with a few employees, simpler than 401(k)

**Basics:**
- Employee contribution: $16,000 (2024; $19,500 if age 50+)
- Employer match: 3% of compensation or 2% non-elective
- Less paperwork than 401(k)

**Pros:**
- Simpler administration
- Lower costs than 401(k)

**Cons:**
- Lower contribution limits than SEP or Solo 401(k)
- Must contribute for employees
- Early withdrawal penalty: 25% in first 2 years

**Best for:** Small bands or teaching studios with a few employees.

### Account Comparison

| Feature | Traditional IRA | Roth IRA | SEP-IRA | Solo 401(k) |
|---------|-----------------|----------|---------|-------------|
| **2024 Limit** | $7,000 | $7,000 | $69,000 | $69,000 |
| **Catch-up (50+)** | $1,000 | $1,000 | None | $7,500 |
| **Roth Option** | No | Yes (only) | No | Yes |
| **Loan Feature** | No | No | No | Yes |
| **Setup Complexity** | Low | Low | Low | Medium |
| **Best For** | Beginners | Young/lower income | Solo, variable income | Max contributions |

## Choosing the Right Account

### Decision Framework

```
Do you have employees (other than spouse)?
├── Yes → SIMPLE IRA or SEP-IRA
└── No → How much can you contribute?
    ├── Under $7,000 → Roth IRA (if eligible)
    ├── $7,000-$23,000 → Roth IRA + SEP-IRA
    └── Over $23,000 → Solo 401(k)
```

### Scenarios

**Just starting out ($20K income):**
- Priority: Roth IRA ($7,000 max)
- Lock in low tax rate now, tax-free growth forever

**Growing career ($50K income):**
- Roth IRA: $7,000
- SEP-IRA: ~$8,400 (20% of net SE income)
- Total: ~$15,400

**Established musician ($100K+ income):**
- Solo 401(k) for maximum contributions
- Mix of Roth (employee) and Traditional (employer)
- Consider backdoor Roth if income too high for direct contribution

**Variable income (touring musician):**
- SEP-IRA for flexibility (skip lean years, max out good years)
- Or Solo 401(k) with Roth option for tax diversification

## Investment Strategy

### Asset Allocation

**Rule of thumb:** Stock percentage = 110 - your age

| Age | Stocks | Bonds | Alternative |
|-----|--------|-------|-------------|
| 25 | 85% | 10% | 5% |
| 35 | 75% | 20% | 5% |
| 45 | 65% | 30% | 5% |
| 55 | 55% | 40% | 5% |
| 65 | 45% | 50% | 5% |

**Stocks:** Higher growth potential, more volatile
**Bonds:** Stability, income, lower growth
**Alternative:** REITs, commodities, crypto (small allocation)

### Investment Options

**Target-date funds:**
- Automatically adjust allocation as you age
- Simple, hands-off approach
- Examples: Vanguard Target Retirement 2050, Fidelity Freedom 2050

**Index funds:**
- Low cost (0.03-0.20% expense ratio)
- Broad diversification
- Examples: VTI (total stock market), VXUS (international), BND (bonds)

**Managed funds:**
- Higher fees (0.50-1.50%)
- Professional management
- Generally underperform index funds over time

### Sample Portfolio (Ages 25-35)

| Fund | Allocation | Purpose |
|------|------------|---------|
| Total Stock Market (VTI) | 60% | US growth |
| International Stock (VXUS) | 25% | Global diversification |
| Total Bond Market (BND) | 10% | Stability |
| REIT (VNQ) | 5% | Real estate exposure |

**Rebalance annually** — sell winners, buy losers to maintain target allocation.

## How Much to Save

### The 15% Rule

Financial advisors suggest saving **15% of gross income** for retirement.

**Example:** $50,000 income → $7,500/year

### Adjust for Your Situation

| Situation | Suggested Rate | Why |
|-----------|----------------|-----|
| Starting in 20s | 10-15% | Time is on your side |
| Starting in 30s | 15-20% | Catch-up needed |
| Starting in 40s | 20-25% | Aggressive saving required |
| Starting in 50s | Max out accounts + catch-up | Make up for lost time |

### The Power of Starting Early

**Scenario:** Save $500/month until age 65, 7% average return

| Start Age | Total Contributed | Final Balance |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------|
| 25 | $240,000 | $1,197,000 |
| 35 | $180,000 | $567,000 |
| 45 | $120,000 | $245,000 |
| 55 | $60,000 | $88,000 |

**Starting 10 years earlier = 2x the final balance.**

### Variable Income Strategy

**Good years:** Max out contributions (build buffer)
**Lean years:** Contribute minimum (or skip if necessary)
**Average target:** 15% over multi-year periods

**Example 5-year plan:**
- Year 1 (tour): $25,000 contribution
- Year 2 (writing): $5,000 contribution
- Year 3 (album release): $20,000 contribution
- Year 4 (promo): $5,000 contribution
- Year 5 (tour): $25,000 contribution
- **5-year average: $16,000/year (16% of $100K average income)**

## Tax Considerations

### Traditional vs. Roth Decision

**Choose Traditional if:**
- Current tax bracket > expected retirement bracket
- Need current-year tax deduction
- Income too high for Roth IRA

**Choose Roth if:**
- Current tax bracket < expected retirement bracket
- Young (more time for tax-free growth)
- Want tax diversification
- Expect to have other taxable retirement income

**Musician consideration:** Income often starts low and grows. Early career = Roth. Peak earning years = mix of both.

### Tax Brackets (2024 - Single Filers)

| Bracket | Income Range | Rate |
|---------|--------------|------|
| 10% | $0 - $11,600 | 10% |
| 12% | $11,601 - $47,150 | 12% |
| 22% | $47,151 - $100,525 | 22% |
| 24% | $100,526 - $191,950 | 24% |
| 32% | $191,951 - $243,725 | 32% |

**Strategy:** If you're in the 12% bracket, Roth makes sense. In the 32% bracket, Traditional deduction is valuable.

### Self-Employment Tax Deduction

Contributions to Traditional SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) reduce:
- Income tax
- **Not** self-employment tax (15.3% still applies to net earnings)

However, the contribution itself is based on net earnings after the SE tax deduction, creating a circular calculation.

### State Tax Considerations

**No state income tax:** Roth becomes more attractive (no state deduction to lose)

**High state tax (CA, NY):** Traditional deduction more valuable now, but consider moving to no-tax state in retirement

## Social Security for Musicians

### How It Works

Social Security benefits are based on your **35 highest-earning years**.

**Musician challenges:**
- Many low-income or zero-income years
- Cash payments unreported (hurts future benefits)
- International touring (complicated credits)

### Maximizing Benefits

**Report all income:** Even cash gigs. Future you will thank present you.

**Work at least 35 years:** Zero-income years drag down your average.

**Delay claiming:** Benefits increase 8% per year from full retirement age to age 70.

| Claim Age | Benefit (vs. Full) |
|-----------|-------------------|
| 62 | 70% |
| 67 (FRA for many) | 100% |
| 70 | 124% |

### Don't Rely on It

Social Security replaces ~40% of pre-retirement income. Musicians need additional savings.

## Healthcare in Retirement

### Medicare

- Eligible at age 65
- Part A (hospital): Free if you paid Medicare taxes
- Part B (medical): Premium based on income ($174-$594/month in 2024)
- Part D (prescription): Additional premium

### Bridge Coverage (Ages 60-65)

If you retire before Medicare eligibility:
- COBRA (expensive, 18 months max)
- ACA marketplace plans
- Spouse's employer plan
- Health sharing ministries (not insurance)

### Health Savings Account (HSA)

**If you have high-deductible health plan:**
- Triple tax advantage: deductible contributions, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals for medical
- Can invest like retirement account
- After 65, can withdraw for any purpose (taxed like Traditional IRA)

**2024 limits:**
- Individual: $4,150
- Family: $8,300
- Catch-up (55+): $1,000

## Estate Planning Basics

### Beneficiary Designations

**Update these:**
- Retirement accounts (primary and contingent beneficiaries)
- Life insurance
- Investment accounts (TOD/POD)

**Why it matters:** Beneficiary designations override wills. Outdated designations = ex-spouse gets your 401(k).

### Will

**Minimum:** Name guardians for minor children, specify asset distribution.

**Musician-specific:** Who gets your catalog? Your instruments?

### Power of Attorney

- **Financial:** Someone to handle money if you're incapacitated
- **Medical:** Healthcare decisions if you can't make them

### Living Will

Your wishes for end-of-life care.

## Action Steps by Age

### In Your 20s

**Priority: Start the habit**
- [ ] Open Roth IRA (even with small contributions)
- [ ] Set up automatic monthly transfers
- [ ] Invest in target-date fund or simple index portfolio
- [ ] Aim for 10-15% of income

**Don't worry about:** Perfect allocation, maximum contributions. Just start.

### In Your 30s

**Priority: Increase contributions, diversify**
- [ ] Bump up to 15-20% of income
- [ ] Consider adding SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k)
- [ ] Review and rebalance portfolio annually
- [ ] Update beneficiaries after life changes

**Watch for:** Lifestyle creep eating savings capacity.

### In Your 40s

**Priority: Catch up if behind**
- [ ] Max out available accounts if possible
- [ ] Consider working with fee-only financial planner
- [ ] Evaluate Traditional vs. Roth based on current bracket
- [ ] Start thinking about retirement timeline

**Reality check:** Use retirement calculators. Are you on track?

### In Your 50s

**Priority: Maximize, protect, plan**
- [ ] Use catch-up contributions ($7,500 extra for 401k, $1,000 for IRA)
- [ ] Shift to more conservative allocation
- [ ] Plan for healthcare bridge years
- [ ] Consider long-term care insurance
- [ ] Meet with estate planning attorney

### In Your 60s

**Priority: Transition planning**
- [ ] Decide when to claim Social Security
- [ ] Plan withdrawal strategy (which accounts, when)
- [ ] Evaluate Medicare options
- [ ] Consider part-time music income in "retirement"
- [ ] Finalize estate plan

## Common Mistakes

### ❌ Not starting because you can't max out
$100/month is infinitely better than $0. Start small, increase over time.

### ❌ Keeping everything in cash
Inflation erodes purchasing power. Invest for growth, especially when young.

### ❌ Chasing hot investments
Stick to diversified index funds. Don't try to pick stocks or time the market.

### ❌ Ignoring fees
1% annual fee vs. 0.1% fee = hundreds of thousands difference over 30 years.

### ❌ Forgetting to update beneficiaries
Divorce, marriage, children — update your designations.

### ❌ Taking early withdrawals
10% penalty + taxes + lost growth. Find another way.
