# Accounting & Taxes for Musicians

Music income comes from everywhere — streaming, gigs, merch, licensing, teaching. Without a system, tax season becomes a nightmare and you miss deductions that could save thousands.

---

## Types of Music Income

Understanding your income streams helps with tracking, forecasting, and tax planning.

### Performance Income
| Source | Tax Treatment | Notes |
|--------|--------------|-------|
| Live gigs | 1099-NEC or cash | Venues pay $600+ on 1099 |
| Session work | 1099-NEC | Studio musicians, hired guns |
| Touring | 1099-NEC or self-reported | Per diems may be taxable |
| Wedding/corporate | Usually cash/venmo | Self-report all income |

### Royalty Income
| Source | Tax Treatment | Notes |
|--------|--------------|-------|
| Streaming (Spotify, Apple) | 1099-MISC or 1042-S | Foreign withholding possible |
| Publishing (ASCAP/BMI/SESAC) | 1099-MISC | Performance royalties |
| Mechanical royalties (MLC) | 1099-MISC | Songwriter/publisher share |
| Sync licensing | 1099-NEC | One-time fees or royalties |
| Neighboring rights | Varies | Foreign collections |

### Product & Service Income
| Source | Tax Treatment | Notes |
|--------|--------------|-------|
| Merchandise | Self-reported | Track COGS (cost of goods sold) |
| Physical music sales | Self-reported | CDs, vinyl at shows |
| Teaching lessons | 1099-NEC or cash | Private or through school/studio |
| Production/engineering | 1099-NEC | Freelance studio work |
| Beat sales | 1099-NEC or platform | BeatStars, Airbit, etc. |

### Platform Income
| Platform | Tax Form | Threshold |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Bandcamp | 1099-K | $600+ (post-2024 rules) |
| Patreon | 1099-K | $600+ |
| YouTube | 1099-MISC | $600+ |
| DistroKid/CD Baby | 1099-MISC | $600+ |
| PayPal/Venmo (business) | 1099-K | $600+ |

**Important:** Even without a 1099, you must report all income. The IRS receives 1099s — they'll know if you don't report.

## Accounting Basics

### Cash vs. Accrual Accounting

**Cash basis** (recommended for most musicians):
- Income counted when received
- Expenses counted when paid
- Simpler, matches how you actually manage money

**Accrual basis:**
- Income counted when earned (even if not paid)
- Expenses counted when incurred
- Required for corporations with inventory or $25M+ revenue

**Minimum viable system:**
- Business bank account (separate from personal)
- Business credit card
- Monthly reconciliation (compare statements to records)
- Receipt capture (phone photos, Expensify, or shoebox)

### Monthly Accounting Routine

**Weekly:**
- [ ] Log all income (gig payments, royalties, sales)
- [ ] Capture receipts (photo or scan)
- [ ] Categorize transactions in software

**Monthly:**
- [ ] Reconcile bank and credit card statements
- [ ] Review profit & loss statement
- [ ] Set aside estimated taxes (25-30% of profit)
- [ ] Pay quarterly estimated taxes (if due)

**Quarterly:**
- [ ] Review year-to-date numbers
- [ ] Adjust estimated tax payments
- [ ] File Form 1040-ES (US) or equivalent

**Annually:**
- [ ] Gather all 1099s and tax documents
- [ ] Review full-year P&L
- [ ] File tax return (or send to accountant)
- [ ] Plan next year's estimated payments

## Tax Write-Offs for Musicians

### The Golden Rule
Expenses must be **ordinary** (common in your industry) and **necessary** (helpful and appropriate) for your music business.

### Equipment & Instruments

**Fully deductible (in year purchased):**
- Instruments under $2,500 (de minimis safe harbor)
- Accessories: strings, reeds, drum sticks, cables
- Cases, stands, straps, capos
- Software: DAWs, plugins, sample libraries

**Depreciated over multiple years:**
- Instruments over $2,500
- Major studio equipment
- Computers and recording gear
- Section 179 allows immediate deduction up to limits

**Repairs & maintenance:**
- Instrument repairs (deductible when incurred)
- Setup and maintenance
- Rehairing bows, fret work, etc.

### Studio & Recording

| Expense | Deductible? | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| Home studio (portion) | Yes | See Home Office section |
| External studio rental | Yes | Hourly or block bookings |
| Mixing/mastering | Yes | Per-song or album rates |
| Session musician fees | Yes | 1099 required if $600+ |
| Producer fees | Yes | Including points/royalties advance |

### Travel & Meals

**Transportation:**
- Mileage: $0.67/mile (2024 rate) or actual expenses
- Flights, trains, rental cars
- Parking and tolls
- Baggage fees for equipment

**Meals:**
- **50% deductible** while traveling for business
- **50% deductible** for business meals with others
- Must document: who, where, business purpose

**Lodging:**
- 100% deductible when traveling for business
- Must be overnight and away from tax home

**Tracking mileage:**
- MileIQ, Everlance, or manual log
- Record: date, destination, purpose, starting/ending odometer

### Home Studio / Home Office

**Simplified method:**
- $5 per square foot, up to 300 sq ft
- Maximum $1,500 deduction
- Easy, no depreciation recapture

**Regular method:**
- Calculate percentage of home used exclusively for business
- Deduct that percentage of:
  - Rent or mortgage interest
  - Property taxes
  - Utilities (electric, gas, water, internet)
  - Homeowners/renters insurance
  - Repairs and maintenance

**Example:**
- Home: 1,000 sq ft
- Studio: 150 sq ft (15%)
- Annual rent: $18,000
- Deductible portion: $18,000 × 15% = **$2,700**

**Requirements:**
- Exclusive use (no personal use)
- Regular use for business
- Principal place of business OR place to meet clients

## Separating Personal & Business Finances

### Why It Matters

**Legal protection:** Commingling funds can pierce your LLC's liability shield.

**Tax accuracy:** Clean separation makes deductions defensible and audits easier.

**Business clarity:** You can't manage what you can't measure.

### The Setup

**1. Business Bank Account**
- Use EIN to open (not SSN)
- All business income goes here
- All business expenses paid from here
- Transfer "owner's draw" to personal account for spending

**2. Business Credit Card**
- Builds business credit
- Clean separation of expenses
- Rewards on business spending
- Easier receipt tracking

**3. Pay Yourself System**

**Sole prop/Single-member LLC:**
- Transfer from business to personal = owner's draw
- Not a deductible expense
- No payroll taxes on draws

**S-Corp:**
- Must run payroll for reasonable salary
- Salary subject to payroll taxes
- Additional distributions not subject to payroll taxes

### Commingling Red Flags

❌ Paying personal rent from business account  
❌ Depositing gig checks to personal account  
❌ Using business card for groceries  
❌ Cash withdrawals with no documentation  
❌ "Loaning" money back and forth constantly  

### Gray Areas

**Music subscriptions:**
- Spotify/Apple Music for business research = deductible
- Personal listening = not deductible
- Solution: Use business percentage or separate accounts

**Vehicle expenses:**
- Track business vs. personal mileage
- Deduct only business portion

**Home internet:**
- Estimate business percentage
- Document your methodology

## Estimated Taxes (US)

### Who Must Pay

Self-employed musicians must pay quarterly estimated taxes if they expect to owe $1,000+ in tax for the year.

### 2024 Due Dates

| Quarter | Period | Due Date |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Q1 | Jan 1 – Mar 31 | April 15, 2024 |
| Q2 | Apr 1 – May 31 | June 17, 2024 |
| Q3 | Jun 1 – Aug 31 | September 16, 2024 |
| Q4 | Sep 1 – Dec 31 | January 15, 2025 |

*(Dates shift for weekends/holidays)*

### How Much to Pay

**Safe harbor rules** (avoid underpayment penalty):
1. Pay 100% of last year's tax liability (110% if AGI > $150K), OR
2. Pay 90% of current year's tax liability

**Simple method:**
- Calculate last year's total tax (Form 1040, line 24)
- Divide by 4
- Pay that amount each quarter

**Better method:**
- Estimate current year income each quarter
- Calculate actual tax owed
- Adjust payments up or down

### Self-Employment Tax

15.3% on net self-employment income:
- 12.4% Social Security (up to wage base: $168,600 for 2024)
- 2.9% Medicare (no cap)
- 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax (income over $200K single / $250K married)

**Deductible:** 50% of self-employment tax on your return.

### Payment Methods

- **EFTPS.gov:** Direct pay from bank account (free)
- **IRS Direct Pay:** Bank account or card (free for bank, fee for card)
- **1040-ES vouchers:** Mail with check

## Canadian Tax Considerations

### Self-Employment Income

Report on Form T2125 (Statement of Business or Professional Activities).

### GST/HST

- Register when revenue exceeds $30,000 in any 12-month period
- Charge GST/HST on Canadian sales
- Claim Input Tax Credits (ITCs) on business purchases
- File returns monthly, quarterly, or annually

### Quebec Considerations

- QST (Quebec Sales Tax) registration and filing
- Separate provincial tax obligations

### Tax Rates (2024)

Federal + Provincial combined rates vary:
- Ontario: ~20-53%
- British Columbia: ~20-54%
- Quebec: ~15-55%

### CPP Contributions

Self-employed individuals pay both employer and employee portions (11.9% on pensionable earnings for 2024).

## Record Keeping

### What to Keep

| Document | How Long |
|----------|----------|
| Tax returns | 7 years |
| 1099s and W-2s | 7 years |
| Receipts for deductions | 7 years |
| Bank statements | 7 years |
| Contracts and agreements | Duration + 7 years |
| Asset purchase records | As long as you own + 7 years |

### Digital vs. Physical

**Digital is fine:**
- Scan or photograph receipts
- Cloud storage with backup
- Organize by year and category

**Keep physical for:**
- Signed contracts
- Asset purchases (instruments, equipment)
- Anything requiring original signature

### Audit Preparation

If audited, you'll need to prove:
1. Income was reported (1099s, bank records)
2. Expenses were business-related (receipts + documentation)
3. Home office calculation (measurements, photos)
4. Mileage logs (if claiming vehicle)

**Pro tip:** The IRS can audit returns from the last 3 years (6 years if substantial understatement). Keep records accordingly.

## Common Tax Mistakes

### ❌ Not reporting cash income
The IRS knows about your 1099s. Unreported income is tax fraud.

### ❌ Missing deductions
Home office, mileage, and small expenses add up. Track everything.

### ❌ Mixing personal and business
Creates audit risk and legal liability issues.

### ❌ Not paying estimated taxes
Results in penalties and a massive April bill.

### ❌ Deducting everything
Personal expenses aren't deductible. Be honest and reasonable.

### ❌ DIYing complex situations
If you have an S-Corp, multiple states, or significant income, hire a CPA.

## When to Hire a Professional

### DIY is fine when:
- Sole proprietorship
- Simple income (mostly 1099s)
- Standard deductions
- Under $75K income

### Hire a CPA when:
- S-Corporation or partnership
- Multi-state income or touring
- $100K+ income
- Complex royalty structures
- Audit risk concerns
- You hate doing taxes

**Cost:** $300-800 for individual returns, $800-2,500 for business returns.
