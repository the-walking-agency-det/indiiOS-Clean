## Introduction: The Deal Landscape

The music industry offers artists a spectrum of deal types, from major label contracts that provide massive resources but demand significant control, to DIY distribution that offers freedom but requires self-sufficiency. Understanding these options is critical—your choice will shape your career trajectory, creative control, and financial outcomes for years.

### The Three Main Categories

1. **Major Label Deals** — Full-service partnerships with the "Big Three" (Universal, Sony, Warner) or their subsidiaries
2. **Independent Label Deals** — Partnerships with indie labels offering varying levels of support and control
3. **Distribution Deals** — Digital distributors that get your music on streaming platforms while you retain ownership

### Key Principle: Everything Is Negotiable (Sort Of)

No deal is set in stone, but your leverage determines what you can change. A developing artist with no track record has minimal negotiating power. An artist with proven streaming numbers, a growing fanbase, or viral momentum has significantly more.

## Major Label Deals

### The "Big Three"

- **Universal Music Group (UMG)** — Largest market share (~30%)
- **Sony Music Entertainment** — ~22% market share
- **Warner Music Group** — ~16% market share

These companies own dozens of subsidiary labels (Interscope, Def Jam, Atlantic, RCA, Columbia, etc.), so you might sign with a "label" that's actually part of a major.

### Traditional Recording Contract (The "Old Model")

**What It Is:** The classic deal where the label funds recording, manufacturing, distribution, and marketing in exchange for owning the master recordings.

**Typical Terms:**
- **Term:** Usually 1 initial album with options for 4-6 additional albums
- **Advance:** $150K–$500K+ for new artists; $1M+ for established acts
- **Royalty Rate:** 12–16% of suggested retail list price (SRLP) for new artists
- **Ownership:** Label owns masters in perpetuity (or for a very long term)
- **Creative Control:** Minimal for new artists; increases with success

**How It Works:**
1. Label gives you an advance to record an album
2. You deliver the completed masters
3. Label manufactures, distributes, and markets
4. You earn royalties only after the advance is recouped
5. Label keeps 85–88% of revenue; you get 12–16%

**Real Example:** A new artist gets a $250,000 advance. Their album generates $1M in gross revenue. At a 14% royalty rate, they earn $140,000 in royalties—but they owe the label $250,000. They won't see additional royalties until the $110,000 deficit is cleared through future releases or catalog sales.

### 360 Deals (The Modern Standard)

**What It Is:** The label receives a percentage of *all* artist income—not just recorded music sales. This includes touring, merchandise, endorsements, publishing, and sometimes even acting income.

**Why Labels Want This:** Record sales declined dramatically after 2000. Labels needed new revenue streams to justify their investments.

**Typical Terms:**
- **360 Participation:** 10–30% of non-recording income
- **Advance:** Often higher than traditional deals ($300K–$1M+)
- **Royalty Rate:** Similar to traditional (12–18%)
- **Term:** Longer, often 5–7 album cycles or time-based (e.g., 7 years)
- **Support:** More comprehensive marketing, tour support, brand partnerships

**What the Label Provides:**
- Recording budget
- Marketing and promotion
- Tour support (sometimes)
- Brand partnership opportunities
- Sync licensing push
- Radio promotion

**The Catch:** You're essentially a business partner with the label across all revenue streams. If you blow up on TikTok and land a Nike deal, the label takes their cut.

**Real Example:** Artist signs 360 deal with 20% participation in non-recording income. They earn:
- $100K from album sales → Label keeps ~$85K, artist gets ~$15K
- $200K from touring → Label takes $40K (20%), artist keeps $160K
- $50K from merch → Label takes $10K (20%), artist keeps $40K
- $30K from sync licensing → Label takes $6K (20%), artist keeps $24K

**When It Makes Sense:**
- You need significant upfront investment
- You want comprehensive marketing support
- You don't have existing revenue streams to protect
- You're early in your career and need infrastructure

**When to Avoid:**
- You already have strong independent revenue (touring, merch)
- You have a built-in fanbase that doesn't need major label marketing
- You want to retain control over all revenue streams

### Joint Ventures (JVs)

**What It Is:** A partnership where the artist (or their company) and the label create a new entity together, sharing costs, risks, and profits more equally.

**Typical Structure:**
- Artist creates a production company/label imprint
- Major label provides distribution, marketing, and funding
- Profits split 50/50 after costs
- Artist retains more creative control

**Who Gets These:** Established artists with proven track records, or artists with significant leverage (viral success, existing fanbase, celebrity status).

**Real Examples:**
- **Drake's OVO Sound** — Joint venture with Warner Records
- **Top Dawg Entertainment** — JV with Interscope/Aftermath for Kendrick Lamar
- **Dreamville** — J. Cole's imprint with Interscope

**Advantages:**
- Better profit splits (often 50/50)
- More creative control
- Ability to sign and develop other artists
- Retain ownership of masters (sometimes)

**Disadvantages:**
- Still requires major label infrastructure
- Complex accounting and legal structures
- Pressure to deliver commercial results

### Major Label Deal Reality Check

**The Math Problem:**

Most major label deals don't result in profit for the artist. Here's why:

1. **Advances are loans, not gifts.** You must recoup before earning royalties.
2. **Recording costs are recoupable.** That $100K studio budget? You pay it back from your royalties.
3. **Marketing costs may be recoupable.** Video budgets, radio promotion, tour support—often charged against your account.
4. **Cross-collateralization.** If Album 1 flops and Album 2 hits, the losses from Album 1 may be recouped from Album 2's success.

**Success Rate:** Industry estimates suggest 85–90% of major label releases never recoup their advances. The label makes money on the hits that subsidize the rest.

## Independent Label Deals

### What Is an Independent Label?

Independent ("indie") labels are not owned by the major label system. They range from small bedroom operations to well-funded entities like Sub Pop, Merge, XL Recordings, and Domino.

### Types of Indie Deals

#### 1. Standard Indie Deal (Profit Split)

**Structure:**
- Label provides recording budget, marketing, and distribution
- Profits split after costs (often 50/50)
- Label may or may not own masters
- Shorter terms (1–3 albums)

**Typical Terms:**
- **Advance:** $10K–$100K (varies wildly by label size)
- **Profit Split:** 40/60 to 60/40 (artist/label)
- **Term:** 1–3 albums with options
- **Master Ownership:** Varies; increasingly artists retain ownership

**Advantages:**
- More artist-friendly terms
- Often better creative control
- Shorter commitments
- More personal attention

**Disadvantages:**
- Smaller marketing budgets
- Less radio/clout influence
- Limited resources for big campaigns

#### 2. Distribution-Plus Deal

Some indie labels offer more than just distribution—they provide marketing, sync licensing, and some funding while you retain more ownership.

**Structure:**
- You own masters
- Label provides distribution, marketing, some funding
- Revenue split (often 70/30 or 80/20 in artist's favor)
- Label takes percentage for services rendered

#### 3. Licensing Deal

**What It Is:** You retain ownership of masters but license them to the label for a specific term and territory.

**Typical Terms:**
- **License Duration:** 10–15 years (or shorter)
- **Territory:** May be limited (US only, North America, etc.)
- **Reversion:** Masters return to you after term expires
- **Royalty:** Higher rates (18–25%) since label doesn't own masters

**Advantage:** You get your masters back eventually.

### Notable Independent Labels

| Label | Known For | Typical Deal Type |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Sub Pop** | Nirvana, Fleet Foxes, The Shins | Traditional indie |
| **Merge** | Arcade Fire, Neutral Milk Hotel | Artist-friendly, profit splits |
| **XL Recordings** | Adele, Vampire Weekend, Radiohead | Selective, artist-focused |
| **Domino** | Arctic Monkeys, Franz Ferdinand | Creative freedom emphasis |
| **Secretly Canadian** | Bon Iver, The War on Drugs | Long-term artist development |
| **Jagjaguwar** | Bon Iver, Angel Olsen | Boutique, curated roster |
| **Captured Tracks** | Mac DeMarco, DIIV | Garage/indie rock focus |
| **Stones Throw** | J Dilla, Madlib, Anderson .Paak | Hip-hop/electronic focus |
| **Warp** | Aphex Twin, Flying Lotus | Electronic/experimental |
| **Ninja Tune** | Bonobo, Coldcut | Electronic/beat music |

### Indie Label Reality

**The Trade-off:**

Indie labels offer more artist-friendly terms but fewer resources. You're betting on creative freedom and fairer deals over maximum commercial reach.

**Questions to Ask an Indie Label:**

1. Who owns the masters? (Should be you, or revert to you)
2. What is the term? (Shorter is better)
3. What services do you actually provide?
4. What is your marketing budget for my release?
5. Who are your distributor and sync licensing partners?
6. Can I talk to other artists on your roster?
7. What happens if we disagree on creative direction?
8. How do you handle accounting and royalty payments?

## Distribution Deals

Digital distributors get your music onto streaming platforms (Spotify, Apple Music, Amazon, Tidal, etc.) and digital stores (iTunes, Bandcamp). You retain ownership and typically pay either a flat fee or percentage of revenue.

### Major Digital Distributors

#### **DistroKid**

**Model:** Annual flat fee per artist

**Pricing:**
- Musician: $22.99/year (1 artist, basic features)
- Musician Plus: $39.99/year (2 artists, more features)
- Label: $89.99/year (5+ artists, bulk pricing available)

**What You Keep:** 100% of royalties

**Key Features:**
- Unlimited uploads for flat fee
- Fast distribution (1–3 days)
- Spotify verification
- Split pay (automatically divide royalties among collaborators)
- HyperFollow (pre-save links)
- YouTube Content ID

**Pros:**
- Cheapest for prolific artists
- Fastest distribution
- Great for independent/self-releasing artists
- Transparent accounting

**Cons:**
- Annual fee (music comes down if you don't pay)
- No advance or marketing support
- Basic customer support
- No physical distribution

**Best For:** Independent artists releasing frequently who want to keep 100% of royalties.

#### **TuneCore**

**Model:** Annual flat fee per release OR percentage-based (TuneCore Unlimited)

**Pricing:**
- Single: $9.99/year
- Album: $29.99/year
- TuneCore Unlimited: $14.99/year (1 artist) or $49.99/year (5 artists) — unlimited releases

**What You Keep:** 100% of royalties

**Key Features:**
- Social platform monetization (TikTok, Instagram, Facebook)
- YouTube Content ID
- Publishing administration available
- Sync licensing opportunities
- Physical distribution (CDs/vinyl) via partnership

**Pros:**
- Established reputation (founded 2005)
- Publishing admin add-on available
- Good for artists who want occasional releases
- Physical distribution option

**Cons:**
- Per-release fees add up if you release frequently
- Annual fees for catalog maintenance
- Can be more expensive than DistroKid for prolific artists

**Best For:** Artists who release less frequently, want publishing admin, or need physical distribution.

- Standard Single: $9.95 (one-time)

- Slower distribution than competitors

**Best For:** Artists who want a "set it and forget it" solution with no ongoing fees, or those releasing physical products.

### Choosing a Distributor: Decision Framework

**Ask yourself:**

1. **How often do you release?**
   - Frequent (6+ times/year) → DistroKid or Ditto (unlimited)
   - Occasional → CD Baby or TuneCore per-release

2. **Do you need physical distribution?**
   - Yes → CD Baby or TuneCore
   - No → Any digital distributor

3. **Do you want publishing administration?**
   - Yes → CD Baby Pro, TuneCore, or Songtrust separately
   - No → Any distributor

4. **Is upfront cost or long-term cost more important?**
   - Lower upfront → Amuse free tier, UnitedMasters free tier
   - Lower long-term → DistroKid unlimited, CD Baby one-time

5. **Do you want potential label-style support?**
   - Yes → UnitedMasters, Amuse, AWAL, Stem
   - No → Traditional distributors

## Deal Structures & Advances

### How Advances Work

**The Advance Is a Loan**

An advance is not a salary or a gift. It's a prepayment of future royalties that you must "recoup" before earning additional money.

**Recoupment Explained:**

1. Label gives you $100,000 advance
2. You earn royalties at 15% of retail
3. Your album sells 100,000 copies at $10 = $1,000,000 gross
4. Your royalties = $1,000,000 × 15% = $150,000
5. $150,000 royalties − $100,000 advance = $50,000 owed to you (after recoupment)

**But Wait—There's More:**

Most contracts also charge recoupable expenses against your account:
- Recording costs: $50,000–$200,000
- Video production: $50,000–$500,000
- Marketing/promotion: $50,000–$300,000
- Tour support: Variable

**Real Example:**

Artist receives $250,000 advance. Actual costs:
- Advance: $250,000
- Recording: $150,000
- Marketing: $200,000
- Video: $100,000
- **Total Recoupable:** $700,000

Artist needs to generate $4.67M in gross revenue (at 15% royalty) just to break even.

### Cross-Collateralization

**The Danger Clause:**

Many contracts allow labels to cross-collateralize across albums. If Album 1 loses money, those losses can be recouped from Album 2's success.

**Example:**
- Album 1: $300K advance, $200K in sales (unrecouped: $100K)
- Album 2: $300K advance, $800K in sales (unrecouped: −$200K profit)
- With cross-collateralization: Album 1's $100K loss is deducted from Album 2's profit
- Artist receives: $100K instead of $200K

**What to Negotiate:**
- Non-cross-collateralized contracts (each album stands alone)
- Or, limit cross-collateralization to the specific term, not perpetually

### Types of Advances

| Advance Type | Description | Typical Amount |
|--------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Recording Fund** | Budget to make the album | $50K–$500K |
| **Personal Advance** | Money to live on while recording | $50K–$250K |
| **Video Budget** | Production of music videos | $50K–$1M+ |
| **Marketing Commitment** | Promotional spend guarantee | $100K–$1M+ |
| **Tour Support** | Funding for touring | Variable |
| **Option Exercise Payment** | Payment when label picks up next album | Similar to initial advance |

### Key Advance Questions

Before accepting an advance, ask:

1. **What is recoupable?** (Ideally: only the personal advance)
2. **What expenses are charged against my royalty account?**
3. **Is the advance payable in installments?** (Tied to deliverables)
4. **What happens if I'm dropped before recouping?**
5. **Can the label demand repayment of unrecouped advances?** (Usually no, but verify)

### The "Walking Dead" Scenario

Many artists find themselves in label purgatory:
- Their album didn't recoup
- The label won't release them (exercising options)
- The label won't invest in new music (no confidence)
- Artist is contractually bound but professionally stuck

**Prevention:**
- Shorter initial terms
- Firm release commitments (label must release within X months)
- Performance thresholds (if label doesn't spend $Y on marketing, contract terminates)
- Key person clauses (if your A&R leaves, you can terminate)

## Royalty Rates & Accounting

### How Royalties Are Calculated

#### Traditional Physical Sales (CDs, Vinyl)

**Suggested Retail List Price (SRLP) Method:**
- Retail price: $12.98
- Royalty rate: 15%
- Royalty per album: $1.95

**Wholesale Price Method (PPD - Published Price to Dealers):**
- Retail price: $12.98
- Wholesale price: $8.00 (roughly 60% of retail)
- Royalty rate: 15% of wholesale
- Royalty per album: $1.20

**Important:** Know which method your contract uses. 15% of wholesale is significantly less than 15% of retail.

#### Streaming Royalties

Streaming royalties are complex and vary by platform. Here's how they generally flow:

**The Chain:**
1. Streaming platform collects subscriber/ad revenue
2. Platform keeps ~30% (roughly)
3. Remaining ~70% goes to rights holders
4. Rights holders = labels (sound recording) + publishers (composition)
5. Label pays artist per contract terms

**What Artists Actually Get:**

| Platform | Approx. per-stream rate | Artist at 15% royalty |
|----------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Spotify | $0.003–$0.005 | $0.00045–$0.00075 |
| Apple Music | $0.01 | $0.0015 |
| Tidal | $0.012–$0.015 | $0.0018–$0.00225 |
| Amazon Music | $0.004 | $0.0006 |
| YouTube Music | $0.002 | $0.0003 |

**The Math:**

To earn $1,000 in royalties at 15%:
- Spotify: ~2.2 million streams
- Apple Music: ~670,000 streams
- Tidal: ~450,000 streams

**Note:** These are estimates. Actual rates vary by country, subscription tier, label deal, and more.

### Who Gets Paid What

**The Revenue Split (Simplified):**

For every dollar of streaming revenue:
- Streaming platform: ~$0.30
- Label: ~$0.50–$0.55
- Publisher/songwriter: ~$0.10–$0.15
- Artist (if on label): ~$0.07–$0.10 (before recoupment)

**If You're Independent:**
- Distributor takes: $0 (or small percentage)
- You keep: ~$0.70 of every dollar (minus distributor fees)

### Royalty Accounting Terms

**Reserve for Returns:**
- Labels withhold 15–35% of royalties for potential returns (unsold inventory)
- Released after 6–12 months if no returns

**Free Goods:**
- Labels can give away 10–15% of units as promotional copies
- You don't earn royalties on free goods

**New Technology Clauses:**
- Older contracts may not specify streaming rates
- Labels often apply lower "new technology" rates (e.g. 50% of standard royalty)
- **Critical:** Ensure streaming pays full contract rate

**Packaging Deductions:**
- Labels may deduct 10–25% for "packaging" even on digital sales
- **Negotiate:** No packaging deductions on digital/streaming

**Foreign Rates:**
- Royalties on foreign sales often reduced by 25–50%
- **Negotiate:** Full rate on worldwide sales

### Accounting and Audits

**Royalty Statements:**
- Labels typically provide statements semi-annually or quarterly
- Statements are notoriously complex and opaque

**Audit Rights:**
- Most contracts allow you to audit label books once per year
- Audits cost $10K–$50K+ (you pay unless significant underpayment found)
- Labels often settle rather than face audit

**What to Watch For:**
- Missing streams/downloads
- Incorrect royalty rates applied
- Unrecouped balances that don't decrease
- Expenses charged incorrectly

## Contract Red Flags

### 🚩 Major Red Flags (Dealbreakers)

#### 1. **Perpetual Copyright Assignment**

**The Problem:** You sign away your masters forever, even after the contract ends.

**What to Demand:**
- Termination of assignment after contract ends
- Or, reversion after fixed period (e.g. 10–15 years)
- Or, reversion if label goes out of business

#### 2. **Blanket 360 Participation**

**The Problem:** Label takes percentage of ALL income without clear contribution to earning it.

**What to Demand:**
- Specific services tied to each revenue stream
- Sunset clauses (label participation ends when contract ends)
- Caps on participation percentages
- Exclusions for pre-existing revenue streams

#### 3. **Unlimited Cross-Collateralization**

**The Problem:** Losses from failed albums follow you forever.

**What to Demand:**
- No cross-collateralization, OR
- Limited to contract term only
- Separate accounting per album

#### 4. **No Release Commitment**

**The Problem:** Label can sit on your masters indefinitely without releasing.

**What to Demand:**
- Firm release date (e.g. within 6 months of delivery)
- If not released, masters revert to you
- "Use it or lose it" clauses

#### 5. **No Key Person Protection**

**The Problem:** Your A&R champion leaves; you're stuck with strangers.

**What to Demand:**
- Right to terminate if key A&R executive departs
- Or, right to match with new A&R before continuing

#### 6. **Controlled Composition Clauses**

**The Problem:** If you write your own songs, label pays reduced mechanical royalties (often 75% of statutory rate).

**What to Demand:**
- Full statutory rate for all compositions
- Or, minimum 90% of statutory rate

#### 7. **Unilateral Option Extensions**

**The Problem:** Label can extend contract indefinitely without your consent.

**What to Demand:**
- Mutual options (both parties must agree)
- Maximum number of option periods
- Performance thresholds for options to be valid

#### 8. **No Audit Rights**

**The Problem:** You can't verify if you're being paid correctly.

**What to Demand:**
- Right to audit annually
- Label pays audit costs if underpayment exceeds 5–10%

### 🟡 Yellow Flags (Negotiate Hard)

#### 1. **Long Initial Terms**
- 5+ album deals for unproven artists
- **Negotiate:** 1 album + 1 option initially

#### 2. **Low Royalty Rates**
- New artist rates below 12%
- **Negotiate:** 15% minimum, escalations based on sales

#### 3. **Excessive Reserve Percentages**
- Reserves above 35%
- **Negotiate:** 15–25% maximum

#### 4. **Packaging Deductions on Digital**
- Charging "packaging" fees on downloads/streaming
- **Negotiate:** No packaging deductions on digital

#### 5. **Broad Assignment of Rights**
- Granting rights to "all media now known or hereafter devised"
- **Negotiate:** Specific, enumerated rights

#### 6. **No Creative Approval**
- Zero say in artwork, videos, singles
- **Negotiate:** Approval rights on artwork, video treatments, single selection

### Predatory Terms to Avoid

| Term | Why It's Predatory | What to Do |
|------|-------------------|------------|
| **Sunset provisions on 360 income** | Label keeps taking % after contract ends | Limit to contract term only |
| **Buyout clauses** | Label can buy you out cheaply if you blow up | Remove or set fair market value |
| **Work-for-hire** | You have zero rights to your work | Never accept for master recordings |
| **Indemnification** | You pay for label's legal problems | Limit to your specific breaches |
| **Non-compete** | Can't record elsewhere during + after term | Limit to term only, narrow scope |
| **Assignment without consent** | Label can sell your contract to anyone | Require your approval for assignment |

## Negotiation Basics

### When to Get a Lawyer

**Always.** No exceptions. Music contracts are complex, and the label has teams of lawyers. You need someone who speaks their language.

**What to Look For:**
- Entertainment lawyer with music industry experience
- Not your cousin who does real estate law
- Someone who has negotiated with major labels before
- Flat fee or percentage of deal (typically 5% of advance)

**When to Involve Them:**
- Before you sign ANYTHING (even a term sheet)
- When negotiating deal points
- For contract review and markup
- For ongoing advice during the relationship

### What You Can Negotiate (By Leverage Level)

#### **New Artist (Minimal Leverage)**

You can still negotiate:
- Shorter initial term (1 album vs. 3)
- Approval rights on artwork
- Audit rights
- Firm release commitment
- No cross-collateralization (or limited)

#### **Developing Artist (Some Leverage)**

Add to the above:
- Higher royalty rate (14–16% vs. 12%)
- Creative approval on singles
- Reduced 360 participation
- Master reversion after 10–15 years
- Key person clause

#### **Proven Artist (Significant Leverage)**

You can negotiate:
- Joint venture structure
- 50/50 profit splits
- Master ownership (or reversion)
- Full creative control
- Limited or no 360 participation
- Termination rights
- Marketing minimums

#### **Star Artist (Maximum Leverage)**

Everything is on the table:
- Ownership of masters from day one
- Distribution deals only (no label services)
- Short terms (1 album)
- No 360 participation
- Full creative and business control

### Negotiation Strategies

#### 1. **Know Your BATNA**

BATNA = Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement

If you walk away from this deal, what's your next best option? Knowing this gives you power.

**Examples:**
- Strong DIY streaming numbers → Distributor deal is viable alternative
- Another label interested → Competitive leverage
- Sustainable independent income → Can wait for better terms

#### 2. **Get Multiple Offers**

Even if you have a preferred label, get offers from others. Competition improves your negotiating position.

#### 3. **Negotiate the Term Sheet First**

Don't let lawyers negotiate the full contract only to find fundamental disagreements. Agree on key terms upfront:
- Advance amount
- Royalty rate
- Term length
- 360 participation scope
- Master ownership

#### 4. **Prioritize Your Must-Haves**

You won't get everything. Decide what matters most:
- Creative control?
- Master ownership?
- Higher advance?
- Shorter term?

Trade less important points for must-haves.

#### 5. **Understand the Label's Perspective**

Labels are risk managers. They spread bets across many artists, knowing most won't recoup. Your job is to either:
- Reduce their perceived risk (proven track record), OR
- Demand terms commensurate with the risk you're taking (unknown artist = retain more rights)

### Key Negotiation Points

| Term | Starting Position | Target Position | Compromise |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|------------|
| **Royalty Rate** | 12% | 15% | 14% with escalations |
| **Term** | 5 albums | 1 album | 2 albums + 2 options |
| **360 Participation** | 25% of all income | 0% | 10–15% with sunset |
| **Master Ownership** | Label owns forever | You own | Reversion after 10–15 years |
| **Creative Control** | None | Full | Approval on artwork/singles |
| **Cross-collateralization** | Unlimited | None | Per-album only |

### Red Lines (When to Walk Away)

Some terms should be dealbreakers:
- Perpetual master ownership with no reversion
- Unlimited 360 participation with no sunset
- No audit rights
- No release commitment
- Work-for-hire language
- Non-compete extending beyond contract term
- You pay all recording costs (not an advance, but your own money)

### The "Standard Contract" Myth

Labels will tell you terms are "standard." This is rarely true. Everything is negotiable if you have leverage, and many "standard" terms are simply what labels prefer.

**Response:** "I understand this is your standard, but these are the terms that work for my career. Let's find a compromise."

### Post-Signature: Managing the Relationship

Once signed:
- Document everything in writing
- Understand your royalty statements
- Meet contractual obligations (delivery deadlines, etc.)
- Communicate regularly with your team
- Start planning your next move before this contract ends

## Summary: Choosing Your Path

### Decision Matrix

| Your Situation | Consider | Avoid |
|----------------|----------|-------|
| No fanbase, need funding | Indie label, investor | Major label 360 (no leverage) |
| Growing DIY, some income | Distribution + team | Long-term major commitments |
| Viral moment, heat | Multiple label offers | Rushing into first offer |
| Established indie, plateau | Joint venture, label services | Traditional deals giving up masters |
| Self-sustaining, control matters | Distribution only | Any deal taking masters forever |
| Maximum commercial ambition | Major with strong terms | Going it alone without resources |
