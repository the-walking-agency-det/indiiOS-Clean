## Detailed Comparisons by Category

### 1. Financial Structure

#### Major Label 360 Deal
```
Revenue Flow:
Gross Revenue → Label (70–85%) → Artist (15–30% of recording)
                              ↓
                    Label takes 10–30% of ALL other income
                    (touring, merch, publishing, endorsements)

Example for $500K total artist income:
- Recording royalties: $100K → Artist gets $15K (15%)
- Touring: $250K → Label takes $50K (20%), artist keeps $200K
- Merch: $100K → Label takes $20K (20%), artist keeps $80K
- Sync: $50K → Label takes $10K (20%), artist keeps $40K

Artist Total: $335K (67% of gross)
Label Total: $165K (33% of gross) + owns masters
```

#### Major Label Traditional Deal
```
Revenue Flow:
Gross Revenue → Label (85–88%) → Artist (12–16%)
Other income (touring, merch, etc.) → Artist keeps 100%

Example for $500K total artist income:
- Recording royalties: $100K → Artist gets $14K (14%)
- Touring: $250K → Artist keeps $250K
- Merch: $100K → Artist keeps $100K
- Sync: $50K → Artist keeps $50K

Artist Total: $414K (82.8% of gross)
Label Total: $86K (17.2% of gross) + owns masters
```

#### Independent Label Deal
```
Revenue Flow:
Gross Revenue → Costs deducted → Profit split 50/50 or 60/40

Example for $100K gross, $40K costs, 50/50 split:
- Gross: $100K
- Costs: $40K (recording, marketing, distribution)
- Net profit: $60K
- Artist: $30K
- Label: $30K

If artist owns masters: Artist can license to other territories later
```

#### Distribution Deal
```
Revenue Flow:
Gross Revenue → Distributor fee (0–15%) → Artist (85–100%)

Example for $50K gross with DistroKid ($23/year):
- Gross: $50K
- Distributor fee: ~$23/year flat
- Artist keeps: ~$49,977

All other income (touring, merch, sync) → Artist keeps 100%
```

### 3. Timeline Comparison

#### Major Label Release Timeline
```
Month 1-3:   Sign deal, receive advance
Month 3-9:   Record album (label approves songs/producers)
Month 9-12:  Mixing, mastering, artwork approval
Month 12-15: Marketing campaign setup
Month 15:    Release date
Month 15+:   Promotion, touring, recoupment tracking

Total: 12–18 months from signing to release
```

#### Indie Label Release Timeline
```
Month 1-2:   Sign deal, receive advance
Month 2-5:   Record album (more collaborative)
Month 5-7:   Mixing, mastering, artwork
Month 7-8:   Marketing setup
Month 8:     Release date

Total: 6–10 months from signing to release
```

#### Distribution Release Timeline
```
Day 1:       Upload to distributor
Day 2-7:     Processing, distribution to platforms
Day 7+:      Live on streaming services

Total: 1–7 days from upload to release
```

### 4. Break-Even Analysis

#### Scenario: Artist releases album generating $500K gross revenue

**Major Label 360 (15% royalty, 20% 360 participation)**
```
Recording Revenue: $200K
- Label keeps: $170K (85%)
- Artist royalties: $30K (15%)

Other Revenue: $300K (touring $200K, merch $80K, sync $20K)
- Label 360 cut: $60K (20%)
- Artist keeps: $240K

Advance: $400K (recoupable)
Artist owes: $400K - $30K (royalties) = $370K unrecouped

Result: Artist receives $240K from non-recording income
        but remains $370K in debt to label for recording
        Label owns masters in perpetuity
```

**Independent Label (50/50 profit split, $50K advance)**
```
Total Revenue: $500K
Costs: $100K (recording, marketing)
Net Profit: $400K

Artist share: $200K
Label share: $200K

Advance recoupment: $200K - $50K = $150K to artist

Result: Artist receives $150K
        Label may own or share masters (negotiable)
```

**Distribution (DistroKid, $23/year)**
```
Total Revenue: $500K
Distributor fee: $23

Artist keeps: $499,977

Result: Artist receives ~$500K
        Artist owns masters completely
```

## Decision Framework

### Choose a Major Label If:
- [ ] You need significant upfront capital ($300K+)
- [ ] You want maximum commercial reach
- [ ] You have limited existing revenue streams
- [ ] You're willing to trade control for resources
- [ ] You understand and accept the recoupment math
- [ ] You have strong negotiating leverage

### Choose an Independent Label If:
- [ ] You want a balance of support and control
- [ ] You value creative collaboration over pure independence
- [ ] You need some funding but not major label scale
- [ ] You want shorter contract terms
- [ ] You prefer a more personal working relationship
- [ ] You're building toward a major deal later

### Choose Distribution Only If:
- [ ] You have existing income streams to protect
- [ ] You want complete creative control
- [ ] You can self-fund recording and marketing
- [ ] You have a team (manager, publicist) or can build one
- [ ] You understand the DIY workload
- [ ] You want to own your masters

## Real-World Examples

### Major Label Success Story
**Artist:** Billie Eilish  
**Deal:** Interscope (major)  
**Why it worked:** Massive marketing budget, global reach, strong A&R support, family team maintained creative input  
**Result:** Multi-platinum albums, Grammy wins, global stardom

### Major Label Cautionary Tale
**Artist:** [Various unrecouped artists]  
**Deal:** Traditional major label  
**What happened:** Album didn't sell, artist dropped, unrecouped balance never cleared, no masters returned  
**Result:** Years of work, no income, no catalog control

### Indie Label Success Story
**Artist:** Arcade Fire  
**Deal:** Merge Records  
**Why it worked:** Artist-friendly terms, creative freedom, label invested in long-term growth  
**Result:** Grammy-winning albums, maintained control, profitable partnership

### Distribution Success Story
**Artist:** Russ  
**Deal:** Self-released via distribution, then Columbia (distribution deal only)  
**Why it worked:** Built fanbase independently, retained ownership, negotiated from strength  
**Result:** Retains masters, keeps majority of revenue, major reach

## Key Takeaways

1. **No deal type is inherently "better"** — it depends on your specific situation, goals, and leverage

2. **The advance is not free money** — understand recoupment before signing anything

3. **Control has value** — calculate what you're giving up vs. what you're receiving

4. **Your leverage determines your terms** — build your career before negotiating, not after

5. **Ownership compounds** — masters become more valuable over time; giving them away is expensive

6. **Lawyers are non-negotiable** — the cost of good legal advice is trivial compared to the cost of a bad deal
