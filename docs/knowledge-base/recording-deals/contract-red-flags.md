# Contract Red Flags: Warning Signs in Recording Deals

> A field guide to spotting predatory terms before you sign  
> **Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## 🚨 DEALBREAKERS: Walk Away If You See These

### 1. Perpetual Copyright Assignment

**What It Looks Like:**
> "Artist hereby assigns all rights, title, and interest in the Masters to Label in perpetuity throughout the universe."

**Why It's Dangerous:**
- You never get your masters back
- Even after the contract ends, the label owns your recordings forever
- Your catalog—your life's work—belongs to someone else indefinitely

**What to Demand:**
- Reversion clause: Masters return to you after contract term ends
- Or after fixed period (e.g., 10–15 years)
- Or upon label bankruptcy/cessation of business
- Or if label fails to commercially exploit for X years

**Red Flag Severity:** 🔴🔴🔴🔴🔴 CRITICAL

---

### 2. Blanket 360 Participation Without Sunset

**What It Looks Like:**
> "Label shall be entitled to twenty percent (20%) of all Artist's income from any and all sources, including but not limited to touring, merchandise, endorsements, publishing, acting, and any other activities."

**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Label takes cut of income they had no hand in generating
- Continues even after recording contract ends (if no sunset clause)
- Can apply to pre-existing revenue streams

**What to Demand:**
- Specific enumerated revenue streams only
- Sunset clause: 360 participation ends when recording contract ends
- Exclusion for pre-existing income streams
- Caps on participation percentage
- Services rendered must be tied to each revenue stream

**Red Flag Severity:** 🔴🔴🔴🔴🔴 CRITICAL

---

### 3. Unlimited Cross-Collateralization

**What It Looks Like:**
> "All advances and recording costs shall be recoupable from any and all royalties payable to Artist under this Agreement or any other agreement between the parties."

**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Losses from Album 1 follow you to Album 2, 3, 4...
- You may never see royalties even if later albums succeed
- Creates "walking dead" scenario where you're contractually bound but financially underwater

**What to Demand:**
- Non-cross-collateralized accounting (each album stands alone)
- Or limit cross-collateralization to specific contract term only
- Or sunset on cross-collateralization after X years

**Red Flag Severity:** 🔴🔴🔴🔴🔴 CRITICAL

---

### 4. No Release Commitment

**What It Looks Like:**
> "Label shall have the right, but not the obligation, to release the Masters."

**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Label can sit on your finished album indefinitely
- You're contractually bound but your music never comes out
- No recourse if label changes priorities

**What to Demand:**
- Firm release commitment: Label must release within X months of delivery
- "Use it or lose it" clause: If not released within X months, masters revert
- Termination right if release doesn't happen

**Red Flag Severity:** 🔴🔴🔴🔴🔴 CRITICAL

---

### 5. No Key Person Protection

**What It Looks Like:**
> *(No mention of what happens if your A&R leaves)*

**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Your champion at the label departs
- You're stuck with strangers who may not believe in you
- Label can reassign you to junior staff or ignore you

**What to Demand:**
- Right to terminate if key A&R executive departs
- Or right to approve new A&R assignment
- Or reduction in term if key person leaves

**Red Flag Severity:** 🔴🔴🔴🔴 HIGH

---

### 6. Controlled Composition Clauses

**What It Looks Like:**
> "With respect to Controlled Compositions, the mechanical royalty rate shall be seventy-five percent (75%) of the statutory rate."

**Why It's Dangerous:**
- If you write your own songs, you get paid LESS than the standard rate
- Penalizes you for being a songwriter
- Can cost you significant publishing income

**What to Demand:**
- Full statutory rate (100%) for all compositions
- Or minimum 90% of statutory rate
- No controlled composition reduction

**Red Flag Severity:** 🔴🔴🔴🔴 HIGH

---

### 7. Unilateral Option Extensions

**What It Looks Like:**
> "Label shall have the right to extend this Agreement for one (1) additional album by written notice to Artist."

**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Label can extend your contract indefinitely without your consent
- No mutual agreement required
- You have no say in continuing the relationship

**What to Demand:**
- Mutual options (both parties must agree)
- Maximum number of option periods (e.g., 2 options maximum)
- Performance thresholds (options only valid if previous album sold X units)

**Red Flag Severity:** 🔴🔴🔴🔴 HIGH

---

### 8. No Audit Rights

**What It Looks Like:**
> *(No provision for auditing royalty statements)*  
> Or: "Artist may audit only with Label's prior written consent."

**Why It's Dangerous:**
- You can't verify if you're being paid correctly
- No way to catch errors or underpayments
- Labels make mistakes; without audit rights, you never know

**What to Demand:**
- Right to audit annually at your expense
- Label pays audit costs if underpayment exceeds 5–10%
- Access to all relevant books and records

**Red Flag Severity:** 🔴🔴🔴🔴 HIGH

---

## ⚠️ YELLOW FLAGS: Negotiate Hard

### 9. Long Initial Terms (5+ Albums)

**Why It's Concerning:**
- Locks you in for most of your career
- No proof of concept before major commitment
- Hard to pivot if things don't work out

**What to Negotiate:**
- 1 album + 1–2 options initially
- Shorter terms with renegotiation triggers

---

### 10. Low Royalty Rates (<12%)

**Why It's Concerning:**
- Below industry standard for new artists
- Harder to recoup
- Less incentive for label to invest

**What to Negotiate:**
- 15% minimum for new artists
- Escalation clauses (e.g., +1% at 100K units, +1% at 500K units)

---

### 11. Excessive Reserve Percentages (>35%)

**Why It's Concerning:**
- Label withholds too much for potential returns
- Delays your royalty payments
- May never be released

**What to Negotiate:**
- 15–25% maximum reserve
- Release of reserves after 6–12 months

---

### 12. Packaging Deductions on Digital Sales

**Why It's Concerning:**
- "Packaging" fees for CDs/vinyl make sense
- Charging packaging on downloads/streaming is pure profit for label
- Outdated concept applied to new technology

**What to Negotiate:**
- No packaging deductions on digital/streaming
- Or maximum 5% on digital

---

### 13. Broad Assignment of Rights

**What It Looks Like:**
> "All rights now known or hereafter devised throughout the universe in perpetuity."

**Why It's Concerning:**
- Grants rights to technologies that don't exist yet
- Impossible to know what you're giving away
- Overly broad language

**What to Negotiate:**
- Specific, enumerated rights
- Limit to current known technologies
- Reversion of unused rights after X years

---

### 14. No Creative Approval Rights

**Why It's Concerning:**
- Zero say in your own art
- Label can change your music, image, direction
- No protection from bad creative decisions

**What to Negotiate:**
- Approval rights on final masters
- Approval rights on artwork
- Approval rights on video treatments
- Consultation on single selection

---

## 🚫 PREDATORY TERMS TO AVOID

### Work-for-Hire Language

**What It Looks Like:**
> "The Masters shall be deemed works made for hire."

**Why It's Unacceptable:**
- You have ZERO rights to your own work
- Not even the limited rights of a typical label deal
- Used for employees, not recording artists

**What to Do:**
- Never accept work-for-hire for master recordings
- Insist on license or assignment with reversion
- This is a dealbreaker

---

### Buyout Clauses

**What It Looks Like:**
> "Label may purchase all rights from Artist for $10,000 upon Artist achieving 1,000,000 streams."

**Why It's Predatory:**
- Label can buy you out cheaply if you blow up
- No fair market value calculation
- Punishes success

**What to Do:**
- Remove buyout clauses entirely
- Or set fair market value based on trailing 12-month revenue
- Or require mutual consent

---

### Indemnification

**What It Looks Like:**
> "Artist shall indemnify and hold harmless Label from any and all claims, damages, and expenses."

**Why It's Dangerous:**
- You pay for label's legal problems
- Even if you didn't cause them
- Can be financially ruinous

**What to Negotiate:**
- Limit indemnification to your specific breaches
- Cap indemnification amounts
- Require label to indemnify you for their breaches

---

### Non-Compete Clauses

**What It Looks Like:**
> "Artist shall not record for any other party during the Term and for five (5) years thereafter."

**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Prevents you from working after contract ends
- Can block your entire career
- May be unenforceable but expensive to fight

**What to Negotiate:**
- Limit to contract term only
- Narrow scope (e.g., only competing recordings, not all music)
- Reasonable geographic limits

---

### Assignment Without Consent

**What It Looks Like:**
> "Label may assign this Agreement to any third party without Artist's consent."

**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Label can sell your contract to anyone
- No say in who controls your career
- Could end up with company you don't trust

**What to Negotiate:**
- Require artist consent for assignment
- Or limit assignment to affiliates only
- Or require notice and right to terminate if assigned

## 📋 RED FLAG CHECKLIST

Before signing any deal, verify:

### Ownership & Rights
- [ ] Masters revert to you after contract ends (or within 10–15 years)
- [ ] No work-for-hire language
- [ ] Specific rights granted, not "all rights now known or hereafter devised"
- [ ] Unused rights revert after X years

### Financial Terms
- [ ] Royalty rate at least 12% (preferably 15%+)
- [ ] No controlled composition clauses (or minimum 90% statutory rate)
- [ ] No packaging deductions on digital/streaming
- [ ] Reserve percentage 25% or less
- [ ] Foreign royalties at full rate (not reduced)

### Recoupment & Accounting
- [ ] No cross-collateralization (or limited to term only)
- [ ] Only personal advance is recoupable (not recording costs)
- [ ] Audit rights included
- [ ] Semi-annual or quarterly royalty statements

### Term & Commitment
- [ ] Initial term 1–2 albums maximum
- [ ] Mutual options (not unilateral)
- [ ] Firm release commitment with reversion if not met
- [ ] Key person protection clause

### 360 & Other Income
- [ ] No 360 participation, OR
- [ ] Specific revenue streams only (not "all income")
- [ ] Sunset clause (ends when recording contract ends)
- [ ] Exclusion for pre-existing income

### Creative Control
- [ ] Approval rights on final masters
- [ ] Approval rights on artwork
- [ ] Consultation on single selection
- [ ] Consultation on video treatments

### Legal Protections
- [ ] No broad indemnification
- [ ] No non-compete extending beyond term
- [ ] Assignment requires your consent
- [ ] No buyout clauses

## 🎯 WHEN TO WALK AWAY

**Walk away immediately if:**
1. Label refuses to negotiate on perpetual ownership
2. Label insists on unlimited 360 participation with no sunset
3. No audit rights included
4. No release commitment
5. Work-for-hire language present
6. Non-compete extends beyond contract term
7. You're being pressured to sign quickly
8. Label won't let you take contract to a lawyer

**Consider walking away if:**
1. Royalty rate below 12% with no escalation
2. Term longer than 3 albums initially
3. Cross-collateralization unlimited
4. Controlled composition clause at 75% or below
5. No creative approval rights
6. Assignment allowed without consent
7. Your lawyer advises against it

---

## 💡 PRO TIPS

1. **Always get a lawyer** — Entertainment law is specialized; your cousin who does real estate can't help here

2. **Read every word** — Don't skip sections because they're "standard" or "boring"

3. **Ask "what if" questions** — What if the label doesn't release? What if my A&R leaves? What if I blow up on TikTok?

4. **Get it in writing** — Verbal promises mean nothing; if it's not in the contract, it doesn't exist

5. **Understand the math** — Calculate what success looks like and what failure looks like under the proposed terms

6. **Trust your gut** — If something feels wrong, it probably is

7. **Remember: everything is negotiable** — "Standard" just means "what the label prefers"
