# Management Contracts: What to Watch For

## The Manager-Artist Relationship

A personal manager guides an artist's career, making decisions about image, team building, and long-term strategy. Unlike agents who book specific gigs, managers shape the entire trajectory.

**This relationship is intimate, long-term, and notoriously difficult to unwind.** Understanding the contract is essential because you'll likely live with it for years.

---

## The Core Components

### 1. Term (Duration)

The length of the contract and how it extends.

**Common Structures:**

| Type | Description | Risk Level |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **Fixed Term** | Set period (1-3 years), no extensions | Low |
| **Initial + Options** | 1 year + 3-4 label options | Medium |
| **Term of Years** | Fixed multi-year (3-5 years) | Medium |
| **Career Term** | Life of career (AVOID) | High |

**What to Negotiate:**
- Shorter initial term (1 year) with mutual options
- Performance benchmarks for extensions
- No automatic renewals — must be actively exercised
- Sunset period separate from active term

**Red Flag:** "Term shall continue for the duration of Artist's career in the entertainment industry."

### 2. Commission Structure

How much the manager takes and from what.

**Standard Commission:** 15-20% of gross income

**Commissionable Income:**

| Income Source | Typically Commissioned | Notes |
|---------------|------------------------|-------|
| **Recording royalties** | Yes | Including advances |
| **Publishing royalties** | Yes | Often 15-20% |
| **Live performance fees** | Yes | Primary income source |
| **Merchandise sales** | Yes | Net or gross depending |
| **Sync licensing** | Yes | Often negotiated separately |
| **Endorsements/sponsorships** | Yes | Manager usually sources |
| **Songwriting for others** | Sometimes | Negotiate separately |
| **Acting/film income** | Sometimes | May need separate deal |
| **Investment income** | No | Personal investments |
| **Inheritance/gifts** | No | Personal funds |

**Commission Calculation Methods:**

**Gross Receipts:**
- Manager gets percentage of total money received
- Simple but includes money artist never sees (production costs, agent commissions)

**Net Receipts:**
- Manager gets percentage after specific deductions
- More fair but "net" must be clearly defined

**Modified Gross:**
- Gross minus specific pre-agreed deductions
- Common compromise

### 3. Sunset Clause (Post-Term Commissions)

The sunset clause determines how long the manager continues to earn commissions after the contract ends.

**Why It Exists:**
- Manager develops opportunities that pay off later
- Projects started during term complete after termination
- Prevents immediate termination once success is achieved

**Standard Sunset Terms:**

| Duration | Commission Rate | Typical Use |
|----------|-----------------|-------------|
| **1-2 years** | Full commission (15-20%) | Short-term management |
| **3-5 years** | Full commission | Standard arrangement |
| **5-7 years** | Full commission | Long-term career building |
| **After initial sunset** | Reduced (5-10%) | Extended protection |
| **Life of deals signed** | Full on those deals | For major contracts negotiated |

**Negotiation Points:**
- Cap sunset period (5 years maximum)
- Reduce commission rate over time
- Limit to deals actually negotiated by manager
- Exclude new projects started after termination
- Require active management for full commission

**Sample Language:**
> "For a period of five (5) years following expiration or termination, Manager shall receive a commission of fifteen percent (15%) on gross income derived from contracts and agreements negotiated or substantially negotiated during the term. After five years, commission shall reduce to five percent (5%) for an additional three (3) years."

### 4. Key Man Provision

Protects the artist if the primary manager leaves the company.

**The Problem:**
You sign because you trust Sarah, but Sarah leaves the management company after 6 months. Now you're stuck with a junior associate you don't know.

**Key Man Clause Options:**

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Termination Right** | Artist can terminate if specific person leaves |
| **Approval Right** | Artist must approve replacement manager |
| **Reduced Commission** | Commission drops if key person departs |
| **Notice Period** | Advance notice before key person can leave |

**Sample Language:**
> "Artist's obligation to remain under management is expressly conditioned upon [Name] remaining actively involved in Artist's career. In the event [Name] ceases to be involved, Artist shall have the right to terminate this agreement upon ninety (90) days written notice."

### 5. Expense Reimbursement

Managers incur costs on behalf of artists. This section defines what gets reimbursed.

**Typical Reimbursable Expenses:**
- Travel for meetings on artist's behalf
- Phone/internet for business use
- Marketing materials and press kits
- Conference fees for industry events
- Legal consultation for contract review

**Expenses That Should NOT Be Reimbursed:**
- Office overhead (rent, staff, utilities)
- Entertainment of other clients
- Personal travel unrelated to artist
- Marketing for management company generally

**Protections to Include:**
- Cap on monthly expenses ($500-2,000 typical)
- Prior approval required for expenses over threshold
- Detailed monthly accounting with receipts
- Right to audit expense records

## The Power Imbalance

### Why Management Contracts Favor Managers

1. **Information advantage** — Managers know the industry better
2. **Desperation** — Artists often sign when they need help most
3. **Long-term value** — A hit song pays for decades
4. **Relationship dynamics** — Personal bonds cloud business judgment
5. **Career dependency** — Hard to switch managers mid-career

### Protecting Yourself

**Before Signing:**
- Get an entertainment lawyer (not the manager's lawyer)
- Talk to other artists managed by the company
- Understand every clause before agreeing
- Negotiate from strength (even if you have none)

**During the Relationship:**
- Maintain independent financial oversight
- Review monthly statements carefully
- Keep communication in writing
- Document performance issues

## Termination Provisions

### Termination for Cause

Either party can terminate if the other:
- Materially breaches the agreement
- Fails to cure breach within 30 days of notice
- Engages in conduct harming the other's reputation
- Becomes insolvent or bankrupt

**Common Breaches by Managers:**
- Failure to account for or pay money owed
- Failure to perform services with reasonable diligence
- Commingling artist funds with manager's funds
- Taking secret commissions or kickbacks

**Common Breaches by Artists:**
- Failure to pay commissions owed
- Signing deals without paying manager's commission
- Disparaging manager publicly
- Refusing reasonable professional opportunities

### Termination for Convenience

Some contracts allow either party to terminate without cause:
- Notice period (usually 30-180 days)
- Commission obligations continue during notice period
- Sunset clause still applies post-termination

### The "Actively Engaged" Requirement

Protects against managers who sign you then ignore you:

**Sample Language:**
> "Manager shall use reasonable efforts to advance Artist's career. If Manager fails to secure any material income for Artist for a continuous period of six (6) months, Artist may terminate this agreement upon thirty (30) days written notice."

## Commission Disputes

### What Counts as "Your" Income?

**Disputed Scenarios:**

| Scenario | Manager's Position | Artist's Position |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Song written before management** | Commission if placed during term | Not commissionable |
| **Deal negotiated after termination** | Commission if started during term | Only if signed during term |
| **Investment income from royalties** | Commission on all derived income | Not commissionable |
| **Income from side business** | Commission if manager helped launch | Personal income |
| **Inheritance** | Commission if career enabled wealth | Personal, not commissionable |

### Avoiding Disputes

**Clear Contract Language:**
- Define "gross income" specifically
- List excluded income categories
- Specify geographic and field of use limitations
- Clarify treatment of pre-existing works

**Best Practice:**
Attach a schedule of excluded income sources:
> "Notwithstanding the foregoing, the following shall not be commissionable: (a) income from investments, (b) inheritance or gifts, (c) income from [specific pre-existing songs], (d) personal loans..."

## Red Flags in Management Contracts

### 🚩 Career-Length Terms
Any contract that extends beyond 5 years or uses "career duration" language.

### 🚩 No Sunset Cap
Sunset period that extends indefinitely or "for the life of all deals signed."

### 🚩 Commission on Everything
No exclusions for personal income, investments, or pre-management work.

### 🚩 No Key Man Protection
No ability to leave if your specific manager departs.

### 🚩 Vague Expense Provisions
"Artist shall reimburse all reasonable expenses" without caps or definitions.

### 🚩 No Termination for Convenience
Can only terminate for cause, locking you in even if the relationship sours.

### 🚩 Automatic Renewals
Contract extends automatically unless artist objects (easy to miss deadlines).

### 🚩 Cross-Collateralization
Manager can use earnings from one project to cover losses on another.

### 🚩 Power of Attorney
Manager can sign contracts and make binding decisions without artist approval.

### 🚩 Assignment Without Consent
Manager can sell your contract to another company without your approval.

## Negotiation Strategies

### For New Artists (Low Leverage)

**What You Can Still Get:**
- 1-year initial term with mutual option
- 15% commission (not 20%)
- Key man provision for your specific manager
- Cap on reimbursable expenses ($1,000/month)
- Termination for convenience with 90-day notice
- Sunset period capped at 3-5 years

**What to Accept:**
- Manager's standard form agreement as starting point
- 20% commission if manager has proven track record
- Limited exclusivity (manager can handle other clients)

### For Established Artists (Higher Leverage)

**What to Push For:**
- 10-15% commission (industry standard for proven earners)
- Modified gross instead of pure gross
- Detailed list of non-commissionable income
- Shorter sunset period (3 years max)
- Approval rights over major deals
- No automatic renewals

**What to Avoid:**
- Any career-length language
- Assignment without consent
- Power of attorney provisions
- Unlimited expense reimbursement

## Management vs. Other Roles

### Personal Manager vs. Business Manager

| Aspect | Personal Manager | Business Manager |
|--------|------------------|------------------|
| **Focus** | Career strategy, image | Finances, taxes, investments |
| **Commission** | 15-20% of gross | 5% of gross or flat fee |
| **Term** | Long-term (3-5 years) | Ongoing, easier to change |
| **Key duties** | Team building, A&R, marketing | Accounting, tax planning, budgeting |

**Best Practice:** Have both, or at least separate business management from personal management.

### Personal Manager vs. Booking Agent

| Aspect | Personal Manager | Booking Agent |
|--------|------------------|---------------|
| **Primary role** | Career oversight | Booking live performances |
| **Commission** | 15-20% of all income | 10% of performance fees only |
| **Exclusivity** | Usually exclusive | Often non-exclusive by territory |
| **Union regulation** | Not union regulated | AFM/SAG-AFTRA regulated |

**Important:** Managers cannot book gigs (union rules). Agents cannot manage careers. Know the difference.

## When to Walk Away

**Terminate the Relationship If:**
- Manager consistently fails to return calls/emails
- No meaningful career progress in 6-12 months
- Financial irregularities or missing payments
- Manager takes on too many clients to focus on you
- Creative vision fundamentally misaligned
- Manager asks you to compromise integrity/ethics
- Better opportunities blocked by manager's relationships

**How to Exit:**
1. Review contract termination provisions
2. Document performance issues
3. Consult entertainment attorney
4. Send formal termination notice per contract
5. Calculate final commission obligations
6. Ensure sunset clause compliance
7. Notify team (agent, lawyer, business manager)
8. Transition ongoing projects professionally
