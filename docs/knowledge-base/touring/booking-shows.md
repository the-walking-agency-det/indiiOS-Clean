# Booking Shows: DIY vs. Hiring an Agent

The decision between booking your own shows and working with a professional agent is one of the most significant choices in a touring career. This guide breaks down both approaches, when to use each, and how to transition between them.

---

## DIY Booking

### When DIY Makes Sense

- **First tours** — Testing markets, building initial relationships
- **Regional circuits** — Areas you know well, within driving distance
- **Genre-specific scenes** — DIY punk, electronic, hip-hop often have established networks
- **Limited budget** — Can't afford agent commissions yet
- **Creative control priority** — You want complete say over every show

### The DIY Booking Process

#### 1. Research Phase

**Identify Target Markets**
- Start with cities where you have streaming data showing listeners
- Look at similar artists' tour routes
- Consider college towns (often easier entry points)
- Factor in drive time between cities (aim for 4-6 hours max)

**Find Venues**
- Capacity should match your draw (be realistic)
- Genre fit matters — metal clubs don't work for folk artists
- Check recent calendars for similar acts
- Note contact information for talent buyers

**Research Tools:**
- **Songkick/T Bandsintown** — See where similar artists play
- **Venue websites** — Most list talent buyer contacts
- **Social media** — Follow venues and promoters in target markets
- **Artist communities** — Reddit, Facebook groups for your genre

#### 2. Outreach Phase

**The Initial Contact**

Email template structure:
```
Subject: [Date Range] Booking Inquiry - [Artist Name] - [Genre]

Hi [Talent Buyer Name],

I'm [Your Name] from [Artist Name], a [genre] act from [hometown]. 
We're planning a tour for [month/season] and would love to discuss 
a show at [Venue Name].

[One compelling sentence about your music or recent achievements]

Recent stats:
- [Monthly listeners/followers number]
- [Recent notable show with attendance]
- [Press quote or playlist placement]

We're looking at [specific date range, e.g., "week of October 15th"] 
and can work with [weekday/weekend preference].

I've attached our EPK and would love to send over music if there's 
interest. Thanks for your time!

Best,
[Your Name]
[Contact info]
[Website/social links]
```

**Follow-Up Strategy**
- Wait 1 week before first follow-up
- Keep follow-ups brief and professional
- Maximum 3 attempts per venue
- Move on if no response — don't burn bridges with persistence

#### 3. Negotiation Phase

**Understanding Deal Types**

| Deal Type | How It Works | Best For |
|-----------|--------------|----------|
| **Flat Guarantee** | Fixed payment regardless of attendance | Established draw, risk aversion |
| **Door Deal** | Percentage of ticket sales (often 70-100% after expenses) | Unknown markets, building draw |
| **Versus Deal** | Guarantee vs. percentage, whichever is higher | Balanced approach |
| **Deposit + Balance** | Partial payment upfront, remainder on show day | Financial security |

**Typical Venue Splits**
- 100% to artist (rare, usually DIY spaces)
- 80/20 (artist/venue) — common for door deals
- 70/30 — standard for many clubs
- 60/40 — venues with higher overhead

**Negotiable Points**
- Ticket price
- Minimum guarantee
- Merchandise percentage (venue typically takes 10-20%)
- Production support (sound, lights, stagehands)
- Hospitality (meals, drinks, green room)
- Marketing commitment

#### 4. Confirmation Phase

**Get It in Writing**

Every confirmed show needs:
- Date and time
- Load-in and soundcheck times
- Performance time and set length
- Financial terms (guarantee, split, expenses)
- Merchandise terms
- Cancellation policy
- Both parties' signatures

**The Contract**
- Can be simple email confirmation for small shows
- Formal contract for larger guarantees
- Deposit requirements vary by market
- Always keep copies of all correspondence

### DIY Booking Best Practices

**Build Relationships**
- Thank talent buyers after shows
- Send follow-up after tour with stats
- Recommend other artists when appropriate
- Attend shows at venues you want to play

**Be Professional**
- Respond to emails within 24 hours
- Meet all deadlines for promo materials
- Show up on time for load-in
- Leave venues clean and undamaged

**Track Everything**
- Spreadsheet of all contacts
- Show history with attendance and financials
- Communication log
- Follow-up reminders

## Working with a Booking Agent

### When to Consider an Agent

- **Consistent draw** — You're selling 100+ tickets in multiple markets
- **Time constraints** — Booking is taking time from creative work
- **Market expansion** — You need access to markets you can't reach DIY
- **Career growth** — Ready for larger venues and better guarantees
- **Industry validation** — Agents add credibility with promoters

### What Agents Do

**Core Responsibilities:**
- Route tours efficiently
- Negotiate with promoters and venues
- Secure better guarantees through relationships
- Handle contracts and deposits
- Advise on career trajectory
- Connect you with other industry professionals

**What They Don't Do:**
- Guarantee attendance (that's on you and your marketing)
- Handle day-of-show logistics (that's tour management)
- Book local support acts (usually)
- Manage your social media or marketing

### Finding an Agent

**Research Agencies**
- Look at agencies representing similar artists
- Check roster fit — do they specialize in your genre?
- Consider agency size — boutique vs. major
- Geographic focus — some specialize in regions

**Approaching Agents**

Your pitch package should include:
- Streaming numbers and growth trends
- Recent show history with attendance
- Press coverage and playlist placements
- Social media following and engagement
- Professional EPK with music, photos, bio
- Clear artistic vision and goals

**The Meeting**
- Be prepared to discuss your career goals
- Ask about their vision for your development
- Understand their communication style
- Discuss their other clients (potential conflicts?)
- Clarify expectations on both sides

### Agent Commission Structure

**Standard Terms:**
- **10%** of gross guarantees (most common)
- **15%** for developing artists or more intensive work
- **20%+** for major agencies or package deals

**What Commission Covers:**
- All live performance bookings
- Festival appearances
- Private/corporate events
- Sometimes TV/radio appearances

**What Commission Doesn't Cover:**
- Recording royalties
- Publishing income
- Merchandise sales (usually)
- Sync licensing

### The Agent Relationship

**Communication Expectations:**
- Weekly or bi-weekly check-ins during active booking
- Response time expectations (24-48 hours typical)
- Preferred communication methods
- After-hours availability for emergencies

**Decision Making:**
- You typically have final approval on all dates
- Agent advises on routing and strategy
- Discuss any show before confirming
- Understand their reasoning for recommendations

**Evaluating Performance:**
- Are they proactive or reactive?
- Do they communicate clearly and promptly?
- Are they getting you appropriate opportunities?
- Is your touring business growing?

## Hybrid Approaches

### Regional Agents
Work with different agents for different territories:
- US agent for North American touring
- UK/EU agent for European dates
- Regional specialists for specific markets

### Co-Agents
Some artists work with multiple agents at the same agency or different agencies for different types of shows (clubs vs. festivals).

### Self-Booking + Agent Assistance
Maintain some DIY relationships while having an agent handle major markets or routing assistance.

---

## Making the Transition

### From DIY to Agent

**Timing Considerations:**
- You have 6-12 months of consistent show history
- You're ready to tour more extensively
- The time cost of DIY booking is too high
- You have momentum that needs professional management

**The Handoff:**
- Inform venues of your new representation
- Provide agent with your complete contact history
- Introduce agent to key promoter relationships you've built
- Maintain personal relationships you've developed

### From Agent to DIY

Sometimes artists return to DIY booking:
- Agent relationship isn't working out
- Career direction changes
- Want more control
- Financial considerations

**Important:** Check your contract for termination clauses and sunset periods (commission on dates booked while under contract).

## Red Flags

### DIY Booking Red Flags
- Venues that won't put terms in writing
- Promoters with no show history or references
- Unreasonable deposit requirements (more than 50%)
- Vague or changing terms

### Agent Red Flags
- Won't provide references from current clients
- Pressures you to take inappropriate shows
- Doesn't communicate regularly
- Asks for money upfront (legitimate agents work on commission)
- Promises unrealistic results
- Roster conflicts with similar artists

## Quick Decision Framework

| Factor | DIY | Agent |
|--------|-----|-------|
| Monthly listeners | <50K | >100K |
| Consistent ticket sales | <100/show | >150/show |
| Markets you can reach | Regional | National/International |
| Time available for booking | High | Low |
| Budget for commission | None | 10-15% of gross |
| Career stage | Developing | Established/Growing |

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **DIY booking builds essential skills** — Even if you get an agent later, understanding the process helps
2. **Relationships are everything** — Whether DIY or with an agent, your reputation travels
3. **Professionalism wins** — Respond promptly, meet commitments, communicate clearly
4. **Know your value** — Don't overplay or undersell your draw
5. **Agents are partners, not saviors** — They amplify what you're already doing, not create it from nothing
6. **Contracts protect everyone** — Always get terms in writing
