## The Touring Ecosystem

### Key Roles on Tour

Understanding who does what is essential for smooth operations:

#### Tour Manager (TM)
The organizational backbone of any tour. The TM handles:
- Overall logistics and scheduling
- Financial management and settlements
- Vendor and venue relationships
- Crisis management and problem-solving
- Communication hub between artist, label, and venues

#### Road Manager
The day-to-day operations person on the ground:
- Travel coordination (flights, ground transport, hotels)
- Local runner duties
- Guest list management
- Day-of-show logistics
- Often overlaps with TM on smaller tours

#### Production Manager
The technical logistics expert:
- Stage plots and input lists
- Equipment coordination and backline
- Crew management
- Load-in and load-out scheduling
- Technical rider fulfillment

#### Artist (You)
Your primary job is the performance. Everything else should support this.

## The Tour Lifecycle

### Phase 1: Planning (3-6 Months Out)
- Define tour goals and budget
- Identify target markets
- Begin booking process
- Secure transportation
- Plan merchandise

### Phase 2: Pre-Production (1-3 Months Out)
- Confirm all dates
- Advance shows with venues
- Finalize travel arrangements
- Order merchandise
- Arrange insurance

### Phase 3: Execution (On Tour)
- Daily operations via day sheets
- Load-in, soundcheck, performance
- Merch sales and settlement
- Travel to next city

### Phase 4: Post-Tour (After)
- Final accounting
- Gear maintenance
- Relationship follow-up
- Performance analysis

## DIY vs. Professional Touring

### DIY Touring
**Best for:** Emerging artists, first tours, testing new markets

**Pros:**
- Complete creative control
- Higher profit margins (no agent/manager cuts)
- Direct fan relationships
- Flexibility in routing

**Cons:**
- Time-intensive booking process
- Limited venue access
- No established promoter relationships
- All logistics burden on you

### Working with Professionals
**Best for:** Established artists, extensive routing, major markets

**Pros:**
- Access to better venues
- Established promoter relationships
- Professional routing expertise
- Time freed for creative work

**Cons:**
- Commission costs (typically 10-15% for agents)
- Less control over certain decisions
- Need to maintain professional relationships

## Financial Realities

### Breaking Even on Tour

Most tours operate at a loss or break-even, especially for emerging artists. Consider touring as:
- **Marketing investment** — building fanbases in new markets
- **Content generation** — live footage, photos, stories
- **Merchandise opportunity** — often the most profitable component
- **Relationship building** — with venues, promoters, other artists

### Typical Tour Expenses

| Category | Percentage of Budget |
|----------|---------------------|
| Transportation | 25-35% |
| Accommodation | 15-25% |
| Food/Per Diems | 10-15% |
| Production | 10-20% |
| Marketing | 5-10% |
| Insurance | 3-5% |
| Contingency | 5-10% |

## North American Touring Considerations

### Market Tiers

**Tier 1 Markets** (Major cities, highest guarantees, most expensive)
- New York, Los Angeles, Chicago
- Toronto, Vancouver
- San Francisco, Seattle, Boston

**Tier 2 Markets** (Strong music scenes, moderate costs)
- Austin, Nashville, Atlanta
- Denver, Portland, Philadelphia
- Montreal, Calgary

**Tier 3 Markets** (Smaller cities, lower guarantees, routing connectors)
- College towns, regional hubs
- Important for building between major markets
- Often more engaged audiences

### Routing Principles

1. **Cluster dates geographically** — minimize travel distance
2. **Follow natural corridors** — I-95 corridor, I-5, I-10, I-80
3. **Consider day-of-week patterns** — weekends in major cities, weekdays in college towns
4. **Build in rest days** — essential for sustainability
5. **Plan around holidays** — avoid Thanksgiving, Christmas, major festivals unless participating

## Essential Documentation

### For Each Show, You Need:
- **Contract** — signed agreement with venue/promoter
- **Day Sheet** — daily itinerary distributed to all
- **Stage Plot** — technical requirements diagram
- **Input List** — audio channel assignments
- **Hospitality Rider** — green room and catering needs
- **Technical Rider** — equipment and production requirements

### For Cross-Border Tours:
- **Work permits/visas** — see Cross-Border Touring section
- **Gear manifest** — detailed equipment list with serial numbers
- **Carnet** (if applicable) — temporary import documentation
- **Tax documentation** — W-8BEN, W-8ECI, or treaty forms
