# Insurance & Liability

Touring involves significant risks — to your health, your equipment, your finances, and your business. Proper insurance coverage is not optional; it's essential protection for your career and livelihood.

---

## Types of Tour Insurance

### 1. Equipment Insurance (Inland Marine)

**What It Covers:**
- Musical instruments
- Audio equipment
- Lighting gear
- Computers and electronics
- Merchandise inventory
- While in transit, storage, and at venues

**Coverage Types:**
- **Replacement cost:** Full cost to replace (recommended)
- **Actual cash value:** Replacement cost minus depreciation

**Typical Costs:**
- $2-5 per $1,000 of coverage annually
- $50,000 gear = $100-250/year
- Varies by deductible and claims history

**What's Usually Covered:**
- Theft
- Fire
- Vandalism
- Accidental damage
- Water damage
- Transit damage

**What's Often Excluded:**
- Normal wear and tear
- Mysterious disappearance (without evidence of theft)
- Damage from improper packing
- Acts of war or terrorism
- Nuclear incidents

**Making a Claim:**
1. Document damage immediately (photos)
2. File police report if theft
3. Notify insurance company within 24-48 hours
4. Provide receipts/proof of value
5. Get repair estimates
6. Keep all documentation

### 2. General Liability Insurance

**What It Covers:**
- Bodily injury to third parties
- Property damage you cause
- Legal defense costs
- Settlements and judgments

**Why You Need It:**
- Venues require it (typically $1-2 million)
- Protects against lawsuits from audience injuries
- Covers damage to rented venues/equipment
- Essential for any public performance

**Typical Coverage:**
- $1 million per occurrence
- $2 million aggregate
- Higher limits available for larger tours

**Typical Costs:**
- $500-2,000/year for basic coverage
- Varies by coverage limits, history, genre

**Common Claims:**
- Audience member injured at show
- Damage to venue property
- Injury to venue staff
- Trip and fall incidents

### 3. Cancellation Insurance

**What It Covers:**
- Lost revenue from cancelled shows
- Non-refundable expenses
- Additional costs to reschedule

**Covered Reasons:**
- Illness/injury (artist or key crew)
- Death (artist, family member, key crew)
- Natural disasters
- Venue damage/unavailability
- Government restrictions (pandemics, etc.)

**What's Usually Excluded:**
- Poor ticket sales
- Artist choice/decision
- Pre-existing conditions
- Drug/alcohol-related incidents
- Known risks at time of purchase

**How It Works:**
- Purchase before tickets go on sale
- Covers specific dates or entire tours
- Premium: 2-4% of insured revenue
- Requires documentation of loss

**When to Consider:**
- High-guarantee shows
- Expensive productions
- Health concerns
- Weather-risky seasons/locations
- International touring

### 4. Travel Insurance

**What It Covers:**
- Trip cancellation/interruption
- Medical emergencies abroad
- Emergency evacuation
- Lost/delayed baggage
- Travel delays

**Types:**
- **Single-trip:** One tour or journey
- **Annual:** Covers all travel in a year
- **Long-term:** Extended touring

**Essential for:**
- International touring
- High-value equipment in checked baggage
- Pre-existing health conditions
- Remote touring locations

### 5. Commercial Auto Insurance

**What It Covers:**
- Vehicles used for business (touring)
- Liability for accidents
- Physical damage to vehicles
- Cargo (gear) in transit

**Requirements:**
- Personal auto insurance often excludes commercial use
- Needed for vans, buses, trailers
- Higher limits than personal policies

**Coverage Options:**
- Liability (required by law)
- Collision
- Comprehensive
- Cargo coverage
- Rental reimbursement

### 6. Workers' Compensation

**What It Covers:**
- Medical expenses for injured employees
- Lost wages during recovery
- Rehabilitation costs
- Death benefits

**Requirements:**
- Required in most states if you have employees
- Covers crew members, not independent contractors
- Requirements vary by state

**Who Needs It:**
- Artists with paid crew
- Tour managers
- Merch sellers
- Drivers

## Venue Insurance Requirements

### Certificate of Insurance (COI)

**What Venues Require:**
- General liability: $1-2 million
- Additional insured endorsement (venue named)
- Specific dates of coverage
- Equipment coverage (sometimes)

**Getting a COI:**
- Request from your insurance agent
- Usually free for existing policyholders
- Provide 48-72 hours notice
- Include venue's exact legal name

**Common Venue Requirements:**
```
General Liability:
- $1,000,000 per occurrence
- $2,000,000 aggregate
- Venue named as additional insured

Auto Liability (if bringing vehicles):
- $1,000,000 combined single limit

Workers' Compensation:
- Statutory limits
- Employer's liability: $1,000,000
```

### Additional Insured Endorsements

**What It Means:**
- Venue is protected under your policy
- Venue can file claims directly
- Standard industry practice
- Usually no extra cost

**How to Add:**
- Provide venue's legal name and address
- Specify dates
- Request before advancing show

## Risk Management

### Preventing Equipment Loss

**Physical Security:**
- Never leave gear in vehicles overnight
- Use hotel safes for small items
- Lock vehicles even for short stops
- Park in secure, well-lit areas
- Consider GPS trackers for trailers

**Documentation:**
- Photograph all equipment
- Record serial numbers
- Keep receipts for high-value items
- Update inventory annually
- Store copies off-site

**Packing:**
- Use quality cases (Anvil, SKB, Pelican)
- Label everything
- Pack strategically (essentials in carry-on for flights)
- Consider shock-mounted racks

### Health and Safety

**Preventing Illness:**
- Hand sanitizer everywhere
- Adequate sleep (build in rest days)
- Healthy eating (not just pizza)
- Hydration
- Flu shots before tour

**Injury Prevention:**
- Proper lifting techniques
- Stage awareness (watch for cables, edges)
- Hearing protection
- Back support for heavy lifting
- Stretching before physical activity

**Mental Health:**
- Check in with band/crew
- Build in downtime
- Maintain connections home
- Professional support if needed

### Financial Protection

**Contracts:**
- Always have written agreements
- Include cancellation clauses
- Specify force majeure terms
- Define payment terms clearly

**Deposits:**
- Request deposits for high guarantees
- Standard: 50% deposit, 50% on show day
- Protects against promoter default

**Escrow:**
- For very large guarantees
- Neutral third party holds funds
- Released upon performance

## Cross-Border Insurance Considerations

### US/Canada Touring

**Equipment:**
- Ensure policy covers both countries
- Some policies are North America-wide
- Others require endorsements

**Auto Insurance:**
- US policy may not cover Canada
- Canadian auto insurance required for Canadian-plated vehicles
- Check with carrier before crossing

**Health Insurance:**
- US health insurance often doesn't cover Canada
- Canadian health care doesn't cover visitors
- Travel medical insurance essential

### International Touring (Beyond North America)

**Equipment:**
- Carnet insurance for temporary imports
- Higher coverage limits
- Transit insurance for shipping

**Health:**
- Comprehensive travel medical
- Emergency evacuation coverage
- Pre-existing condition coverage

**Liability:**
- International liability coverage
- Local requirements vary
- May need local policies

## Cost Management

### Reducing Premiums

**Increase Deductibles:**
- Higher deductible = lower premium
- Only choose what you can afford to pay
- Common deductibles: $500-2,500

**Bundle Policies:**
- Package equipment, liability, auto
- Multi-policy discounts
- Simplified management

**Risk Management:**
- Security measures (alarms, GPS)
- Safe storage practices
- Claims-free history
- Professional operation

**Pay Annually:**
- Monthly payments often include fees
- Annual payment saves money

## Claims: When Things Go Wrong

### Immediate Steps

**Theft:**
1. Call police immediately
2. File official report
3. Document what was stolen
4. Photograph scene
5. Notify insurance within 24 hours
6. Notify venues on upcoming dates

**Damage:**
1. Document damage (photos/video)
2. Preserve damaged items
3. Get repair estimates
4. Notify insurance promptly
5. Keep all receipts

**Injury:**
1. Seek medical attention
2. Document incident
3. Get witness information
4. Notify insurance
5. Don't admit fault

### Working with Adjusters

**Be Prepared:**
- Have inventory list ready
- Provide serial numbers
- Show receipts or proof of value
- Be honest and thorough
- Respond promptly to requests

**Documentation:**
- Keep copies of everything
- Note all conversations
- Get claim numbers
- Follow up regularly

## Key Takeaways

1. **Insurance is essential, not optional** — Venues require it, and it protects your livelihood
2. **Equipment coverage is the foundation** — Your gear is your business
3. **Liability protects against the worst-case scenario** — One lawsuit can end a career
4. **Cancellation insurance for high-stakes tours** — Worth it when guarantees are large
5. **Document everything** — Photos, receipts, serial numbers make claims easier
6. **Work with entertainment specialists** — They understand touring
7. **Prevention is cheaper than claims** — Security and safety save money and stress
