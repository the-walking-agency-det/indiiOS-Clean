# Cross-Border Touring: US/Canada

Touring between the United States and Canada opens up significant opportunities — larger markets, new fans, and expanded reach. But it also introduces complexity: work permits, visas, taxes, and customs requirements that must be handled correctly.

---

## Overview: Why Cross-Border Touring Matters

### Market Opportunities

**Canada for US Artists:**
- Major markets: Toronto, Vancouver, Montreal
- Strong music scenes: Calgary, Edmonton, Ottawa, Halifax
- English-speaking (mostly) reduces language barriers
- Similar music tastes to northern US cities
- Often easier initial entry than Europe

**US for Canadian Artists:**
- Massive market (10x Canada's population)
- Industry hub (NYC, LA, Nashville)
- Higher guarantees in major markets
- Essential for international career growth

### The Challenge

Cross-border touring requires navigating:
- Work authorization (permits/visas)
- Customs and equipment importation
- Tax obligations in both countries
- Different business practices
- Currency exchange

## Work Permits and Visas

### US Artists Entering Canada

#### Work Permit Requirements

**Most US artists need a work permit to perform in Canada.**

**Exemptions (No Work Permit Required):**
- **CUSMA/USMCA (formerly NAFTA) Professionals** — Specific categories, limited applicability to performers
- **Very short appearances** — Some exemptions for single events under specific circumstances
- **Invited competitors/judges** — Not performance-based

**Reality:** Most touring artists need work permits.

#### Types of Canadian Work Permits

**1. Temporary Resident Permit (TRP) with Work Authorization**
- Most common for performers
- Applied for in advance
- Valid for specific dates/engagements
- Requires supporting documentation

**2. International Experience Canada (IEC)**
- For younger artists (18-35)
- Working holiday visa
- Limited duration
- Not specifically for performers

#### The Application Process

**Step 1: Labor Market Impact Assessment (LMIA) Exemption**

Most performers qualify for an LMIA exemption under **C12** (significant benefit) or **C11** (intra-company transfer) categories.

**Required Documentation:**
- Completed work permit application forms
- Passport copies
- Contract or letter of engagement from Canadian promoter/venue
- Proof of status as professional performer
- Itinerary of performances
- Proof of financial support

**Step 2: Apply Online**

Through Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC):
- Create online account
- Complete forms IMM 1295 (Application for Work Permit)
- Pay fees (approximately CAD $155)
- Upload supporting documents

**Step 3: Biometrics**

- May be required
- Schedule at application support center
- Additional fee (~CAD $85)

**Timeline:**
- Apply 6-12 weeks before travel
- Processing varies by country and volume
- Expedited processing available for urgent cases

#### Entering Canada

**At the Border:**
- Present passport
- Present work permit approval (if received in advance)
- Or apply for permit at port of entry (some cases)
- Declare all equipment
- Have documentation ready

**What to Bring:**
- Passport (valid for duration of stay)
- Work permit approval letter
- Contracts/itinerary
- Equipment manifest
- Proof of financial means
- Return/onward travel confirmation

### Canadian Artists Entering the US

#### Visa Requirements

**Canadian citizens generally need a visa to perform professionally in the US.**

**Visa Waiver Program (ESTA) — NOT FOR PERFORMERS:**
- Tourist/business visits only
- Does NOT cover paid performances
- Using ESTA for performances = deportation risk and future entry bans

#### O-1 and P-1 Visas for Performers

**O-1B Visa: Individuals with Extraordinary Ability**
- For artists with demonstrated extraordinary ability
- Requires extensive documentation of achievements
- Initial period: up to 3 years
- Renewable

**P-1B Visa: Internationally Recognized Entertainment Group**
- For performing groups
- Group must be internationally recognized
- For specific events/tours
- Initial period: up to 1 year

**P-2 Visa: Reciprocal Exchange Program**
- For artists in official exchange programs
- Must be part of organized exchange

**P-3 Visa: Culturally Unique Programs**
- For culturally unique performances
- Educational/cultural focus

#### The O-1B/P-1B Application Process

**Step 1: US Petitioner**

A US-based entity must petition:
- US agent
- Management company
- Promoter
- Venue

**Step 2: Petition Filing (Form I-129)**

File with USCIS:
- Form I-129 (Petition for Nonimmigrant Worker)
- O or P supplement
- Supporting evidence
- Fee: $460-2,500+ (varies by service type)

**Required Evidence (O-1B):**
- Awards/nominations
- Press coverage
- High salary compared to peers
- Leading roles in distinguished productions
- Commercial successes
- Critical recognition
- Testimonials from industry experts

**Required Evidence (P-1B):**
- Group has international recognition
- Significant achievements
- Press coverage
- Awards/recognition
- Testimonials

**Step 3: Visa Application**

After petition approval:
- Complete DS-160 (Online Nonimmigrant Visa Application)
- Pay visa fee ($190)
- Schedule consular interview
- Attend interview at US consulate

**Timeline:**
- Petition processing: 2-6 months (or 15 days with premium processing)
- Visa processing: Days to weeks
- **Start 4-6 months before tour**

#### Entering the US

**At the Border:**
- Present passport
- Present visa
- Present approved I-797 (petition approval)
- Declare all equipment
- Answer questions honestly

**What to Bring:**
- Passport
- Visa
- I-797 approval notice
- Itinerary
- Equipment manifest
- Proof of ties to Canada (return intent)

## Customs and Equipment

### Temporary Importation

Both US and Canada allow temporary importation of professional equipment without duties, provided it's exported when you leave.

### Equipment Manifest

**Create a Detailed List:**
```
| Item | Make/Model | Serial Number | Value (USD) | Origin |
|------|------------|---------------|-------------|--------|
| Guitar | Fender Strat | SN12345 | $1,500 | USA |
| Amp | Vox AC30 | SN67890 | $1,200 | UK |
| Laptop | MacBook Pro | SN11111 | $2,000 | USA |
```

**Tips:**
- Include photos of high-value items
- Note any items with warranties
- List consumables separately
- Have receipts for new purchases

### ATA Carnet

**What is an ATA Carnet?**

An international customs document that:
- Simplifies temporary imports
- Eliminates duties and taxes
- Valid for one year
- Accepted in 80+ countries

**When You Need One:**
- High-value equipment
- Multiple border crossings
- Professional gear you want to protect
- International touring beyond US/Canada

**How to Get One:**
- Apply through US Council for International Business (USCIB) or Canadian Chamber of Commerce
- Provide equipment list with values
- Pay fee (varies by value, typically $500-1,000+)
- Post security (bond, insurance, or cash)

**Using the Carnet:**
- Present at each border crossing
- Get stamps entering and exiting each country
- Keep carnet with gear at all times
- Return to issuing chamber when expired

### Border Crossing Best Practices

**Before Arrival:**
- Have all documentation organized
- Know your itinerary
- Declare everything
- Be honest about purpose (professional performance)

**At the Border:**
- Answer questions directly
- Don't volunteer extra information
- Be respectful
- Have backup documentation

**Common Questions:**
- Purpose of visit?
- How long staying?
- Where performing?
- Equipment value?
- Bringing merchandise to sell?

**Red Flags:**
- Inconsistent answers
- Incomplete documentation
- Attempting to enter on wrong visa/status
- Undeclared merchandise for sale

## Taxes

### US Artists in Canada

#### Income Tax Obligations

**Withholding Tax:**
- Canada withholds 15% of gross Canadian income
- Applies to performance fees, merchandise sales
- Collected by venue/promoter

**Tax Treaty Benefits (US-Canada Tax Treaty):**
- Article XVI may reduce or eliminate withholding
- Requires proper documentation
- File Canadian tax return to claim benefits

**Reducing Withholding:**
- Apply for waiver (Form R105)
- Provide US tax residency documentation
- Show treaty benefits apply
- Apply well in advance of performance

**Filing Canadian Tax Return:**
- File T1 return if you have Canadian income
- Report worldwide income
- Claim foreign tax credits for US taxes paid
- Due: April 30 (following year)

#### GST/HST (Goods and Services Tax)

**Merchandise Sales:**
- Must register for GST/HST if selling merchandise
- Collect tax on sales (5-15% depending on province)
- File regular returns
- Or: Venue may handle collection

**Performance Fees:**
- Generally exempt from GST/HST
- But verify with accountant

### Canadian Artists in the US

#### Income Tax Obligations

**Withholding Tax:**
- US withholds 30% of gross US income (unless treaty reduces)
- Applies to performance fees
- Collected by venue/promoter

**US-Canada Tax Treaty (Article XVI):**
- May reduce or eliminate withholding
- Requires proper documentation
- File US tax return to claim benefits

**Reducing Withholding:**
- File Form W-8BEN with payer
- Claim treaty benefits
- Provide Canadian tax residency documentation

**Filing US Tax Return:**
- File 1040-NR (Nonresident Alien Return)
- Report US income
- Claim treaty benefits
- Due: June 15 (following year)

#### State Taxes

**Many US states also tax performance income:**
- California, New York, etc.
- Varies by state
- May require separate filings
- Consult tax professional

### Tax Best Practices

**Before Tour:**
- Consult cross-border tax specialist
- Understand withholding requirements
- Prepare proper documentation
- Budget for withheld amounts

**During Tour:**
- Track all income and expenses by country
- Keep all contracts and receipts
- Document work days in each country

**After Tour:**
- File required returns in both countries
- Claim all available treaty benefits
- Apply for refunds of excess withholding

## Currency and Banking

### Handling Two Currencies

**Exchange Rate Considerations:**
- Rates fluctuate daily
- Budget using conservative rates
- Consider hedging for large tours

**Getting Paid:**
- Specify currency in contracts
- Consider exchange rate clauses
- Use bank transfers for large amounts
- Services like Wise (TransferWise) for better rates

### Banking

**Canadian Artists in US:**
- US bank account helpful but not required
- Many Canadian banks have US operations
- Consider multi-currency accounts
- PayPal, Stripe work across borders

**US Artists in Canada:**
- Canadian bank account rarely needed for short tours
- Use credit cards with no foreign transaction fees
- ATMs for cash (watch fees)

## Practical Considerations

### Health Insurance

**US Artists in Canada:**
- US health insurance may not cover Canada
- Purchase travel medical insurance
- Ensure coverage includes emergency evacuation

**Canadian Artists in US:**
- Canadian healthcare doesn't cover US
- Travel medical insurance essential
- US healthcare is extremely expensive

### Cell Phones and Data

**Options:**
- International roaming plans
- Local SIM cards
- eSIM services (Airalo, etc.)
- WiFi calling

**Tips:**
- Download offline maps
- Communicate with venues about WiFi
- Consider dual-SIM phones

### Vehicle Considerations

**Driving:**
- Valid license from home country
- Insurance must cover cross-border driving
- Check rental policies on international travel
- Know metric (Canada) vs. imperial (US) conversions

**Bringing Vehicles:**
- Temporary importation allowed
- Have registration and insurance
- Commercial vehicles may have additional requirements

## Common Mistakes

### Immigration
- **Entering on wrong visa/status** — Leads to deportation, future bans
- **Working without authorization** — Serious consequences
- **Inconsistent information** — Triggers additional scrutiny
- **Last-minute applications** — Delays, stress, potential cancellations

### Customs
- **Not declaring equipment** — Seizure, fines
- **Undeclared merchandise for sale** — Duties, penalties
- **Incorrect valuations** — Delays, additional taxes
- **Missing documentation** — Border delays

### Taxes
- **Not budgeting for withholding** — Cash flow problems
- **Missing filing deadlines** — Penalties, interest
- **Not claiming treaty benefits** — Overpaying taxes
- **Not tracking expenses** — Lost deductions

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Plan 4-6 months ahead** — Visas and permits take time
2. **Get proper authorization** — Never work on tourist status
3. **Document everything** — Equipment, contracts, income, expenses
4. **Budget for withholding** — Taxes will be held; plan cash flow
5. **Consult professionals** — Immigration attorneys and tax specialists save money and stress
6. **Be honest at borders** — Declarations and truthful answers prevent problems
7. **Consider ATA Carnet** — Simplifies equipment movement
8. **Buy travel medical insurance** — Healthcare across borders is expensive
