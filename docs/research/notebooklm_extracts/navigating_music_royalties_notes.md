### Music Industry Details

* **Snoop Dogg's Royalty Case Study:** Snoop Dogg was highly frustrated with Spotify because he earned only about $45,000 for an accumulation of 1 billion streams, prompting him to temporarily release an album on a different platform called Tune.fm. He also previously revealed that at the height of his career, he had never received a publishing check, indicating either bad deals or a lack of music business knowledge. He recently reconciled with Spotify after meetings with their executives helped him understand the complexity of royalties.
* **Contracts and Deal Structures:** Whenever you do a project, you must have a written contract to determine how and when you get paid, and to prove the agreement if someone tries to screw you over. A contract allows you to go over a collaborator's head directly to a platform or distributor if necessary.
* **Ownership and Splits:** You should retain ownership of your song whenever possible, offering collaborators "profit participation" instead of giving up equity in the copyright. If collaborating as fully independent artists (e.g., with a producer), a 50/50 split is common; the 1% to 3% producer split standard often heard about applies to major label deals, not independent ones.
* **Administrative Control:** It is crucial to try and maintain administrative control over your releases, meaning you hold the power to decide when music gets distributed and the money flows directly into your bank account.
* **The Two Sides of a Song:** Royalties are generated from two distinct copyrights: the composition itself (the chords, lyrics, and the underlying work) and the sound recording (commonly referred to as the master).
* **Music Distributors:** Distributors (like CD Baby, TuneCore, and Symphonic) are the only way to get music onto platforms like Spotify and Amazon Music to generate streaming royalties. Distributors pay artists quarterly, usually about three months after the money is earned. If you terminate a distributor to move to another, they will often withhold trailing royalties unless you explicitly send them a communication demanding them.
* **Distributor Upsells to Avoid:** Distributors often offer upsells to manage your publishing or YouTube Content ID monitoring, but they will take a 15% to 20% cut. You should sign up for these platforms directly to keep 100% of your earnings.
* **Performance Rights Organizations (PROs):** Entities like SOCAN (Canada), PRS (UK), and BMI, SESAC, and ASCAP (US) collect public performance royalties, such as money earned from TV show placements. You must be registered before the money accrues; if your song gets placed and you aren't registered, you lose out on that money. PROs also generally pay out quarterly.
* **SoundExchange:** This is the only entity that collects non-interactive streaming revenue (such as internet radio like Pandora or SiriusXM) for the master recording side of the song. SoundExchange royalties can be collected retroactively, meaning you get paid for past plays as soon as you finally register. The payouts are split: 50% to the sound recording owner, 45% to the featured artist, and 5% to the AFM/SAG-AFTRA fund (which is notoriously difficult for artists to actually access).
* **The Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC):** In the US, the MLC collects mechanical royalties from digital service providers (like Spotify and Amazon) which are generated when a song is "duplicated" via streaming. These royalties can also be collected retroactively upon joining.
* **Songtrust:** This company collects international performance royalties and registers with the MLC. However, you should not give them a percentage to collect your domestic royalties when you can register directly with your local PRO.
* **Direct-to-Fan Release Strategy ("Sell Before You Stream"):** Instead of releasing a full album or EP at once, independent artists should release music piecemeal as singles. Sell the song as a digital download directly to fans before releasing it to streaming platforms.
* **Platforms for Selling Music:** Bandcamp allows you to sell music directly but takes a 15% commission (dropping to 10% after $5,000 in sales). Alternatively, you can use a personal website like Wix, which costs between $17 and $35 a month but allows you to keep 100% of your earnings.
* **"Pay What You Want" Model:** When selling direct, use a minimum price of $1 but let fans pay what they want. Superfans will often pay $50, $100, or even $200 for a single song.
* **Upcoming Legislation:** The Living Wage for Musicians Act of 2024 is a proposed federal act that recently gained support from the New York council; if passed, it would set up a new government agency to potentially secure more royalties for artists from platforms.
* **Royalty Audits and Disputes:** If a company is withholding royalties, send a formal demand letter clearly designating a deadline for them to respond, release funds, or provide documents. Having an attorney send the letter on legal letterhead is far more intimidating and effective. A DIY audit involves requesting and reviewing documents, but the "nuclear option" is hiring a professional to do a forensic accounting of their books.
* **Preserving Evidence:** Before deleting an account or terminating a relationship with a company, you must download all of your statements and documents because you will likely lose access to them once the relationship goes awry.

### High-Quality Concepts

* **Preparation Precedes Luck:** You cannot predict which song will become a hit, so you must understand the business and have your royalty registrations fully set up before a song gains popularity. Once a track blows up, the infrastructure is already in place to capture the revenue.
* **Contracts as Leverage:** Beyond outlining terms, a contract serves as an objective tool of leverage. If a collaborative relationship breaks down, the written agreement acts as proof that allows you to bypass the uncooperative party and appeal directly to a distributor or platform to ensure you get paid.
* **Control vs. Equity:** You can financially incentivize team members and collaborators without giving up your core assets. By offering "profit participation" rather than copyright ownership, you retain full equity over your intellectual property.
* **Administrative Power:** The person who controls the administrative rights—meaning they decide when the project is distributed and hold the bank accounts where the money flows—holds the true power in a collaborative relationship.
* **Audience Harvesting Over Algorithm Reliance:** Social media platforms only show your posts to roughly 5% of your followers. To build a reliable business, you must prioritize "audience harvesting" by aggressively collecting email addresses, giving you a guaranteed, direct line of communication to your true fans.
* **Monetizing Superfans First:** The traditional model of immediately placing music on low-paying streaming platforms wastes earning potential. By utilizing the "Sell Before You Stream" method, you give your dedicated core audience the opportunity to financially support you at a premium price (often voluntarily paying exponentially more than standard rates) before offering the product to the broader public.
